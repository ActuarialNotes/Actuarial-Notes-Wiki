Given the following information as of December 31, 2018:

| Accident Year | Reported Claims | Age-to-Ultimate | Earned Premium | Pure Premium Trend Factors |
|---------------|----------------|-----------------|----------------|---------------------------|
| 2016 | 2,900 | 2.300 | 6,500 | 1.067 |
| 2017 | 1,800 | 3.900 | 8,100 | 0.983 |
| 2018 | 1,000 | 7.600 | 8,000 | 1.000 |

- There are no historical rate changes.
- There is no premium trend.

## Part a (1.5 points)

Calculate the ultimate claims for accident year 2017 using the reported Cape Cod technique.

### Answer
7835

### Explanation
The pure premium trend factors given are cumulative factors to bring each accident year's losses to the **2018** cost level. To estimate the expected claims ratio (ECR) for AY 2017 using the Cape Cod method, we must trend all reported losses to a common level (2017) and compute a weighted average loss ratio.

**Step 1: Trend reported losses to the AY 2017 cost level**

The trend factor to reach 2018 level is given. To bring each year's losses to the 2017 level, divide by the 2018-level trend factor for AY 2017 (0.983):

$$\text{Trended Claims at 2017 level} = \text{Reported Claims} \times \frac{\text{Trend Factor to 2018}}{0.983}$$

| AY | Reported Claims | Trend Factor (to 2017) | Trended Claims |
|----|----------------|------------------------|----------------|
| 2016 | 2,900 | 1.067 / 0.983 | 3,148 |
| 2017 | 1,800 | 0.983 / 0.983 | 1,800 |
| 2018 | 1,000 | 1.000 / 0.983 | 1,017 |

**Step 2: Compute used-up premium (on-level)**

$$\text{Used-Up Premium} = EP \times \frac{1}{LDF_{age-to-ult}}$$

| AY | Earned Premium | LDF | Used-Up Premium |
|----|---------------|-----|-----------------|
| 2016 | 6,500 | 2.300 | 2,826 |
| 2017 | 8,100 | 3.900 | 2,077 |
| 2018 | 8,000 | 7.600 | 1,053 |
| **Total** | | | **5,956** |

**Step 3: Compute ECR at the 2017 cost level**

$$ECR_{2017} = \frac{\sum \text{Trended Claims}}{\sum \text{Used-Up Premium}} = \frac{3{,}148 + 1{,}800 + 1{,}017}{5{,}956} = \frac{5{,}965}{5{,}956} \approx 1.002$$

**Step 4: Apply Cape Cod (BF-style) formula to AY 2017**

$$\text{Ult. Claims}_{2017} = \text{Reported}_{2017} + ECR_{2017} \times EP_{2017} \times \left(1 - \frac{1}{LDF_{2017}}\right)$$

$$= 1{,}800 + 1.002 \times 8{,}100 \times \left(1 - \frac{1}{3.9}\right) = 1{,}800 + 1.002 \times 8{,}100 \times 0.7436 \approx 1{,}800 + 6{,}035 = 7{,}835$$

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to estimate ultimate losses using the reported Cape Cod method, while taking into account a changing pure premium trend.

Common mistakes included:
- Not taking the pure premium trend into account in the calculation
- Using the pure premium trends on premiums, rather than reported losses
- Not trending the reported losses to the 2017 level (i.e., trending losses to the 2018 level, but not de-trending that value to the 2017 level)
- Using 2018 reported losses, earned premium, and age-to-ultimate factors to calculate the ultimate loss, rather than the 2017 values

## Part b (0.5 points)

Describe a scenario where the paid Cape Cod technique is preferred to the reported Cape Cod technique.

### Explanation
When there has been a change in case reserve adequacy (i.e., case reserve strengthening or weakening), the reported Cape Cod technique will produce distorted ultimate estimates because the reported claims are inflated or deflated relative to prior periods. This distortion affects both the expected claims ratio (ECR) used to estimate IBNR and the percentage unreported factor.

The paid Cape Cod technique is unaffected by changes in case reserve adequacy because it relies only on paid claims. Therefore, when case outstanding strength has changed, the paid Cape Cod method is preferred because it avoids the distortion that would otherwise flow through the LDFs and ECR in the reported technique.

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to know the differences between paid and reported Cape Cod methods, and state a scenario where the reported Cape Cod method would calculate a distorted ultimate loss, while the paid Cape Cod method would not have a distorted ultimate loss.

Common mistakes included:
- Simply stating that a change in case reserve adequacy would cause a preference of paid Cape Cod over reported Cape Cod without further explanation
- Stating that a large loss would result in a preference for the reported method over the paid
