An insurance company is researching three new rating variables to include in its homeowners risk classification system. The insurer has determined the following information about the existing book of business.

**Credit Score:**

| Credit | Exposures | Pure Premium | Competitor's Rating Plan Factor | Base Class |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Excellent | 1,500 | $116.67 | 0.85 | No |
| Good | 2,500 | $128.00 | 1.00 | No |
| Fair | 1,000 | $155.00 | 1.30 | No |
| Total | 5,000 | $130.00 | — | — |

**Age of Homeowner:**

| Age of Homeowner | Exposures | Pure Premium | Competitor's Rating Plan Factor | Base Class |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Under 30 years | 800 | $150.00 | 0.80 | No |
| 30 to 40 years | 1,200 | $116.67 | 1.00 | Yes |
| Over 40 years | 3,000 | $120.00 | 2.00 | Yes |
| Total | 5,000 | $130.00 | — | — |

**Loss Prevention:**

| Loss Prevention | Exposures | Pure Premium | Competitor's Rating Plan Factor | Base Class |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fire extinguisher | 4,700 | $128.72 | 1.00 | Yes |
| Smoke detector | 100 | $175.00 | 1.50 | No |
| None | 200 | $175.00 | 1.50 | No |
| Total | 5,000 | $130.00 | — | — |

Additional information:

- Credit is being used as the credit score for the primary homeowner.
- Age of homeowner is the age of the primary homeowner.
- A homeowner with both a fire extinguisher and a smoke detector would be classified with a smoke detector.
- Full credibility claim standard = 400.
- The square root rule is used to determine partial credibility.
- A competitor's rating relativities are used as the credibility complement.
- Frequency for every risk variable = 10%.
- Assume that the insurer can implement only one new rating variable at this time.
- Assume that each variable is independent.

## Part a (1.5 points)

For each potential rating variable, briefly describe two possible concerns of adding it to a risk classification system.

### Answer

**Credit Score:**
1. Lacks causality — credit score is correlated with loss exposure, but it is difficult to establish a direct causal relationship. Some argue that credit score is a proxy for socioeconomic status, raising concerns about indirect discrimination.
2. Invades the privacy of insureds — using a financial metric not directly related to the insured property may be seen as an invasion of privacy or cause social concern.

**Age of Homeowner:**
1. Lacks controllability — insureds cannot control their age, so being charged more for a characteristic beyond their control may be considered inequitable.
2. The indicated relativities differ significantly from competitor relativities (e.g., the indicated relativity for Under 30 is greater than 1.0, implying higher risk, while the competitor uses a factor of 0.80 — the opposite direction). This discrepancy raises questions about the reliability or appropriateness of the variable.

**Loss Prevention:**
1. Difficult and expensive to verify — the presence of fire extinguishers and smoke detectors is subject to manipulation by insureds (e.g., a policyholder could claim a device they do not actually own), making the variable costly to administer.
2. Nonsensical classification structure — the current definition classifies a homeowner with both a fire extinguisher and a smoke detector into the "smoke detector" class, which carries the same factor as "None" (1.50). This implies that having a fire extinguisher actually worsens the rating, which lacks face validity.

### Examiner Report

Candidates needed to provide a brief description along with the characteristic they listed. Most candidates lost points for either no, or an insufficient, description of the characteristic listed. For example, a common insufficient answer is that "credit is discriminatory." Such an answer is not quite accurate, since all classification plan factors discriminate among insureds. Thus, a clarification of the nature of discrimination that causes concern is warranted.

Some candidates mentioned concern that the age of homeowners relativities curve does not trend monotonically. Candidates who received credit typically mentioned lack of credibility in the youngest age group or the dissimilar direction compared to competitor relativities. However, the lack of a monotonic relationship in and of itself was not accepted as a valid concern.

## Part b (0.75 points)

Without performing any calculations, recommend and justify which rating variable the insurer should implement in a risk classification system.

### Answer

**Recommend: Credit Score.**

Justification:
1. **Significant loss cost differentiation** — credit score shows material variation in pure premiums ($116.67 for Excellent vs. $155.00 for Fair), demonstrating meaningful risk segmentation.
2. **Objective and well-defined** — credit score levels are objectively defined by a third party and are consistently applied.
3. **Easy and inexpensive to verify and administer** — credit score data is readily available from credit bureaus at low cost, unlike physical inspection required for loss prevention devices.
4. **Social concerns alone are not sufficient** to prevent using this variable, provided it is legal to do so and the actuarial considerations (loss cost differentiation, availability, accuracy) are met.

By contrast: Age of homeowner has counterintuitive relativities vs. competitors and lacks controllability. Loss prevention has a nonsensical classification scheme and is difficult to verify.

### Examiner Report

Many candidates did not provide a description commensurate with the point value assigned. In order to receive full credit, candidates needed to briefly describe at least three reasons to support their choice. Some candidates provided reasons for choosing a variable that contradicted the concerns listed in Part a, which lost them points. Often, candidates described reasons why they would not choose other variables — points were awarded when the reason a variable was not selected for one variable was a valid reason to select the chosen variable. However, if a candidate said they didn't select age of homeowner because of lack of credibility and they chose loss prevention (which also has an issue with credibility), points were not awarded. Many candidates who chose credit score lost points for saying the levels were "fully credible," as opposed to "good credibility."

## Part c (1.25 points)

Develop the indicated credibility-weighted rating factors for the variable recommended in part b above.

### Answer

Using credit score, the credibility-weighted relativities (re-based) are:

| Credit | Indicated Rel | Competitor Rel | Credibility $Z$ | Cred-Wtd Rel | Re-based |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Excellent | 0.8975 | 0.8374 | 61.24% | 0.8741 | **0.888** |
| Good | 0.9846 | 0.9852 | 79.06% | 0.9847 | **1.000** |
| Fair | 1.1923 | 1.2808 | 50.00% | 1.2366 | **1.256** |

### Explanation

**Step 1 — Indicated relativities**

Using Total as the base (Total pure premium = $130.00):

$$\text{Indicated Rel}_{\text{Excellent}} = \frac{116.67}{130.00} = 0.8975$$

$$\text{Indicated Rel}_{\text{Good}} = \frac{128.00}{130.00} = 0.9846$$

$$\text{Indicated Rel}_{\text{Fair}} = \frac{155.00}{130.00} = 1.1923$$

**Step 2 — Competitor relativities**

Revenue-neutral competitor relativities (as provided, re-based to Total):
- Excellent: 0.8374
- Good: 0.9852
- Fair: 1.2808

**Step 3 — Credibility**

Full credibility requires 400 claims. Expected claims = Exposures × Frequency (10%).

$$Z = \sqrt{\frac{n_{\text{claims}}}{400}}$$

- Excellent: $n = 1500 \times 0.10 = 150$, $Z = \sqrt{150/400} = \sqrt{0.375} = 61.24\%$
- Good: $n = 2500 \times 0.10 = 250$, $Z = \sqrt{250/400} = \sqrt{0.625} = 79.06\%$
- Fair: $n = 1000 \times 0.10 = 100$, $Z = \sqrt{100/400} = \sqrt{0.25} = 50.00\%$

**Step 4 — Credibility-weighted relativities**

$$\text{Cred-Wtd Rel} = Z \times \text{Ind Rel} + (1-Z) \times \text{Comp Rel}$$

- Excellent: $0.6124 \times 0.8975 + 0.3876 \times 0.8374 = 0.5496 + 0.3245 = 0.8741$
- Good: $0.7906 \times 0.9846 + 0.2094 \times 0.9852 = 0.7784 + 0.2063 = 0.9847$
- Fair: $0.5000 \times 1.1923 + 0.5000 \times 1.2808 = 0.5962 + 0.6404 = 1.2366$

**Step 5 — Re-base so Good = 1.000**

Divide each credibility-weighted relativity by the Good level result (0.9847):

$$\text{Excellent} = \frac{0.8741}{0.9847} = 0.888$$

$$\text{Good} = \frac{0.9847}{0.9847} = 1.000$$

$$\text{Fair} = \frac{1.2366}{0.9847} = 1.256$$

### Examiner Report

To receive full credit, candidates needed to correctly calculate the full credibility standard, calculate the credibility using the square root rule, calculate the company indicated relativities, credibility weight the company relativities with the competitor relativities, and finally re-base the credibility-weighted relativities. The most common mistake was claiming full credibility, not recognizing that the 400 full credibility standard refers to claim count and not exposure. For candidates who calculated the indicated company relativities relative to the total pure premium, a common mistake was not calculating the revenue-neutral competitor relativities as well. Additionally, some candidates missed the instruction to use the competitor's relativities as the complement of credibility.
