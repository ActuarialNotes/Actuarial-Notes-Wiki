**Target Loss Ratio** (also called the permissible loss ratio) is the loss ratio that, if exactly achieved, produces the desired combined ratio and profit margin; it serves as the benchmark in the [[Loss Ratio Method]] to determine whether rates are adequate.

> $$\text{Target LR} = 1 - V - F - Q$$

- $V$ = variable expense ratio, $F$ = fixed expense ratio (as % of premium), $Q$ = target underwriting profit provision; all as proportions of premium
- When the projected loss ratio exceeds the target, rates are inadequate; when it falls below, rates are excessive; the ratio of projected to target drives the indicated change
- The target loss ratio equals $100\%$ minus the target combined ratio (where combined ratio = loss ratio + expense ratio + profit provision)
- The target loss ratio is identical to the [[Permissible Loss Ratio]]; both terms appear in CAS Exam 5 literature and refer to the same concept

> [!example]- Using the Target Loss Ratio to Indicate a Rate Change {Example}
> Variable expenses are 25%, fixed expenses are 7%, and the profit provision is 3%. The projected loss ratio is 70%. What rate change is indicated?
>
> > [!answer]-
> > $$\text{Target LR} = 1 - 0.25 - 0.07 - 0.03 = 0.65 = 65\%$$
> > $$\text{Indicated Change} = \frac{70\%}{65\%} - 1 = 1.0769 - 1 = +7.7\%$$
> > Rates must increase approximately 7.7% to achieve the target loss ratio and desired profit.
