**Loss Ratio** is the ratio of incurred losses (and often loss adjustment expenses) to earned premium, measuring what fraction of premium revenue is consumed by claims and the cost of settling them.

> $$\text{Loss Ratio}$$
>
> $$= \frac{\text{Losses (+ LAE)}}{\text{Earned Premium}}$$

- The loss ratio is the primary profitability metric in property-casualty insurance; combined with the expense ratio it forms the [[Combined Ratio]]
- If the actual loss ratio exceeds the [[Permissible Loss Ratio]], rates are inadequate and a rate increase is indicated
- Loss ratios should be computed on an ultimate basis (developed losses) rather than on reported losses to avoid distortion from immature data
- Calendar year loss ratios mix accident years and are simpler to compute; accident year or policy year loss ratios are actuarially preferable

> [!example]- Indicated Rate Change Using Loss Ratio {Example}
> An insurer has $650,000 in ultimate losses and $1,000,000 in earned premium. The permissible loss ratio is 65%.
>
> > [!answer]-
> > $$\text{Loss Ratio} = \frac{\$650{,}000}{\$1{,}000{,}000} = 65\%$$
> > Since the actual loss ratio equals the permissible loss ratio, no rate change is indicated. Had losses been $715,000, the loss ratio would be 71.5%, indicating a rate increase of $71.5\% / 65\% - 1 = +10\%$.
