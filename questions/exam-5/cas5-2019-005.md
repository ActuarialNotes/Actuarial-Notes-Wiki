| Trend Period | Frequency | Total Severity |
|---|---|---|

## Part a (3.75 points)

Calculate the projected trended ultimate losses for accident year 2018 to be used to determine a rate change effective January 1, 2020. Briefly justify the trend selections.

### Explanation

**Trend Selections:**

Frequency trend appears stable across all trend periods; select **1.4%** using the 16-point trend for credibility.

Severity trend shows a decreasing pattern over time, requiring **two-step trending**:
- Historical step (experience to future period midpoint): select **5.4%** based on the 16-point regression (longer-term data provides more credibility).
- Future step (future period midpoint to average loss date of new rates): select **0.1%** (approximately 0%) based on the most recent short-term regression.

**Trend Periods:**

New rates effective January 1, 2020. Average written date of future policies ≈ July 1, 2020; average loss date ≈ January 1, 2021.

- Step 1: from average accident date of each historical AY to July 1, 2018 (midpoint of experience period)
- Step 2: from July 1, 2018 to January 1, 2021 = **2.5 years**

Combined pure premium trend: $1.014 \times 1.001$ per year for the future step (frequency × severity).

**Calculate Trended Gross Reported Losses (AY 2012–2015, \$000s):**

AY 2012 (average accident date 7/1/2012 → 6 years to 7/1/2018, then 2.5 years future):

$$169{,}000 \times 1.054^6 \times 1.001^{2.5} \times 1.014^{6+2.5} = 267{,}068$$

AY 2013 (5 years historical, 2.5 years future):

$$181{,}000 \times 1.054^5 \times 1.001^{2.5} \times 1.014^{5+2.5} = 261{,}061$$

AY 2014: $\approx 243{,}762$

AY 2015: $\approx 212{,}659$

$$\text{Total Trended Gross Losses} = 984{,}550$$

**Trended Excess (Shock) Losses (\$000s):**

Apply same trend factors to ground-up shock losses, then subtract $n \times \$500$ to retain only the amount above the threshold:

AY 2012 (3 shock losses in the \$000s data, gross = 3,000):

$$3{,}000 \times 1.054^6 \times 1.001^{2.5} \times 1.014^{8.5} - 3 \times 500 = 3{,}141$$

AY 2013: $\approx 5{,}237$

AY 2014: $\approx 2{,}563$

AY 2015: $\approx 260$

$$\text{Total Trended Excess Losses} = 11{,}200$$

**Excess Loss Ratio (using AY 2012–2015 only):**

$$\text{XS Ratio} = \frac{11{,}200}{984{,}550 - 11{,}200} = \frac{11{,}200}{973{,}350} = 1.15\%$$

**Develop AY 2018 to Ultimate:**

$$121{,}000 \times 1.2 \times 1.1 \times 1.05 = 167{,}706$$

$$\text{Shock loss developed: } 900 \times 1.2 \times 1.1 \times 1.05 = 1{,}247$$

**Trend AY 2018 Ultimate (future step = 2.5 years only, since 2018 is already in the experience period):**

$$167{,}706 \times 1.015^{2.5} = 174{,}066$$

$$1{,}247 \times 1.015^{2.5} = 1{,}294$$

**Remove excess losses, retain threshold:**

$$\text{Trended primary losses} = 174{,}066 - 1{,}294 + 500 = 173{,}272$$

**Apply excess loss provision $(1 + \text{XS Ratio})$:**

$$173{,}272 \times (1 + 0.01150) = 173{,}272 \times 1.0115 \approx 175{,}246$$

$$\boxed{\approx \$175{,}246 \text{ thousand}}$$

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to:
- Make frequency and severity trend selections with brief justification, and identify the need for two-step severity trending.
- Apply the correct number of trend years to each accident year's losses (both cumulative and shock losses for AY 2012–2015).
- Remove trended excess losses from trended cumulative losses while retaining \$500,000 per claim in the cumulative total.
- Calculate an excess loss factor using only AY 2012–2015 data.
- Apply trend and development factors to AY 2018, remove excess losses (retaining threshold), and multiply by $(1 + \text{excess loss factor})$.

Common mistakes included:
- Not justifying the trend selections.
- Not identifying the need for two-step severity trending (making a selection on recent points only).
- Not trending AY 2012–2015 losses (both cumulative and shock) used in the excess of loss calculation.
- Not retaining \$500,000 for each shock loss in the cumulative accident year losses.
- Not removing excess losses from the cumulative losses in the denominator of the excess loss calculation or from the AY 2018 losses.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Briefly describe two approaches for selecting an excess loss threshold used for capping shock losses.

### Explanation

Any two of the following:

1. **Select a quantile from the loss distribution**: Analyze the empirical loss distribution and select a threshold at a chosen percentile where large losses become volatile and data thins out.

2. **Percentage of coverage amount**: Set the threshold as a certain percentage of the amount of insurance (e.g., for homeowners, cap at 70% of Coverage A).

3. **Reinsurance attachment point or policy limit**: Use a natural contractual boundary such as the reinsurance attachment point, basic limit, or policy limit as the threshold.

4. **Balance stability and responsiveness**: Choose a threshold that balances the stability of the ratemaking indication against its responsiveness to actual large loss experience.

5. **Actuarial judgment based on data review**: Examine the distribution and select the value where losses become volatile and sparse, using judgment informed by the data.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to demonstrate knowledge and thought behind an appropriate selection of an excess loss threshold.

Common mistakes included:
- Stating "arbitrary selection" without some justification or use of judgment.
- Stating "historical selections" without explaining why those selections might not be relevant.
- Suggesting "ask Claims Department" because shock losses for Claims Department use may differ from those for ratemaking purposes.
- Listing a data source without explanation (e.g., "use industry data" without describing how to use it to select a threshold).
- Repeating the same answer in different words to count as two approaches.
- Explaining how to apply the threshold rather than how to select one.
- Stating "quantile approach" without relating the quantile to losses.
