**Unpaid Claims** are the insurer's estimated liability for claims that have occurred but have not yet been fully paid as of the valuation date. The unpaid claim estimate is the central output of a reserve analysis and comprises case reserves, development on known claims, and claims not yet reported.

> $$\text{Unpaid Claims}$$
>
> $$= \text{Case Reserves} + \text{IBNR}$$
>
> $$= \underbrace{\text{Ultimate Loss} - \text{Paid Loss}}_{\text{total unpaid}}$$

- The total unpaid estimate equals [[Ultimate Loss|ultimate losses]] minus paid losses. It splits into **case reserves** (claim-adjuster estimates on reported open claims) and **[[IBNR]]** — which itself is *pure IBNR* (unreported claims) plus *IBNER* (incurred-but-not-enough-reported development on known claims).
- "Unpaid claims" is the modern term used by Friedland and ASOP 43 in preference to "reserves," emphasizing that it is an estimate of a future-payment liability, not a fund.
- Estimation methods include the [[Chain Ladder Method]], [[Expected Loss Method]], [[Bornhuetter-Ferguson Method]], [[Cape Cod Method]], [[Frequency-Severity Method]], and [[Benktander Method]]; the actuary selects by accident-year maturity and data credibility.
- The estimate applies to insurers and to any [[Types of Insurance|self-insured or risk-retaining entity]]; it must also reflect [[Salvage and Subrogation]] and reinsurance recoveries to state losses net of recoverables.

> [!example]- Splitting the Unpaid Estimate {Example}
> For AY 2024 an actuary selects ultimate losses of \$$5{,}000{,}000$. Paid to date $= \$1{,}800{,}000$ and case reserves $= \$2{,}200{,}000$.
>
> > [!answer]-
> > Total unpaid $= 5{,}000{,}000 - 1{,}800{,}000 = \$3{,}200{,}000$
> >
> > Reported (paid + case) $= 1{,}800{,}000 + 2{,}200{,}000 = \$4{,}000{,}000$
> >
> > IBNR $= \text{Ultimate} - \text{Reported} = 5{,}000{,}000 - 4{,}000{,}000 = \$1{,}000{,}000$
> >
> > Check: Unpaid $=$ Case $+$ IBNR $= 2{,}200{,}000 + 1{,}000{,}000 = \$3{,}200{,}000$ ✓
