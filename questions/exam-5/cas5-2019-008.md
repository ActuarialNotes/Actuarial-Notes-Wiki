Given the following pricing strategies for an insurer with two classes of business:

**Strategy 1 – No Change**

| Class | Number of Risks | Losses & Expenses per Risk | Rate per Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | 5,000 | 900 | 1,050 |
| B | 10,000 | 1,000 | 1,050 |

One-time expense to implement Strategy 1: \$0

**Strategy 2 – New Rating Variable**

| Class | Number of Risks | Losses & Expenses per Risk | Rate per Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | 5,000 | 900 | 1,000 |
| B | 10,000 | 1,000 | 1,100 |

One-time expense to implement Strategy 2: \$500,000

**Strategy 3 – Rate Increase for All Risks**

| Class | Number of Risks | Losses & Expenses per Risk | Rate per Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | 5,000 | 900 | 1,100 |
| B | 10,000 | 1,000 | 1,100 |

One-time expense to implement Strategy 3: \$500,000

## Part a (0.75 points)

Calculate the expected total profit for the insurer under each strategy.

### Explanation

Total Profit = Total Revenue − Total Losses & Expenses − One-time Implementation Expense

**Strategy 1:**

$$\text{Revenue} = 5{,}000 \times 1{,}050 + 10{,}000 \times 1{,}050 = 5{,}250{,}000 + 10{,}500{,}000 = 15{,}750{,}000$$

$$\text{L\&E} = 5{,}000 \times 900 + 10{,}000 \times 1{,}000 = 4{,}500{,}000 + 10{,}000{,}000 = 14{,}500{,}000$$

$$\text{Profit}_1 = 15{,}750{,}000 - 14{,}500{,}000 - 0 = \$1{,}250{,}000$$

**Strategy 2:**

$$\text{Revenue} = 5{,}000 \times 1{,}000 + 10{,}000 \times 1{,}100 = 5{,}000{,}000 + 11{,}000{,}000 = 16{,}000{,}000$$

$$\text{L\&E} = 5{,}000 \times 900 + 10{,}000 \times 1{,}000 = 14{,}500{,}000$$

$$\text{Profit}_2 = 16{,}000{,}000 - 14{,}500{,}000 - 500{,}000 = \$1{,}000{,}000$$

**Strategy 3:**

$$\text{Revenue} = 5{,}000 \times 1{,}100 + 10{,}000 \times 1{,}100 = 5{,}500{,}000 + 11{,}000{,}000 = 16{,}500{,}000$$

$$\text{L\&E} = 14{,}500{,}000$$

$$\text{Profit}_3 = 16{,}500{,}000 - 14{,}500{,}000 - 500{,}000 = \$1{,}500{,}000$$

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to calculate expected total profit by computing total revenue under each strategy (number of risks × rate per risk, summed across classes), subtracting total losses and expenses, and subtracting the one-time implementation expense.

Common mistakes included:
- Failing to subtract the one-time expense when calculating expected total profit under each strategy.
- Adding the one-time expense instead of subtracting it.

## Part b (1 point)

Briefly evaluate the following for Strategy 2 and Strategy 3:

i. Assumption that the number of risks by class will be the same as Strategy 1.

ii. Impact this assumption has on each strategy's expected total profit.

### Explanation

**Strategy 2 – New Rating Variable:**

i. **The assumption is not valid.** Under Strategy 2, Class A's rate decreases from \$1,050 to \$1,000, making it more competitively priced for lower-risk insureds. Class A policyholders are more likely to stay (or new Class A risks to join), so the number of Class A risks may **increase**. Conversely, Class B's rate increases from \$1,050 to \$1,100, making it less competitive; Class B risks may shop elsewhere, so the number of Class B risks may **decrease**.

ii. **Impact on expected profit:** The calculation in Part a may **overstate or understate** the actual profit under Strategy 2. If the decrease in Class B risks (who are more numerous and less profitable at \$1,100 vs. \$1,000 in L&E) outweighs the gain in Class A risks, total profit could fall below \$1,000,000. However, over the long run, shedding adverse risks (Class B) and attracting better risks (Class A) generally **improves profitability** — Strategy 2 is appropriately pricing by risk class and avoids adverse selection.

**Strategy 3 – Rate Increase for All Risks:**

i. **The assumption is not valid.** Both Class A and Class B rates increase from \$1,050 to \$1,100. Price-sensitive policyholders in both classes may seek cheaper alternatives from competitors. The number of risks in both Class A and Class B may **decrease**.

ii. **Impact on expected profit:** The calculation in Part a likely **overstates** the expected profit under Strategy 3. As policyholders leave — particularly Class A risks who are favorably priced elsewhere — total revenue will fall and the true profit will be lower than \$1,500,000. Additionally, adverse selection may occur if Class B risks are less price-sensitive (because they cannot find better rates elsewhere due to their higher risk), leaving the insurer with a worse-than-expected mix.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to evaluate the assumption that the number of risks by class under Strategies 2 and 3 will be the same as Strategy 1. Candidates should have recognized the price sensitivity of individual insureds and the potential for individuals to switch companies based on price elasticity and market competition.

Common mistakes included:
- Stating there would be zero impact to expected profit under Strategy 2, which is only true if the increase and decrease in the number of risks for each class exactly offset.
- Failing to consider the impact on number of risks and expected profit for the different strategies, and instead basing answers solely on the results calculated in Part a.
- Not understanding the behavior of individual insureds as it pertains to price sensitivity and competition in the marketplace.
