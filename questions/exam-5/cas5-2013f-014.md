Given the following information:

| Claim ID | Accident Date | Report Date | CY 2010 | CY 2011 | CY 2012 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | June 15, 2010 | July 1, 2010 | $100 | $400 | $0 |
| 2 | December 15, 2010 | January 15, 2011 | $0 | $300 | $150 |
| 3 | April 1, 2011 | May 1, 2011 | | $200 | $200 |
| 4 | November 15, 2011 | January 1, 2012 | | $0 | $500 |
| 5 | April 1, 2012 | May 1, 2012 | | | $200 |

(Incremental payments by calendar year)

## Part a (0.5 points)

Construct an annual incremental accident year paid loss triangle as of December 31, 2012.

### Answer

| AY | 12 | 24 | 36 |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | 100 | 700 | 150 |
| 2011 | 200 | 700 | |
| 2012 | 200 | | |

### Explanation

Assign each claim to its accident year (year of the accident date). Incremental payments are the amounts paid in each calendar year.

**AY 2010** (Claims 1 and 2):
- Age 12 (CY 2010): Claim 1 pays $100 → $100
- Age 24 (CY 2011): Claim 1 pays $400, Claim 2 pays $300 → $700
- Age 36 (CY 2012): Claim 1 pays $0, Claim 2 pays $150 → $150

**AY 2011** (Claims 3 and 4):
- Age 12 (CY 2011): Claim 3 pays $200, Claim 4 pays $0 → $200
- Age 24 (CY 2012): Claim 3 pays $200, Claim 4 pays $500 → $700

**AY 2012** (Claim 5):
- Age 12 (CY 2012): Claim 5 pays $200 → $200

### Examiner Report

The vast majority of candidates correctly answered this question. A small number made a calculation mistake or wrote a cumulative triangle instead of incremental.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Construct an annual cumulative report year paid loss triangle as of December 31, 2012.

### Answer

| RY | 12 | 24 | 36 |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | 100 | 500 | 500 |
| 2011 | 500 | 850 | |
| 2012 | 700 | | |

### Explanation

Assign each claim to its report year (year the claim was first reported). Cumulative payments sum all payments through each evaluation date.

**RY 2010** (Claim 1 reported July 1, 2010):
- Age 12 (as of 12/31/2010): Claim 1 cumulative = $100
- Age 24 (as of 12/31/2011): Claim 1 cumulative = $100 + $400 = $500
- Age 36 (as of 12/31/2012): Claim 1 cumulative = $100 + $400 + $0 = $500

**RY 2011** (Claims 2, 3, 4 reported in 2011):
- Age 12 (as of 12/31/2011): Claim 2 = $300, Claim 3 = $200, Claim 4 = $0 → cumulative = $500
- Age 24 (as of 12/31/2012): Claim 2 += $150, Claim 3 += $200, Claim 4 += $500 → cumulative = $500 + $350 = $850

**RY 2012** (Claim 5 reported May 1, 2012):
- Age 12 (as of 12/31/2012): Claim 5 = $200. Also Claim 4 (reported Jan 1, 2012) = $500 → cumulative = $700

### Examiner Report

The vast majority of candidates correctly answered this question. A small number made a calculation mistake or wrote an incremental triangle instead of cumulative.

## Part c (0.5 points)

Assess whether an accident year approach or a report year approach is more appropriate for reserving auto liability insurance.

### Answer

Accident year is generally more appropriate for auto liability. However, report year is more appropriate if changes in the social or legal climate cause severity to correlate more with report date than accident date.

Alternatively: Accident year is more appropriate because auto liability is a long-tailed line requiring IBNR projection. The report year method does not project pure IBNR (claims incurred but not yet reported).

### Explanation

The accident year method groups claims by when the underlying event occurred, which aligns with the economic incurrence of the liability. It is the standard approach for most liability lines because:

1. It captures pure IBNR — claims that occurred in the accident year but have not yet been reported.
2. It isolates the loss experience by underwriting period, enabling better matching of losses to the exposures that generated them.

The report year method is more appropriate in specific circumstances where there is no true IBNR (e.g., claims-made policies) or where social/legal changes make the report date more economically meaningful than the accident date.

### Examiner Report

Candidates generally answered this question correctly. Some responded that accident year is more appropriate because auto claims are typically reported quickly — this was not accepted. Another common incorrect answer was that report year should be used since auto has a long tail or large reporting lag.

## Part d (0.5 points)

Assess whether annual or quarterly triangles are more appropriate for a small company selling a long-tailed line of business.

### Answer

Annual triangles are more appropriate. A small company's quarterly data will be too thin and volatile, causing loss development factors to be unreliable. Larger annual volumes produce more stable, credible factors.

Alternatively: Quarterly triangles may be more appropriate because, for a growing book of business, changes in exposure volume will shift the average accident date within each annual period and distort annual development factors.

### Explanation

For a **small company**, the primary concern is data credibility:

- Quarterly data is divided into four time slices per year, reducing the volume per cell and increasing random fluctuation.
- Thin data leads to highly volatile age-to-age development factors that are difficult to select reliably.
- Annual triangles aggregate four quarters of experience, producing more stable factor selections.

For a long-tailed line, the development pattern already spans many years; quarterly resolution of early development adds little value if the resulting factors are unreliable.

Note: Seasonality is not a valid reason to prefer quarterly triangles in this context, as the question asks for a reason specific to small companies.

### Examiner Report

Candidates generally answered this question correctly. Some responded that quarterly is more appropriate because there can be seasonality in the claims — this was not accepted, since the question was asking for a reason that is specific to small companies. Credit was not awarded for that response.
