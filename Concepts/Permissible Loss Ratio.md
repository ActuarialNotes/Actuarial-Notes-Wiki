**Permissible Loss Ratio** (also called the target loss ratio) is the loss ratio that, if exactly achieved, produces the insurer's desired underwriting profit margin after covering all expense provisions; it is the benchmark used in the [[Loss Ratio Method]] to determine indicated rate changes.

> $$\text{PLR} = 1 - V - \frac{F}{1+V} - Q \approx 1 - \text{Expense Ratio} - \text{Profit Provision}$$

- $V$ = variable expense ratio (as a proportion of premium), $F$ = fixed expense ratio (as a proportion of premium), $Q$ = target underwriting profit provision
- A higher variable expense ratio lowers the permissible loss ratio, leaving less room for losses before the book becomes unprofitable
- The permissible loss ratio is also equal to 100% minus the combined ratio target, which is equivalent to $1 - (V + F + Q)$
- When the actual projected loss ratio exceeds the PLR, rates are inadequate; when it falls below the PLR, rates are excessive

> [!example]- Computing the Permissible Loss Ratio {Example}
> An insurer has variable expenses of 22% of premium, fixed expenses of 8% of premium, and a target underwriting profit provision of 5%. What is the permissible loss ratio?
>
> > [!answer]-
> > $$\text{PLR} = 1 - 0.22 - 0.08 - 0.05 = 0.65 = 65\%$$
> > If the projected loss ratio is 72%, the indicated rate change is $72\%/65\% - 1 = +10.8\%$.
