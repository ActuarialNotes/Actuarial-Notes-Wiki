An insurer proposes to increase rates by 6.0% where many individual policy impacts will be above 10%. The insurer proposes a capping rule that will restrict premium changes at the policy level to plus or minus 10.0%.

## Part a (0.5 points)

Identify two problems that a capping rule may cause for an insurer.

### Answer

Two separate problems (any two of the following):

1. The insurer will not be charging what they should be to keep the fundamental insurance equation in balance and earn their target underwriting profit — rates will be inadequate for the capped policies.
2. Systems limitations: the capping rule must be programmed into rating systems, which can be complex when determining what gets capped, what does not, and how this changes the rating algorithm.
3. The capping rule may require a premium transition period, complicating administration.
4. Because policies that would otherwise receive increases above 10% are capped, the overall average rate change actually implemented will be less than the 6% indication — the insurer may not get all the rate it needs.
5. Adverse selection risk: knowing that large rate increases are capped, higher-risk insureds may stay on the book while lower-risk insureds who receive decreases may leave, worsening the insurer's portfolio mix.

### Explanation

A capping rule limits the premium change any single policy can experience at renewal. While this helps with retention, it creates tension with the actuarially indicated rates:

**Problem 1 — Rate inadequacy / equation imbalance:** The fundamental insurance equation requires that premium covers expected losses, expenses, and a target profit. When a policy's needed rate increase is greater than 10% but is capped, the insurer is collecting less than its indicated premium for that policy. The overall portfolio rate change will therefore fall short of the 6% indication.

**Problem 2 — Operational/systems complexity:** Implementing a capping rule requires programming the cap into underwriting and rating systems. This is technically complex: the system must identify what component of the premium change is being capped, how uncapped amounts are handled, and how multiple rating factor changes interact with the cap.

Additional concerns include the need for a premium transition plan (phasing in the remaining rate increase over future renewals) and adverse selection risk, since policies that would benefit from large decreases might leave (if decreases are also capped at −10%), while policies that need large increases stay at inadequate prices.

### Examiner Report

Candidates not receiving partial credit often restated the same idea twice or identified two sides of the same issue. To receive full credit, two separate and distinct problems were required. Any of the following pairs (or similar pairings) were accepted:

- Rates will be inadequate / The insurer will not earn its target profit
- System complexity / implementation burden
- Need for a premium transition period / rate may not be fully achieved
- Adverse selection risk

## Part b (0.75 points)

Explain why an insurer would propose a capping rule in light of the problems identified in part a. above.

### Answer

Despite the problems, an insurer proposes a capping rule primarily to maximize policyholder retention. A large premium increase at renewal — even if actuarially justified — can cause policyholders to shop competitors and leave. By capping the increase at 10%, the insurer reduces the shock to individual policyholders, retains more of its book, and can recover the remaining needed rate increase over subsequent renewal cycles.

### Explanation

The core business rationale is the trade-off between actuarial precision and retention:

**Retention concern:** If policyholders who should receive a 20% or 30% rate increase instead see a 10% cap, many will renew rather than shop. Retaining profitable policies — even at temporarily inadequate rates — can be more valuable than losing them to competitors while being technically "correct."

**Regulatory constraint:** Some jurisdictions restrict the maximum rate change a policyholder can experience at any single renewal. In these environments, a capping rule may not be discretionary — it may be legally mandated.

**Long-run adequacy:** An insurer can plan to phase in the remaining rate over future renewals ("premium transition plan"), ultimately reaching adequate rate levels while maintaining retention. The more profitable business retained now generates more long-run premium volume even if current margins are slightly compressed.

$$\text{Trade-off: } \underbrace{\text{Short-term inadequacy}}_{\text{cost of cap}} \text{ vs. } \underbrace{\text{higher retention } \rightarrow \text{ long-run profit}}_{\text{benefit of cap}}$$

### Examiner Report

On part b, very few candidates received only partial credit. Examples of full credit statements include:

- "An insurer's retention may decline if a rate cap is not adopted."
- "State laws may require a maximum rate change be followed for all policies."

Candidates who tied the answer back to maximizing long-run profitability through retention generally received full credit. A single, well-explained business reason was sufficient.
