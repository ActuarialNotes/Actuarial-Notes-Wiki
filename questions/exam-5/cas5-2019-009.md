The following graphs show two competing generalized linear models' (GLMs) predictions versus the data used in modeling ("training") and a hold-out sample. Data in each graph has been sorted into equal volume deciles, ranked from low to high actual loss.

**Training Data** — Both models vs. actual pure premium, by decile (1 = lowest, 10 = highest actual loss):

- Actual: steadily increasing from ~\$500 at decile 1 to ~\$3,400 at decile 10
- Model 1 (dashed): near-perfect fit to actual in training data, tracking closely through all deciles
- Model 2 (solid): flat/dampened response, consistent but substantially below actual at higher deciles

**Hold-Out Sample Data** — Same axes:

- Actual: increases from ~\$500 to ~\$2,500 with some variability
- Model 1 (dashed): erratic after decile 5, overshoots actual at high deciles
- Model 2 (solid): smooth upward trend, broadly consistent with actual though with less amplitude

## Part a (0.5 points)

Assess Model 1.

### Explanation

Model 1 achieves a near-perfect fit on the training data, tracking the actual pure premium closely across all deciles. However, on the hold-out sample, Model 1's predictions become erratic and volatile, particularly above decile 5, where it diverges significantly from actual results. This pattern indicates that Model 1 is **overfit** — it has captured noise in the training data rather than the underlying signal, and therefore fails to generalize to new data.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to comment on both training and hold-out performance for Model 1 and identify the overfitting pattern. A complete assessment required at least two observations: the strong training fit and the deterioration in hold-out performance.

Common mistakes included only listing one brief observation; evaluating the data sets rather than the GLMs; not specifying which model the commentary was for; commenting on low data volume (the prompt stated all deciles have equal volume); and recommending a model without supporting commentary.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Assess Model 2.

### Explanation

Model 2 shows a consistent, dampened prediction in both the training and hold-out samples. In training data, it does not match the amplitude of the actual results — it underestimates at high deciles. However, in the hold-out sample, Model 2 performs more reliably than Model 1, maintaining a steady upward trend that broadly tracks actual results. Model 2 appears to focus on the underlying signal rather than noise, suggesting it is **less overfit** and more likely to generalize well. The flat response may indicate that the model is missing some predictive variables.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to comment on both training and hold-out performance for Model 2 and note its consistency relative to Model 1.

Common mistakes included only listing one brief observation; not specifying which model the commentary was for; and recommending a model without any supporting commentary (recommendation of one model over the other was not required).
