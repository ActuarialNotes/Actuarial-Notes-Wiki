An insurance company has recently entered a new state and plans to invest heavily on marketing in an attempt to aggressively grow its homeowners book of business. Relative to the low initial premium in the state, these marketing expenses will be significant.

The company's senior management proposes that the actuary develop a rate level where the expense provisions reflect only the typical variable costs. Construct a thorough argument in support of this proposal.

### Answer

Several arguments support building expense provisions around only the typical (ongoing) variable costs, excluding the abnormally large initial marketing spend:

**1. Marketing expenses are non-recurring and not expected to persist.**
The heavy upfront marketing investment is a one-time cost to enter the market and build brand awareness. Rates are designed to cover future costs over the entire policy period, not to recover sunk acquisition costs. Including a transient expense in an ongoing rate level would cause rates to be artificially high during the build-up phase and then fall dramatically once marketing spending normalizes — an unstable and counterproductive rate trajectory.

**2. Including the atypical fixed cost would grossly overstate the expense ratio at low premium volume.**
When premium volume is small (as it is in a newly-entered state), even a moderately-sized marketing budget produces an extremely high expense-to-premium ratio. Using this ratio as the expense provision would embed a spike that rapidly disappears as the book grows:

- Year 1: Marketing cost = \$1M; Premium = \$1M → Expense ratio = 100%
- Year 2: Marketing cost = \$1M; Premium = \$10M → Expense ratio = 10%
- Year 3: Marketing cost = \$1M; Premium = \$100M → Expense ratio = 1%

Rates set on the Year-1 ratio would be far above market, hindering the very growth the company is trying to achieve.

**3. Rates that include atypical fixed costs violate the principle that rates should be prospectively adequate but not excessive.**
Charging policyholders for an expense that will not exist in subsequent years results in rates that are excessive over the life of the book. The CAS Ratemaking Principles state that rates should reflect the expected value of future costs; costs that are clearly temporary and abnormal should not be built into permanent rate levels.

**4. Asset-share / long-term profitability model supports the approach.**
Companies entering new markets routinely accept near-term losses to build a profitable renewal book. Renewal policyholders generate better loss experience and require far less acquisition spending than new business. The asset-share model recognizes that writing business at a short-term loss during the growth phase is economically rational when the long-run renewal profit stream more than compensates. Setting rates to recover only typical variable expenses is consistent with this strategy.

**5. Competitive pricing is essential for market entry and would be undermined by loading the atypical expense.**
Loading the full marketing cost into the rate would produce prices significantly above the competition, making it nearly impossible to write the volume of business needed to spread the overhead — a self-defeating outcome. Pricing to typical variable costs keeps the company competitive while management separately finances the one-time market-entry investment.

### Examiner Report

In general, candidates did not provide a thorough argument in support of the proposal. Often, candidates provided either one well-developed point or only briefly touched on two or three points. To earn full credit, candidates needed at least two well-developed points or at least four basically discussed points. Common errors: arguing against the proposal instead of for it, misinterpreting the question to mean all expenses should be treated as variable or should include the extra marketing expense, and offering arguments that were not clear or not thorough.
