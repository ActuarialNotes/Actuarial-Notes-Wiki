Given the following data evaluated as of December 31, 2018:

| Accident Year | Net of Reinsurance Reported Claims (\$000s) at 12 months | at 24 months | at 36 months | Ultimate Frequency |
|---------------|----------------------------------------------------------|--------------|--------------|-------------------|
| 2016          | 5,000                                                    | 5,500        | 5,700        | 15%               |
| 2017          | 5,800                                                    | 6,400        |              | 15%               |
| 2018          | 12,000                                                   |              |              | 15%               |

- There are no catastrophes or large claims in accident year 2018.
- No changes have been made to claims handling practices.

## Part a (0.5 points)

Briefly describe two scenarios that could drive the elevated reported claims in accident year 2018.

### Explanation
Any two of the following:

**Scenario 1: Per claim severity has increased**, possibly due to a mix of business change (e.g., writing more severe risks).

**Scenario 2: Tort reform** that has increased losses paid after 2017, raising claim costs for 2018.

**Scenario 3: Deductibles on policies were reduced or removed**, resulting in more claims being captured in reported figures.

**Scenario 4: More business written** — a significant increase in exposure (volume) would inflate aggregate reported claims even at constant frequency and severity.

**Scenario 5: Acquisition of another business** bringing in a new block of claims.

**Scenario 6: Increasing the Stop Loss threshold** so more losses fall into the retained (net) layer.

**Scenario 7: Decreasing the Quota Share percentage**, so a greater share of each claim is retained net of reinsurance.

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to identify a cause of the elevated reported claims.

Common mistakes included:
- Not providing two causes.
- Listing a change in case reserve adequacy as a cause, as that was stated to not be the case in the question.
- Listing a large or catastrophe claim as a cause, as that was stated to not be the case in the question.
- Listing a change in frequency as a cause, since frequency was stated to be constant at 15%.

## Part b (1 point)

Discuss a reasonable approach to estimate ultimate claims for each scenario identified in part a. above.

### Explanation
The appropriate development approach depends on the cause of the elevated claims:

**If severity increased (mix of business or tort reform):**
Use a **frequency/severity method** to directly address and account for the increased severity. This separates the frequency component (which is stable at 15%) from the severity component (which has changed), allowing you to project ultimate severity on its own trend and multiply by expected claim counts.

Alternatively, use the **reported development technique** since it is responsive to changes in the claims ratio; this is appropriate when there are no claims handling changes. If the new business is similar to existing, reported development factors may be applied directly. If not, separate the claims into homogeneous groups and apply the reported development method to each group.

**If volume increased (more business written / acquisition):**
Use the **Bornhuetter-Ferguson technique**, which blends reported claims with an a priori expected claims ratio. B-F will take into account the growth in the company in the unreported portion of the ultimate estimate, anchoring the unreported piece to an expected claims level rather than being driven purely by the elevated reported figure.

Alternatively, use the **expected claims ratio approach** which accounts for the change in premium (exposure) underlying the elevated volume.

**If reinsurance terms changed (Stop Loss threshold / Quota Share):**
Estimate claims using **gross insurance data** if available, then make adjustments based on the new contract. Development methods can be applied to gross claims if consistent, or the development technique can be applied to net claims with prior years adjusted to the new terms so all years are on an apples-to-apples comparison.

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to identify a development method that addresses the scenarios identified in part a.

Common mistakes included:
- Not providing two approaches (one for each scenario).
- Listing a correct approach without adequate detail or explanation.
- Listing an approach that did not account for the cause identified in part a.
