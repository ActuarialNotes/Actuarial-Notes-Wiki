A company writes homeowners insurance in a large state divided in half by a mountain range. The company currently uses two geographic rating territories, one on either side of the mountains, as the range has an effect on weather patterns. Each territory has sufficient exposures for its loss experience to be considered fully credible.

## Part a (0.5 points)

Briefly discuss two disadvantages of the company's current territorial rating approach.

### Explanation

Any two of the following disadvantages:

1. **Insufficient granularity / too few territories:** The company uses only two territories for a large state. This means only the mountain range is accounted for, while many other geographic risk factors (urban vs. rural concentration, proximity to fire stations, local crime rates, coastal exposure, etc.) are ignored. The two relativities are unlikely to adequately capture the true geographic risk variation across the entire state.

2. **Territories are not homogeneous:** By splitting the state on only one criterion (the mountain range / weather patterns), each territory contains a highly heterogeneous mix of risks. Homogeneous risks within a territory are a key goal of territorial rating — without homogeneity, there is adverse selection risk as insureds on the "favorable" end of each territory are undercharged and those on the "unfavorable" end are overcharged.

3. **Competitive disadvantage:** Other insurers that use more refined territories may be able to identify and underwrite lower-risk segments within each territory more accurately. The company may attract disproportionately higher-risk insureds (adverse selection) from competitors who use more granular territories.

4. **Incomplete peril consideration:** Homeowner claims arise from many perils beyond weather — theft, fire, liability, water damage, etc. Defining territories based solely on the mountain range's effect on weather patterns may not capture geographic risk differences for these other perils.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to understand why a company would not want to use overly broad territorial definitions.

Common mistakes included:
- Providing only one disadvantage
- Providing advantages of the company's territorial rating approach

## Part b (2 points)

Discuss the process by which an actuary would develop new rating territory definitions for this state. Briefly explain a consideration for each step in the process.

### Explanation

The standard process for developing new rating territory definitions involves four steps:

**Step 1: Define basic geographic units.**
Divide the state into small, granular geographic units — typically zip codes or counties. These units serve as the building blocks from which territories will ultimately be assembled.

*Consideration:* Zip codes offer greater granularity but their boundaries change over time (postal service updates), which can create data continuity issues. Counties are more stable but less granular. The actuary must balance granularity against data stability and administrative practicality.

**Step 2: Quantify each geographic unit's systematic risk using a GLM.**
Estimate the geo-demographic and geo-physical relativities for each basic unit using a generalized linear model (GLM), controlling for other rating variables (e.g., age of home, coverage amount, construction type) that may be correlated with geography.

*Consideration:* Geographic units are often highly correlated with other rating variables (e.g., urban zip codes tend to have older homes, higher property values). The GLM must isolate the geographic signal from these correlated variables to avoid double-counting their effect in both the territory factor and other rating variables.

**Step 3: Calculate and smooth residual geographic risk across units.**
After the GLM accounts for known rating variables, compute the residual geographic factor — the remaining unexplained geographic variation — and apply spatial smoothing across adjacent units to avoid abrupt discontinuities at borders.

*Consideration:* The choice of smoothing method matters. Distance-based smoothing (inversely weighting neighbors by distance) works well for continuous perils like weather. Adjacency-based smoothing (weighting only touching neighbors equally) may be more appropriate for spatially discontinuous perils like theft (a theft-prone neighborhood may not spill over to the adjacent one). The actuary must select the smoothing approach appropriate to the dominant perils.

**Step 4: Cluster basic geographic units into final rating territories.**
Group the basic units into a manageable number of territories using a clustering algorithm, so that units with similar relativities are in the same territory.

*Consideration:* The actuary must determine how many territories to create and which clustering method to use (e.g., quantile-based clustering creates equal-sized groups; similarity-based clustering groups units by proximity of their relativities). Practical constraints such as regulatory requirements, minimum credibility per territory, and competitive considerations also factor into this decision.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to understand the steps involved in developing new rating territories.

Common mistakes included:
- Simply listing each step without providing any consideration for each step
- Neglecting to cluster geographic units into final rating territories
- Describing criteria for evaluating rating variables (a separate concept) rather than the territory development process
