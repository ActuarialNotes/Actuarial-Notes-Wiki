An insurer writes private passenger automobile liability coverage in two states. Given the following bodily injury (BI) and property damage (PD) information as of December 31, 2018:

**State A:**

| Accident Year | Earned Exposure | Ultimate Claim Count BI | Ultimate Claim Count PD |
|---------------|----------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| 2016 | 36,000 | 50 | 950 |
| 2017 | 37,800 | 60 | 1,140 |
| 2018 | 41,580 | 72 | 1,368 |

| Coverage | Paid Age-to-Ultimate Factors 12-to-Ult | Paid Age-to-Ultimate Factors 24-to-Ult | Paid Age-to-Ultimate Factors 36-to-Ult |
|----------|---------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| BI | 5.00 | 2.10 | 1.05 |
| PD | 1.20 | 1.01 | 1.00 |

| Coverage | Ultimate Severity |
|----------|-------------------|
| BI | 15,000 |
| PD | 5,000 |

**State B:**

| Accident Year | Earned Exposure | Ultimate Claim Count BI | Ultimate Claim Count PD |
|---------------|----------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| 2016 | 100,000 | 1,250 | 5,000 |
| 2017 | 105,000 | 1,300 | 5,200 |
| 2018 | 110,250 | 1,375 | 5,500 |

| Coverage | Paid Age-to-Ultimate Factors 12-to-Ult | Paid Age-to-Ultimate Factors 24-to-Ult | Paid Age-to-Ultimate Factors 36-to-Ult |
|----------|---------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| BI | 9.00 | 2.10 | 1.30 |
| PD | 1.30 | 1.10 | 1.00 |

| Coverage | Ultimate Severity |
|----------|-------------------|
| BI | 10,000 |
| PD | 2,500 |

- There is no exposure, claim count, or severity trend.

## Part a (0.5 points)

Discuss an argument for performing a bodily injury and property damage combined unpaid claims analysis for state A.

### Explanation
The BI claim count for State A is small (50–72 per year) relative to the PD claim count (950–1,368 per year). Because BI has low claim counts, the data is not fully credible on its own. Combining BI and PD for State A provides more data and greater credibility, producing a more stable and accurate unpaid claims estimate than using BI data alone.

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to review two sets of data and determine that one set was not credible on its own due to low claim counts, and that combining it with the second set would produce more accurate results.

Common mistakes included:

- Stating that data or exposure was not credible instead of identifying claim count as the issue.
- Misreading the question and comparing State A and State B data instead of comparing BI and PD within State A.
- Stating that BI and PD should not be combined and providing a reason for separation.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Discuss an argument against performing an all-state combined unpaid claims analysis for bodily injury.

### Explanation
The BI data for State A and State B are not homogeneous. The paid age-to-ultimate development factors differ significantly between the two states (e.g., State A 12-to-ultimate factor is 5.00 vs. State B's 9.00), indicating different development patterns. Additionally, the exposure bases and BI claim counts are growing at different rates across the two states. Combining the two states' BI data would mix unlike populations and distort the development pattern.

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to review the two sets of data and determine that they should not be combined because they are not homogeneous — the states have different paid development factors (different development patterns) and different growth rates.

Common mistakes included:

- Making the opposite argument that State A and State B should be combined.
- Misreading the question and comparing BI and PD within a single state instead of comparing BI across State A and State B.

## Part c (0.75 points)

Fully evaluate management's assertion that State A should be charged a higher premium than State B for bodily injury coverage due to its higher ultimate severity.

### Explanation
Management's assertion is incorrect. Pricing decisions should be based on loss costs (pure premiums), not severity alone. Frequency must also be considered.

Calculate the BI pure premium (loss cost per exposure) for each state:

$$\text{Pure Premium} = \frac{\text{Ultimate Claim Count} \times \text{Ultimate Severity}}{\text{Earned Exposure}}$$

**State A — AY 2018:**

$$\frac{72 \times 15{,}000}{41{,}580} = \frac{1{,}080{,}000}{41{,}580} \approx 25.97$$

**State B — AY 2018:**

$$\frac{1{,}375 \times 10{,}000}{110{,}250} = \frac{13{,}750{,}000}{110{,}250} \approx 124.72$$

Although State A has higher severity (\$15,000 vs. \$10,000), State B has a much higher frequency, resulting in a pure premium roughly five times larger (\$124.72 vs. \$25.97). State B should be charged the higher premium for BI coverage.

| State | AY | Exposure | Ult BI Count | Ult BI Severity | Loss Cost |
|-------|----|----------|-------------|-----------------|-----------|
| A | 2016 | 36,000 | 50 | 15,000 | 20.83 |
| A | 2017 | 37,800 | 60 | 15,000 | 23.81 |
| A | 2018 | 41,580 | 72 | 15,000 | 25.97 |
| B | 2016 | 100,000 | 1,250 | 10,000 | 125.00 |
| B | 2017 | 105,000 | 1,300 | 10,000 | 123.81 |
| B | 2018 | 110,250 | 1,375 | 10,000 | 124.72 |

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to realize that pricing decisions should not be made based on severity alone — frequency must also be considered. Candidates needed to calculate pure premium (frequency × severity, or ultimate claim counts × severity ÷ exposure) and show that the results contradict management's conclusion.

Common mistakes included:

- Applying loss development factors to ultimate claim counts.
- Combining PD with BI in the calculations.
- Calculating ultimate losses without dividing by exposure.
- Not directly addressing the comparison between State A and State B (e.g., discussing credibility of State A's data, complements of credibility, or waiting for more data, without computing and comparing pure premiums).
