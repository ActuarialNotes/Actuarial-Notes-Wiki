For each scenario described below, justify an appropriate reserving technique for estimating the unpaid claim liabilities at 12 months maturity.

## Part a (0.5 points)

Excess-of-loss reinsurance with an average attachment point of $100 million on product liability policies.

### Answer

Use the **Bornhuetter-Ferguson (BF) method** (or the **Expected Claims / Cape Cod method**).

At 12 months maturity, excess-of-loss reinsurance with a $100 million attachment point will have extremely thin, volatile data — claims must exceed $100 million before any loss is ceded, so very few (possibly zero) claims will have been reported at this early stage. Development factors would be highly leveraged and unreliable. The BF method is appropriate because it blends the sparse observed data with a stable a priori expected loss estimate, giving the estimate stability at early maturities.

The Cape Cod method is **not** acceptable because the expected loss ratio it uses is derived from the (essentially non-existent) experience at this attachment level — there is insufficient data to develop a credible Cape Cod expected loss ratio.

### Explanation

When data is thin and volatile — as it will be at high excess-of-loss attachment points — methods that rely heavily on observed historical development are unreliable. The BF and Expected Claims methods anchor the estimate to an independent a priori expectation, which is more stable when actual experience is sparse.

### Examiner Report

Many candidates acknowledged that data at this attachment point would be thin and volatile. About half the candidates received either partial or full credit. The most common method listed that did not receive credit was the Cape Cod method. This was not accepted because the expected loss ratio used in the method is based off the experience and at such a high attachment point there is little to no experience.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Basic limits auto liability for an insurer that has recently implemented a new claims processing system to make faster payments to insureds without changing the company's reserving methodology.

### Answer

Use the **Berquist-Sherman paid development adjustment** (settlement rate adjustment).

The faster payment system has accelerated the settlement of claims without changing case reserving philosophy, so paid claims development patterns will be distorted — more claims are being paid sooner than the historical triangle reflects. Both the paid development method and the reported development method will be distorted by this change (paid because payments are faster; reported because the new system also affects how quickly claims appear in the triangle). The Berquist-Sherman settlement rate adjustment re-states historical paid development triangles to reflect current payment speed, restoring the consistency needed to apply the development method.

The reported (incurred) development method is **not** acceptable with a simple justification that it is unaffected by the payment change — it is also distorted.

### Explanation

When a company changes its claims settlement speed but not its reserving philosophy, the paid loss triangle is non-stationary — earlier diagonal data was developed under the old (slower) payment pattern, while the most recent diagonal reflects the new (faster) pattern. Applying development factors from the old pattern to current data would understate unpaid claims. The Berquist-Sherman adjustment reconstructs the historical paid triangle as if the current payment speed had always been in effect.

### Examiner Report

A large portion of candidates received no credit for using the reported development method with justification that this method will not be impacted by the change. Both the paid and reported development methods are distorted by the new claims processing system. For full credit, the candidate needed to acknowledge there would be a change in the claim reporting pattern and select a method that would account for this appropriately, such as the paid Berquist-Sherman method. Some responses said case reserves would be impacted and to use an incurred Berquist-Sherman method — no credit was awarded as the reserving philosophy did not change.

## Part c (0.5 points)

Self-insured workers compensation for a large corporation in a state where the statute of limitations for filing a claim has been recently reduced.

### Answer

Use the **Expected Claims method** (or the **Bornhuetter-Ferguson method**).

Reducing the statute of limitations means that claims must now be filed within a shorter time window. This will cause a surge of claims to be reported at early maturities (as claimants rush to file before the new deadline), distorting both paid and reported development patterns. Neither the paid development method nor the reported development method will reliably reflect the future development pattern given this structural change in reporting. The Expected Claims or BF method relies on an a priori expectation of ultimate losses rather than on the distorted historical development patterns.

The Case Outstanding method is **not** appropriate for workers compensation — it is best suited for claims-made coverages.

### Explanation

A change in statute of limitations is a one-time structural event that invalidates the stationarity assumption underlying development methods. When the claim reporting pattern changes discontinuously, methods that rely on historical development factors produce unreliable projections. The Expected Claims method bypasses historical development entirely by estimating ultimate losses from first principles (e.g., expected loss ratios based on pricing assumptions).

### Examiner Report

Some candidates incorrectly interpreted the change in statute of limitations as a change in benefit limits (instead of a reduction in the time to file a claim), causing some candidates to say ultimate losses would increase/decrease as a result. A portion of the candidates that acknowledged there would be a speed up in claims filed stated that the paid development or paid BF methods would be suitable — no credit was awarded as there would be a change in the payment pattern. Some candidates listed the case outstanding method as an appropriate method as it works well with self-insurers; however, the case outstanding is best for claims-made coverage, which workers compensation is not.

## Part d (0.5 points)

Property catastrophe coverage in a year with higher-than-expected catastrophe losses reported to the insurer but not yet paid.

### Answer

Use the **Bornhuetter-Ferguson reported (incurred) development method**.

At 12 months maturity following a catastrophe, large losses have been reported but not yet paid. Applying paid development factors would be unreliable because the catastrophe event introduces atypical paid development patterns (actual losses are being developed from an unusually high reported base). The BF reported method uses reported (incurred) claims — which already reflect the catastrophe losses — combined with an a priori expected loss estimate, preventing the catastrophe losses from excessively inflating the projection. The BF approach also dampens the leverage effect of high CDFs at 12 months maturity.

The paid development method is **not** appropriate for property catastrophe: these claims pay out slowly relative to when they are reported, so paid CDFs are very high and leveraged at 12 months. Catastrophe modeling is also not an acceptable answer — it is a prospective pricing tool, not a reserving technique.

### Explanation

Property catastrophe claims differ from standard property losses: losses are reported relatively quickly after the event, but payments lag as loss adjusters assess complex claims. This means reported (incurred) data is more informative than paid data at early maturities after a catastrophe. The BF reported method captures the reported magnitude of the catastrophe while providing stability through the a priori component.

### Examiner Report

Many candidates mistook property catastrophe coverage for property coverage, leading to selections more fit for property coverage. For instance, many answered separating out the catastrophe portion and completing a separate analysis on this piece as a method — this received no credit. For points to be awarded for justification, the candidate needed to demonstrate understanding that there was a distortion due to higher-than-normal catastrophe activity but at the same time incorporate that into the method. A fair amount of responses suggested using the paid development method with a catastrophe load — this was not acceptable as the development method is inappropriate for this type of coverage. Catastrophe modeling was also listed; no credit was awarded because catastrophe modeling is not a reserving technique, but rather a prospective pricing tool.
