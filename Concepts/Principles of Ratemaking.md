**Principles of Ratemaking** are the four foundational guidelines established by the CAS governing how actuarially sound insurance rates must be constructed and evaluated.

- **Principle 1:** A rate is an estimate of the expected value of future costs — rates are prospective, based on statistical projections of losses, LAE, expenses, and profit
- **Principle 2:** A rate provides for all costs associated with the transfer of risk — this means losses, LAE, underwriting expenses, premium taxes, and a provision for profit and contingencies must all be reflected
- **Principle 3:** A rate provides for the costs associated with an individual risk transfer — risks should be segmented into classes that are homogeneous and actuarially justified, reflecting each group's expected cost
- **Principle 4:** A rate is reasonable and not excessive, inadequate, or unfairly discriminatory — the standard embedded in most state insurance regulations; differentials must reflect actual cost differences, not arbitrary distinctions

> [!example]- Applying Principle 2 to a Rate Calculation {Example}
> Expected pure premium (losses + LAE) $= \$250$. Variable expenses $= 20\%$, fixed expenses $= 10\%$, profit $= 5\%$.
>
> > [!answer]-
> > $$\text{PLR} = 1 - 0.20 - 0.10 - 0.05 = 0.65$$
> > $$\text{Rate} = \$250 / 0.65 = \$385 \text{ per exposure}$$
> > All cost components (Principle 2) are covered; the rate reflects expected future costs (Principle 1).
