Given the following information:

**Reported Losses Gross of Reinsurance ($000,000)**

| Accident Year | Age 12 | Age 24 | Age 36 |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $20 | $40 | $60 |
| 2011 | $15 | $30 | |
| 2012 | $18 | | |

**Reported Losses Net of Reinsurance ($000,000)**

| Accident Year | Age 12 | Age 24 | Age 36 |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $16 | $32 | $30 |
| 2011 | $14 | $24 | |
| 2012 | $11 | | |

- Each accident year has a 20% quota share reinsurance treaty.
- Each accident year has an aggregate stop loss treaty attaching at $30 million applied after quota share.
- Assume the gross data is correct.

## Part a (0.5 points)

Review the loss triangles above and briefly discuss whether the net data is reasonable based on both reinsurance treaties.

### Answer

The net data contains errors for AY 2010 at age 36, AY 2011 at ages 12 and 24, and AY 2012 at age 12. The net data as presented is **not fully reasonable**.

### Explanation

Under the two treaties, the net retained loss for each AY at each age should be:

1. Apply 20% quota share: net before stop loss = gross × 0.80
2. Apply aggregate stop loss at $30M (net of quota share): net after stop loss = min(net before stop loss, $30M)

**Expected net amounts (before checking stop loss):**

| AY | Age | Gross | Net before SL (×0.80) | Stop loss cap ($30M) | Expected Net |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | 12 | $20 | $16 | not triggered | $16 |
| 2010 | 24 | $40 | $32 | triggered ($32 > $30) | **$30** |
| 2010 | 36 | $60 | $48 | triggered ($48 > $30) | **$30** |
| 2011 | 12 | $15 | $12 | not triggered | **$12** |
| 2011 | 24 | $30 | $24 | not triggered | **$24** |
| 2012 | 12 | $18 | $14.4 | not triggered | **$14.4** |

Comparing to the given net data:

- **AY 2010, age 12** ($16): Correct.
- **AY 2010, age 24** ($32): The stop loss should cap net losses at $30M, but the data shows $32M — this is unreasonably high; the stop loss should have triggered.
- **AY 2010, age 36** ($30): Correct (stop loss cap applies).
- **AY 2011, age 12** ($14): Should be $12 after the 20% quota share — the data is too high relative to what quota share alone would produce (15 × 0.80 = 12, not 14).
- **AY 2011, age 24** ($24): Correct.
- **AY 2012, age 12** ($11): Should be $14.4 after the 20% quota share — the data is too low.

Therefore, net data is not reasonable for AY 2010 at age 24, AY 2011 at age 12, and AY 2012 at age 12.

### Examiner Report

Many candidates answered this question correctly. Most candidates who did not receive full credit missed either the stop loss error in AY 2010 (at age 24 the net exceeds $30M) or both of the quota share errors in AY 2011 and AY 2012 at age 12.

## Part b (0.75 points)

Explain and justify an approach for estimating gross, ceded, and net ultimate claim estimates.

### Answer

Develop the gross data using the reported development method; then apply the reinsurance treaties to the gross ultimate to estimate ceded and net ultimates. Do not use the net data directly, as it contains errors.

### Explanation

Because the gross data is stated to be correct and the net data contains errors (it cannot be relied upon directly), the recommended approach is:

1. **Develop gross ultimate claims** using the loss development technique applied to the gross reported loss triangle. The gross data is credible and free of treaty distortions.

2. **Apply the reinsurance treaties to the gross ultimates** to estimate the ceded portion:
   - Apply the 20% quota share to the gross ultimate: ceded quota share = 20% × gross ultimate.
   - Apply the aggregate stop loss treaty (attaching at $30M, after quota share): if net after quota share exceeds $30M, the stop loss cedes the excess.

3. **Net ultimate = Gross ultimate − Ceded ultimate** (quota share + stop loss cessions).

This approach ensures that the ultimate estimates are consistent and that the errors in the net triangle do not propagate into the reserve estimates. Directly developing the net data would produce distorted development factors due to the erroneous entries.

### Examiner Report

Most candidates answered this question correctly or received nearly full credit. The majority of points taken off were for not correcting/discarding the defective net data. Most candidates knew to develop gross losses and were able to explain the relationship between gross, net, and ceded.

## Part c (0.75 points)

Predict the relationship between the gross reported loss tail factor and the ceded reported loss tail factor. Explain the impact of both the quota share agreement and the stop loss agreement.

### Answer

The **ceded reported loss tail factor will be greater than the gross reported loss tail factor**.

### Explanation

Consider the impact of each treaty on the tail:

**Quota share (20% cession):**
The quota share is purely proportional — it cedes a flat 20% of every dollar of gross loss at every age. A proportional treaty does not change the shape of the development pattern, so the quota share alone has **no effect** on the tail factor. The ceded development factor under quota share alone would equal the gross development factor.

**Aggregate stop loss (attaching at $30M net of quota share):**
The stop loss treaty creates a non-linear (leveraged) effect on the tail. Before the stop loss attachment point ($30M) is reached, all additional emerging losses remain on the net side. Once the net retained losses (after quota share) exceed $30M, any further development flows entirely to the ceded (stop loss) layer. In the tail period, many AYs that have already reached or are approaching the $30M stop loss attachment will cede a higher proportion of late-emerging losses to the stop loss reinsurer.

Because late-emerging losses (in the tail) are disproportionately ceded under the stop loss treaty, the **ceded loss development pattern has more weight in the tail** relative to the gross pattern. Therefore:

$$\text{Ceded tail factor} > \text{Gross tail factor}$$

The stop loss makes the ceded development pattern more leveraged — a larger fraction of total ceded losses emerges late compared to the gross pattern.

### Examiner Report

Few candidates received full credit on this question. A majority of candidates misread the question and assumed it asked them to compare gross and **net** tail factors (rather than gross and **ceded**). Of those who did compare ceded and gross tails, many got the direction of the relationship wrong. For candidates who correctly explained the relationship, some were unable to separately explain the impact of the quota share (no effect on relative pattern) and the stop loss (increases the ceded tail factor due to leveraged development above the attachment point).
