Given the following data for an insurance company evaluated as of December 31, 2018:

| Accident Year | Cumulative Reported Claims (\$000s) at 12 | Cumulative Reported Claims (\$000s) at 24 | Cumulative Reported Claims (\$000s) at 36 | Cumulative Reported Claims (\$000s) at 48 |
|---------------|------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| 2015 | 900 | 2,150 | 3,125 | 3,900 |
| 2016 | 800 | 2,075 | 3,225 | |
| 2017 | 850 | 2,125 | | |
| 2018 | 950 | | | |

| 1.100 | 48 to ultimate reported claim development factor |
|-------|--------------------------------------------------|

## Part a (0.75 points)

Calculate the company's accident year 2018 ultimate claims using the reported claim development technique.

### Answer
4892

### Explanation
**Step 1: Calculate age-to-age development factors**

$$12\text{-}24: \quad \frac{2{,}150}{900} = 2.389, \quad \frac{2{,}075}{800} = 2.594, \quad \frac{2{,}125}{850} = 2.500$$

$$24\text{-}36: \quad \frac{3{,}125}{2{,}150} = 1.453, \quad \frac{3{,}225}{2{,}075} = 1.554$$

$$36\text{-}48: \quad \frac{3{,}900}{3{,}125} = 1.248$$

**Step 2: Select age-to-age factors (volume-weighted averages or simple averages)**

| Period | AY 2015 | AY 2016 | AY 2017 | Selected |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|----------|
| 12-24 | 2.389 | 2.594 | 2.500 | 2.494 |
| 24-36 | 1.453 | 1.554 | | 1.504 |
| 36-48 | 1.248 | | | 1.248 |
| 48-Ult | | | | 1.100 (given) |

**Step 3: Calculate cumulative development factor for AY 2018 (at 12 months)**

$$\text{CDF}_{12\text{-Ult}} = 2.494 \times 1.504 \times 1.248 \times 1.100$$

$$= 2.494 \times 1.504 = 3.751; \quad 3.751 \times 1.248 = 4.681; \quad 4.681 \times 1.100 = 5.149$$

**Step 4: Apply CDF to AY 2018 current reported claims**

$$\text{Ultimate Claims} = 950 \times 5.149 \approx 4{,}892 \text{ (\$000s)}$$

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to:

- Accurately calculate reported claim development factors based on the triangle of data and select reasonable factors for each age-to-age period.
- Calculate a cumulative development factor that incorporates both the selected age-to-age factors and the given tail factor.
- Multiply the cumulative factor by the current reported claims for AY 2018.

Common mistakes included:

- Failure to include the tail factor (1.100) in the cumulative development factor calculation.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Briefly describe two factors the company should take into consideration prior to supplementing their own loss development data with industry loss development data.

### Explanation
Any two of the following factors:

1. **Mix of business**: The company's mix of business may differ from the industry's (e.g., different lines of business, policy sizes, or geographic territories), making the industry development pattern inappropriate.

2. **Gross vs. net of reinsurance**: The company should determine whether industry claim data is reported gross or net of reinsurance, since the company's own data may be on a different basis.

3. **Claims handling procedures**: If the industry uses different claims handling processes — such as different settlement rates, reserving philosophies, or case adequacy standards — its development pattern will differ from the company's.

4. **Large claims / data distortions**: The industry data may include large claims or unusual events that could distort the development factors.

5. **Data aggregation basis**: The company should determine whether industry data is aggregated on a policy year, accident year, or calendar year basis, to ensure comparability.

6. **Definition of claims**: Industry data may count claims differently (e.g., whether closed-without-payment counts are included, and whether losses are on a paid or reported basis).

7. **Credibility of own data**: The company should assess whether their own data is credible enough that industry data is even necessary.

8. **Cost vs. benefit**: The cost of obtaining industry data may not be justified if the added credibility is marginal.

9. **Independence**: If the company has a large market share, the industry data is not truly independent (e.g., if the company represents 90% of the market, industry data largely reflects the company's own experience).

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to provide two factors to consider when using industry data to supplement company data. Responses could address specific concerns that might make industry data irrelevant or inappropriate compared to the company's own data.

Common mistakes included:

- Assuming the industry development pattern must be similar to the company's pattern to use it. The rationale for using industry data is precisely that the company is skeptical of its own pattern — so the patterns may differ by design. What matters is whether definitional or operational differences make industry data irrelevant.
- Vague references to industry data being irrelevant or biased without identifying the specific cause.
- Providing two responses that were not sufficiently different from each other (e.g., "claim settlement rates" and "claim handling procedures" are too similar, as are "mix of products" and "mix of business").

## Part c (0.5 points)

Describe the impact that doubling the number of claims handlers may have on the reported development technique.

### Explanation
Doubling the number of claims handlers could affect the reported development pattern in multiple ways depending on how the change impacts claims operations:

**Scenario 1 — Faster settlement, no change in case adequacy:**
More claims adjusters accelerate the rate of claim settlement (closing claims faster with payment). If case reserve adequacy is unchanged, then paid claims increase while case reserves decrease proportionately, leaving reported (paid + case reserves) largely unaffected. In this scenario, the reported development pattern is not meaningfully distorted, and the reported development technique remains appropriate.

**Scenario 2 — Faster settlement with reserve run-off:**
If claims are closed faster, case reserves decline as claims move to payment more quickly. Reported claims in early maturities would be higher than historical patterns (larger paid component, smaller reserve), and the historical reported LDFs applied to current data could overstate ultimate claims because the LDFs reflected a slower-settlement environment.

**Scenario 3 — Faster case reserve establishment:**
More adjusters may mean reserves are set up more promptly after a claim is reported, increasing reported losses in early maturities. Applying historical LDFs — developed when reserves were established more slowly — would overestimate ultimate claims.

In general, any change in claims handling processes that alters the timing or adequacy of case reserves will distort the reported development pattern. The direction of the distortion (over- or underestimation) depends on which specific aspect of the operation changes.

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to:

- Describe how hiring additional claims adjusters affects the claim operation (e.g., faster settlement, faster case reserve establishment, or improved case adequacy).
- Describe how that operational change manifests in the reported development pattern — i.e., whether it causes the pattern to increase or decrease relative to historical factors.

Common mistakes included:

- Drawing an incorrect conclusion about the direction of impact, or making no statement at all about the effect on the reported development pattern.
- Stating that changed payment patterns have no impact on reported development without clarifying whether case reserves are also assumed to change.
- Demonstrating an incorrect understanding of how payment patterns interact with reported claim patterns.
- Assuming additional adjusters would affect the number of claims ultimately reported or increase ALAE, without explaining the mechanism.
