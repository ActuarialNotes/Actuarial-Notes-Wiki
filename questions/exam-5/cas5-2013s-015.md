An employer negotiated a workers compensation retrospective policy with an insurer, effective from January 1, 2011 to December 31, 2011. The first adjustment of the retrospective premium occurs six months after the end of the policy period and annually thereafter until the tenth adjustment.

The reported losses during the policy period evaluated as of June 30, 2012 are as follows:

| Claim | Reported Losses |
|---|---|
| #1 | $300,000 |
| #2 | $200,000 |
| #3 | $100,000 |

The provisions for this retrospective rating plan are as follows:

| Item | Value |
|---|---|
| Minimum retrospective premium ratio | 50% |
| Maximum retrospective premium ratio | 150% |
| Loss Conversion Factor | 1.2 |
| Per Accident Loss Limitation | $150,000 |
| Expense Allowance Excluding Tax Multiplier | 25% |
| Expected Loss Ratio | 60% |
| Tax Multiplier | 1.05 |
| Net Insurance Charge | 44.6% |
| Standard Premium | $540,000 |

## Part a (2 points)

Calculate the retrospective premium as of June 30, 2012.

### Answer

$\$810,000$

### Explanation

**Step 1: Calculate the Basic Premium.**

The basic premium formula is:

$$B = (e_b + \phi - \psi) \times T \times SP$$

where the net insurance charge is $\phi - \psi = 44.6\%$ (provided directly as "Net Insurance Charge"), $e_b$ is the expense allowance excluding taxes, $T$ is the tax multiplier, and $SP$ is the standard premium.

Using the net insurance charge as provided:

$$B = (e_b + \text{Net Insurance Charge}) \times T \times SP$$

$$e_b = \text{Expense Allowance Excluding Tax Multiplier} = 25\% = 0.25$$

$$B = (0.25 + 0.446) \times 1.05 \times 540{,}000$$

$$B = 0.696 \times 1.05 \times 540{,}000 = 0.7308 \times 540{,}000 = \$394{,}632$$

**Step 2: Calculate capped losses per accident.**

The per-accident loss limitation is $150,000. Each claim is capped at this limit:

| Claim | Reported | Capped at $150,000 |
|---|---|---|
| #1 | $300,000 | $150,000 |
| #2 | $200,000 | $150,000 |
| #3 | $100,000 | $100,000 |
| **Total** | **$600,000** | **$400,000** |

**Step 3: Calculate the converted losses.**

$$\text{Converted Losses} = \text{Capped Losses} \times \text{LCF} = 400{,}000 \times 1.2 = \$480{,}000$$

**Step 4: Calculate the retrospective premium before min/max.**

$$\text{Retro Premium} = (B + \text{Converted Losses}) \times T$$

$$= (394{,}632 + 480{,}000) \times 1.05$$

$$= 874{,}632 \times 1.05 = \$918{,}363$$

Wait — the basic premium formula already includes the tax multiplier. The correct formula is:

$$\text{Retro Premium} = B + (\text{Converted Losses} \times T)$$

$$= 394{,}632 + (480{,}000 \times 1.05) = 394{,}632 + 504{,}000 = \$898{,}632$$

**Step 5: Apply maximum and minimum premium constraints.**

$$\text{Maximum Retro Premium} = \text{Max ratio} \times SP = 1.50 \times 540{,}000 = \$810{,}000$$

$$\text{Minimum Retro Premium} = \text{Min ratio} \times SP = 0.50 \times 540{,}000 = \$270{,}000$$

Since the calculated retro premium ($898,632) exceeds the maximum ($810,000), the final retrospective premium is capped at the maximum:

$$\boxed{\text{Retrospective Premium} = \$810{,}000}$$

**Formula summary:**

The standard retrospective rating premium formula:

$$RP = (B + cL) \times T$$

where:
- $B$ = basic premium
- $c$ = loss conversion factor (LCF)
- $L$ = capped (limited) losses
- $T$ = tax multiplier

Subject to:
$$\min(SP) \leq RP \leq \max(SP)$$

### Examiner Report

This question was answered poorly with few candidates receiving full credit.

**Part a common errors:**

- Incorrectly calculating the capped losses — many did not apply the $150,000 per-accident limitation, using the full $600,000 reported losses instead.
- When calculating the basic premium, applying factors to adjust the net insurance charge that was already provided in the question (the 44.6% net insurance charge is given directly and should not be further adjusted).
- Using an incorrect basic premium formula — particularly forgetting that the expense allowance already excludes the tax multiplier, and then mishandling where the tax multiplier applies.
- Not applying the maximum or minimum premium cap to the calculated retro premium before stating the final answer.

In order to get full credit, candidates needed to: (1) calculate the basic premium correctly, (2) correctly cap losses per occurrence and convert them, and (3) calculate and apply the maximum/minimum premium.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Discuss what could cause the retrospective premium in part a. above to change for the insured between June 30, 2012 and the tenth adjustment.

### Answer

Since the retrospective premium is currently at the maximum cap ($810,000), it can only **decrease** (not increase further) between the first adjustment and the tenth adjustment. The premium would decrease if:

- Reported losses develop **downward** — for example, if reserves are reduced upon re-evaluation.
- Claims are **closed with no payment** (or with payment less than previously reserved), reducing the total capped losses.

If losses continue to develop upward, the premium remains at the maximum cap and does not change.

### Explanation

The retrospective premium is currently pegged at the maximum ($810,000 = 150% × $540,000) because the formula-calculated premium exceeds this cap. Over subsequent adjustments, reported losses are re-evaluated annually.

**Scenarios that would cause a decrease:**

- A previously open claim (e.g., Claim #1 or #2) could have its reserve reduced if the claimant settles for less than the current case reserve. This reduces capped losses, reducing converted losses, and the formula premium falls below the maximum.
- A claim that was open and reserved could close with no additional payment, reducing the reported total.

**Scenarios that would NOT cause an increase:**

- Further adverse development (increases) in losses would not increase the premium because it is already at the ceiling.

$$\text{Current position: } RP = \$810{,}000 = \text{Maximum} \Rightarrow \text{can only stay flat or decrease}$$

### Examiner Report

Candidates did better on part b than on part a. The most common error was providing reasons that the premium could **increase**, when the question context makes clear that the premium is already at the maximum level — there is nowhere to go but down (or flat).

However, if a candidate incorrectly calculated the retrospective premium in part a and produced a number that fell between the minimum and maximum, the examiners awarded full credit in part b if the candidate stated that premium could rise or fall (since for an uncapped premium, both directions are possible at future evaluations).
