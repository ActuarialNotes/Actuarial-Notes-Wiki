**Underwriting Changes** are shifts in risk selection criteria, eligibility guidelines, or underwriting standards that alter the mix or average quality of risks in the book of business, causing historical loss development patterns to be less predictive of future experience.

- Tightening underwriting (eliminating high-hazard accounts, raising minimum premium thresholds) tends to reduce average loss severity and may accelerate closure, while loosening underwriting does the opposite — both can make historical age-to-age factors misleading
- Underwriting changes are harder to detect and adjust for than [[Policy Provision Changes]] because they do not produce a clean, documented effective date; the actuary must look for shifts in average premium, loss ratios by risk class, or changes in the distribution of insureds across rating tiers
- The Berquist-Sherman method addresses some consequences of underwriting changes (particularly those that alter the settlement rate or case reserve adequacy), but a fundamental mix shift may require segmenting the data or relying more heavily on industry benchmarks

> [!example]- Underwriting Tightening Effect {Example}
> An insurer stops writing contractor accounts with more than three prior losses effective $7/1/2022$. The book previously had $30\%$ of premium in high-hazard contractors; after the change, that segment drops to $5\%$.
>
> > [!answer]-
> > Development triangles that blend pre- and post-change accident years will show declining loss ratios in recent diagonals. An actuary who applies historical all-year average development factors to the post-change years will **overstate** IBNR because the historical factors reflect a riskier book. The appropriate response is to give less weight to older accident years when selecting development factors, or to restate historical years to reflect the new mix.
