Given the following data as of December 31, 2017:

| Accident Year | Cumulative Reported Claims ($000) as of (months) | | | |
|---------------|--------------------------------------------------|-------|-------|-------|
| | 12 | 24 | 36 | 48 |
| 2014 | 500 | 1,100 | 1,800 | 2,500 |
| 2015 | 900 | 1,700 | 2,300 | |
| 2016 | 1,000 | 1,900 | | |
| 2017 | 1,100 | | | |

| Calendar Year | Earned Premium ($000) |
|---------------|-----------------------|
| 2014 | 5,300 |
| 2015 | 7,200 |
| 2016 | 7,800 |
| 2017 | 8,500 |

- 1.3 = 48 to ultimate reported claim development factor
- In 2015 the company started writing a new class of insureds within this line of business.
- Both existing and new classes of insureds are priced accurately.

## Part a (1.25 points)

Estimate ultimate claims for accident year 2017 using the reported Cape Cod technique.

### Answer

Approximately $5,406 thousand (range of acceptable answers based on LDF selection)

### Explanation

**Step 1: Calculate age-to-age development factors**

| Period | 12-24 | 24-36 | 36-48 | 48-Ult |
|--------|-------|-------|-------|--------|
| 2014 | 1,100/500 = 2.200 | 1,800/1,100 = 1.636 | 2,500/1,800 = 1.389 | 1.300 |
| 2015 | 1,700/900 = 1.889 | 2,300/1,700 = 1.353 | — | |
| 2016 | 1,900/1,000 = 1.900 | — | — | |

Selected volume-weighted LDFs (all years):

$$f_{12-24} = \frac{1,100+1,700+1,900}{500+900+1,000} = \frac{4,700}{2,400} = 1.9583$$

$$f_{24-36} = \frac{1,800+2,300}{1,100+1,700} = \frac{4,100}{2,800} = 1.4643$$

$$f_{36-48} = \frac{2,500}{1,800} = 1.3889$$

$$f_{48-\text{ult}} = 1.300$$

**Step 2: Calculate cumulative development factors (CDF to ultimate)**

$$\text{CDF}_{36} = 1.3889 \times 1.300 = 1.8056$$

$$\text{CDF}_{24} = 1.4643 \times 1.8056 = 2.6444$$

$$\text{CDF}_{12} = 1.9583 \times 2.6444 = 5.1782$$

**Step 3: Calculate % reported (= 1 / CDF) for each AY**

| AY | Age (months) | CDF | % Reported |
|----|-------------|-----|------------|
| 2014 | 48 | 1.300 | 76.92% |
| 2015 | 36 | 1.8056 | 55.38% |
| 2016 | 24 | 2.6444 | 37.82% |
| 2017 | 12 | 5.1782 | 19.31% |

**Step 4: Calculate used-up premium**

$$\text{Used-Up Premium} = \text{Earned Premium} \times \text{\\% Reported}$$

| AY | Earned Premium ($000) | % Reported | Used-Up Premium ($000) |
|----|----------------------|------------|------------------------|
| 2014 | 5,300 | 76.92% | 4,076.9 |
| 2015 | 7,200 | 55.38% | 3,987.4 |
| 2016 | 7,800 | 37.82% | 2,949.9 |
| 2017 | 8,500 | 19.31% | 1,641.2 |
| **Total** | | | **12,655.4** |

**Step 5: Calculate expected claims ratio (ECR)**

$$\text{ECR} = \frac{\text{Total Reported Claims}}{\text{Total Used-Up Premium}} = \frac{2,500 + 2,300 + 1,900 + 1,100}{12,655.4} = \frac{7,800}{12,655.4} = 61.63\%$$

**Step 6: Estimate ultimate claims for AY 2017**

$$\text{Ultimate}_{2017} = \text{Reported}_{2017} + \text{ECR} \times \text{EP}_{2017} \times (1 - \text{\\% Reported}_{2017})$$

$$= 1,100 + 0.6163 \times 8,500 \times (1 - 0.1931) = 1,100 + 0.6163 \times 8,500 \times 0.8069$$

$$= 1,100 + 4,225 \approx 5,325$$

Note: The sample answer of approximately $5,406 thousand uses slightly different volume-weighted selections. Full credit was given for a range of reasonable LDF selections (e.g., weighting only the latest diagonals to reflect the new class of business introduced in 2015).

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to select claim development factors from a reported claims triangle and use these development factors to calculate used-up premium. Candidates were expected to calculate the expected claims ratio as the ratio of reported losses to used-up premium (as defined by the Cape Cod technique), and apply this expected claims ratio to get the estimated unreported claims, and subsequently, the ultimate claims.

Common mistakes include:
- Calculating the expected claims ratio as something other than total reported losses over total used-up premium (e.g., straight average or total developed claims over total earned premium)
- Multiplying the expected claims ratio by the earned premium to get the ultimate claims (rather than applying the ECR only to the unreported portion)

## Part b (0.5 points)

Discuss the effect the new class of insureds has on the reported Cape Cod technique for accident year 2017.

### Explanation

The new class of insureds introduced in 2015 appears to have **sped up** the reporting pattern (shorter tail). Since both classes are priced accurately, the new class does not introduce a loss ratio distortion — the expected claims ratio component of the Cape Cod method should not be significantly affected by the mix change.

However, the development factors derived from the historical triangle (which includes 2014 data with the slower reporting pattern) will be **overstated** relative to what is appropriate for the current mix. Higher-than-appropriate LDFs mean a **lower % reported** for AY 2017, which lowers the used-up premium, which in turn **overstates the ECR**. This leads to **overstatement of AY 2017 ultimate claims**.

The effect is less severe than with the pure development technique, because the Cape Cod method's premium component partially offsets the development distortion, but the method still **overstates IBNR** for AY 2017 if no adjustment is made to reflect the accelerated reporting pattern.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to recognize the change in the development factors as a result of the change in the mix of business, and to discuss the impact of this change in development factors on the estimated ultimate claims.

Common mistakes include:
- Stating that the Cape Cod ultimate claims would be impacted without detailing how
- Stating that the new mix of business would have no impact on Cape Cod ultimate claims
- Confusion regarding the loss ratio as the source of the effect to the Cape Cod technique rather than the change in reported development
- Stating that the new mix of business would lower the estimated ultimate claims
