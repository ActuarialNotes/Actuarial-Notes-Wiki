Given the following information:

| Accident Year | Selected Ultimate Claims as of 12/31/2011 | Actual Reported Claims as of 12/31/2011 | Actual Reported Claims as of 12/31/2012 |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2009 | $5,000 | $5,000 | $5,500 |
| 2010 | $5,000 | $3,333 | $5,033 |
| 2011 | $5,000 | $2,500 | $4,000 |

The selected ultimate claims as of December 31, 2011 were determined using the reported development technique.

## Part a (1.25 points)

For accident years 2009 through 2011, compare actual claim emergence to expected reported claim emergence between December 31, 2011 and December 31, 2012.

### Answer

| Accident Year | Expected Emergence | Actual Emergence | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2009 | $0 | $500 | $500 excess actual |
| 2010 | $1,667 | $1,700 | $33 excess actual |
| 2011 | $833 | $1,500 | $667 excess actual |

All three accident years show actual emergence greater than expected, with AY 2009 and AY 2011 showing the most significant excess.

### Explanation

Because the selected ultimates were determined using the reported development technique, the **implied age-to-age factors** are:

From the ratio of selected ultimate to actual reported as of 12/31/2011:

| AY | Ultimate | Reported @12/31/2011 | Implied CDF (ult/reported) | Age at 12/31/2011 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2009 | $5,000 | $5,000 | 1.000 | 36 months (or later — fully developed) |
| 2010 | $5,000 | $3,333 | 1.500 | 24 months |
| 2011 | $5,000 | $2,500 | 2.000 | 12 months |

Implied age-to-age factors from these CDFs:
- $f_{12\text{-}24} = \text{CDF}_{12} / \text{CDF}_{24} = 2.000 / 1.500 = 1.333$
- $f_{24\text{-}36} = \text{CDF}_{24} / \text{CDF}_{36} = 1.500 / 1.000 = 1.500$
- $f_{36\text{-ult}} = 1.000$ (AY 2009 was assumed fully developed)

**Expected emergence** between 12/31/2011 and 12/31/2012 (one additional development period):

- **AY 2009** (already at ultimate CDF = 1.0): Expected additional emergence = $0.
  $$\text{Expected@}12/31/2012 = \$5{,}000 \times 1.000 = \$5{,}000 \Rightarrow \text{Emergence} = \$0$$

- **AY 2010** (CDF = 1.5 at 24 months, moving to 36 months → CDF = 1.0):
  $$\text{Expected@}12/31/2012 = \$5{,}000 \times \frac{1.000}{1.500} \times 1.500 - \$3{,}333$$
  More directly: Expected reported at 36 months = Ultimate / CDF$_{36}$ = $5,000 / 1.000 = $5,000.
  But expected emergence = Ultimate × (1/CDF$_{36}$ − 1/CDF$_{24}$) = $5,000 × (1.000 − 0.667) = **$1,667**

- **AY 2011** (CDF = 2.0 at 12 months, moving to 24 months → CDF = 1.5):
  $$\text{Expected emergence} = \text{Ultimate} \times \left(\frac{1}{\text{CDF}_{24}} - \frac{1}{\text{CDF}_{12}}\right) = 5{,}000 \times \left(\frac{1}{1.5} - \frac{1}{2.0}\right) = 5{,}000 \times (0.6\overline{6} - 0.500) = \mathbf{\$833}$$

**Actual emergence** between 12/31/2011 and 12/31/2012:

- AY 2009: $5,500 − $5,000 = **$500**
- AY 2010: $5,033 − $3,333 = **$1,700**
- AY 2011: $4,000 − $2,500 = **$1,500**

**Comparison:**

| AY | Expected Emergence | Actual Emergence | Excess Actual |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2009 | $0 | $500 | +$500 |
| 2010 | $1,667 | $1,700 | +$33 |
| 2011 | $833 | $1,500 | +$667 |

All AYs show more actual emergence than expected, suggesting the selected development factors as of 12/31/2011 were understated.

### Examiner Report

Many candidates received full credit. Credit was also given for comparisons based on age-to-age factors (actual vs. expected). Common mistakes included:

- Miscalculation of the AY 2010 actual emergence.
- Not explicitly offering a comparison of actual to expected (just calculating one or the other).
- Calculating AY 2011 expected emergence incorrectly (e.g., using the wrong CDF interval).

## Part b (0.25 points)

Briefly discuss what change, if any, the actuary should make to the reported claim development factors based on the actual claim emergence as of December 31, 2012.

### Answer

The actuary should **increase the reported development factors**, particularly the age 12-to-24 factor and the 36-to-ultimate tail factor.

### Explanation

All three accident years showed actual emergence exceeding expected emergence during 2012:

- **AY 2009** was assumed fully developed (tail factor = 1.0), yet it showed $500 of actual emergence — this suggests the **tail factor should be greater than 1.0** to account for late-developing losses.
- **AY 2010** showed actual emergence ($1,700) modestly in excess of expected ($1,667) — the 24-to-36 factor is approximately correct but may need a modest upward revision.
- **AY 2011** showed actual emergence ($1,500) nearly double the expected ($833) — this suggests the **12-to-24 age-to-age factor should be increased** significantly.

Given the systematic pattern of underestimated emergence across all years, the actuary should revise the development factors upward. It would also be appropriate to consider whether a Berquist-Sherman adjustment is warranted to investigate whether the underestimation is due to a change in claims settlement patterns.

### Examiner Report

Slightly more than half of the candidates received full credit on this part. Full credit responses ranged from a simple statement of "increase factors" to more specific guidance such as "increase the 12-to-24 and 36-to-ultimate factors." Other acceptable responses included the suggestion to perform a Berquist-Sherman adjustment if warranted by the data and to use the adjusted data to recalculate factors. There was no partial credit for this part.

## Part c (1.5 points)

For each accident year, justify what changes, if any, the actuary should make to the ultimate claim selections based on the actual claim emergence as of December 31, 2012.

### Answer

- **AY 2009:** Increase the selected ultimate. The actual reported claims of $5,500 already exceed the prior ultimate selection of $5,000; the minimum selection should be raised to $5,500. Given the pattern of late-developing losses, the actuary may also select a higher ultimate to reflect continued possible development.
- **AY 2010:** Modestly increase the selected ultimate. Actual emergence was close to expected, but the claims have now reached $5,033 with possible further development (the tail factor was underestimated). Apply revised development factors to arrive at a new ultimate above $5,000.
- **AY 2011:** Increase the selected ultimate meaningfully. Actual emergence of $1,500 significantly exceeded the expected $833. Applying revised development factors to the $4,000 reported as of 12/31/2012 will produce an ultimate well above the prior $5,000 selection.

### Explanation

**AY 2009:**

The reported amount at 12/31/2012 is $5,500, which already exceeds the prior selected ultimate of $5,000. The actuary must increase the selection to at least $5,500. If the tail factor is revised upward from 1.0, the new ultimate = $5,500 × revised tail factor > $5,500.

$$\text{New ultimate}_{2009} \geq \$5{,}500$$

**AY 2010:**

Reported at 12/31/2012 = $5,033, and AY 2010 is now at 36 months of development. If the prior tail factor was 1.0 (no further development beyond 36 months), the selection based purely on current reported is $5,033. However, given that AY 2009 showed development beyond what was previously assumed, the actuary should apply a revised tail factor:

$$\text{New ultimate}_{2010} = 5{,}033 \times (\text{revised tail factor}) > \$5{,}033$$

This represents an increase from the prior selection of $5,000.

**AY 2011:**

Reported at 12/31/2012 = $4,000 (at 24 months). The revised 24-to-ultimate CDF should be based on the updated development factors. Using the revised 24-to-36 factor (approximately 1.5) and the new tail factor, the updated ultimate will be substantially above $5,000:

$$\text{New ultimate}_{2011} = 4{,}000 \times (\text{revised CDF}_{24}) \gg \$5{,}000$$

For example, if the 24-to-36 factor remains 1.5 and a tail factor of 1.1 is applied: $4,000 × 1.5 × 1.1 = $6,600.

In summary, all three AYs warrant upward revisions to ultimate claim selections, with the magnitude of increase being largest for AY 2011 (where the development pattern was most significantly underestimated).

### Examiner Report

Candidates who did not receive full credit were those who either specified a change in the ultimate without justifying it, or who justified how to make a change without specifying the direction. Typical full credit responses included adjusting ultimates based on the new reporting pattern and applying revised development factors to the latest diagonal. Credit was given for selecting revised reporting patterns and for descriptive responses with no specific values specified. Some candidates mistakenly used the prior reported amounts (at 12/31/2011) rather than the new reported amounts (at 12/31/2012) when developing new ultimates.
