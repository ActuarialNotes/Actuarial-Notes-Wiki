An actuary develops an overall indicated rate increase of 4.5% using the following assumptions:

- All expenses are variable.
- Total permissible loss ratio = 65%.
- Profit and contingency provision = 5%.

The actuary's manager asks that the expenses be split into fixed and variable components as follows:

- Fixed = 75% of total expenses.
- Variable = 25% of total expenses.

## Part a (1.25 points)

Calculate the revised overall rate indication with the new expense split suggested by the actuary's manager.

### Answer

+3.34% (increase)

### Explanation

**Deriving expense components from the original assumptions**

Under the original (all-variable) assumption:

$$\text{Permissible Loss Ratio} = 1 - V_{\text{total}} - Q = 65\%$$

where $Q = 5\%$ (profit provision), so:

$$V_{\text{total}} = 1 - 0.65 - 0.05 = 30\%$$

All expenses are variable: $V_{\text{total}} = 30\%$.

Under the revised split:
- Variable expense ratio: $V = 25\% \times 30\% = 7.5\%$
- Fixed expense ratio: $F = 75\% \times 30\% = 22.5\%$ of premium

**Revised permissible loss ratio**

When fixed expenses are separated and placed in the numerator of the indication formula, the permissible loss ratio is revised as:

$$\text{Revised Permissible LR} = 1 - V - Q = 1 - 0.075 - 0.05 = 87.5\%$$

**Deriving the experience loss ratio**

The original indication of +4.5% came from:

$$\text{Indication} = \frac{\text{Experience LR}}{\text{Permissible LR}} - 1 = 4.5\%$$

$$\text{Experience LR} = 1.045 \times 0.65 = 67.925\%$$

**Revised indication formula**

With fixed expenses separated, the indication becomes:

$$\text{Indication} = \frac{\text{Experience LR} + F}{\text{Revised Permissible LR}} - 1 = \frac{0.67925 + 0.225}{0.875} - 1$$

$$= \frac{0.90425}{0.875} - 1 = 1.0334 - 1 = \mathbf{3.34\%}$$

### Examiner Report

Many candidates received full credit for this question. When there was an error, candidates either used the permissible loss ratio as the experience loss ratio or flipped the variable and fixed expense percentages.

## Part b (0.25 points)

Briefly explain why splitting the expenses as described above results in a different indication.

### Answer

Splitting expenses into fixed and variable components changes where expenses appear in the indication formula. Fixed expenses are added to the loss ratio in the numerator rather than being subtracted from 1 in the denominator (permissible loss ratio), raising the permissible loss ratio and reducing the indicated rate change.

### Explanation

In the all-variable framework, every expense dollar scales with premium — a 4.5% rate increase raises expense dollars by 4.5%. When some expenses are reclassified as fixed, they do not scale with premium. Fixed expenses are therefore placed above the line (added to the loss ratio), while only variable expenses reduce the permissible loss ratio. This increases the permissible loss ratio and lowers the indicated change because the denominator is larger relative to the numerator.

### Examiner Report

Many candidates had trouble with this question. The answer was a verbalization of part a. Many candidates didn't realize this and tried to define fixed and variable expenses rather than stating how reflecting fixed expenses impacted the indication.

## Part c (0.5 points)

Identify two reasons an actuary may want to split expenses into fixed and variable components.

### Answer

1. Treating all expenses as variable overcharges high-premium risks and undercharges low-premium risks; separating fixed expenses improves equity across risk sizes.
2. Fixed and variable expenses may behave differently over time (e.g., fixed expenses may be subject to trend independently), so separating them allows more accurate prospective loading.

### Explanation

**Reason 1 — Equity across premium sizes:** If all expenses are assumed variable, a risk with twice the premium is implicitly assumed to cost twice as much to administer. In practice, many expenses (policy issuance, inspection fees, etc.) are the same regardless of premium size. Separating these fixed costs ensures small-premium risks bear their fair share.

**Reason 2 — Separate trending and monitoring:** Fixed expenses trend differently than variable expenses. By separating them, the actuary can apply appropriate trend factors to each component and better monitor expense ratios for management purposes.

### Examiner Report

The most common mistake on this part was providing the same or very similar responses twice, or only defining fixed and variable expenses without explaining why the split is useful.
