Given the following information:

**Unadjusted Case Outstanding Claims ($000s)**

| Accident Year | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $10,300 | $21,300 | $37,500 |
| 2011 | $11,400 | $29,400 | |
| 2012 | $15,600 | | |

**Open Claim Counts**

| Accident Year | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | 1,030 | 1,420 | 1,500 |
| 2011 | 1,140 | 1,470 | |
| 2012 | 1,200 | | |

**Unadjusted Cumulative Paid Claims ($000s)**

| Accident Year | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $2,575 | $15,975 | $30,000 |
| 2011 | $2,850 | $18,200 | |
| 2012 | $3,900 | | |

Selected annual severity trend = +5%

## Part a (1.5 points)

Calculate the adjusted cumulative reported claim triangle using the Berquist-Sherman case outstanding adjustment technique.

### Answer

**Step 1: Calculate unadjusted average case outstanding (Case OS / Open Counts)**

| AY | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $10,300 / 1,030 = **$10.000** | $21,300 / 1,420 = **$15.000** | $37,500 / 1,500 = **$25.000** |
| 2011 | $11,400 / 1,140 = **$10.000** | $29,400 / 1,470 = **$20.000** | |
| 2012 | $15,600 / 1,200 = **$13.000** | | |

**Step 2: Select the latest diagonal average case severities as the base and trend back**

The most recent observed diagonal is used as the anchor. Reading the latest diagonal (the most recent evaluation for each age):
- 36 months: AY 2010 diagonal = $25.000
- 24 months: AY 2011 diagonal = $20.000
- 12 months: AY 2012 diagonal = $13.000

Trend back using 1/1.05 per year to fill in earlier cells:

**Adjusted Average Case Outstanding ($000s)**

| AY | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $13 / 1.05² = **$11.791** | $20 / 1.05¹ = **$19.048** | **$25.000** |
| 2011 | $13 / 1.05¹ = **$12.381** | **$20.000** | |
| 2012 | **$13.000** | | |

**Step 3: Multiply adjusted average case by open claim counts to get adjusted case outstanding**

| AY | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $11.791 × 1,030 = **$12,144.7** | $19.048 × 1,420 = **$27,047.6** | $25.000 × 1,500 = **$37,500** |
| 2011 | $12.381 × 1,140 = **$14,114.3** | $20.000 × 1,470 = **$29,400** | |
| 2012 | $13.000 × 1,200 = **$15,600** | | |

**Step 4: Add adjusted case outstanding to cumulative paid claims to get adjusted cumulative reported claims**

$$\text{Adjusted Reported} = \text{Cumulative Paid} + \text{Adjusted Case Outstanding}$$

| AY | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $2,575 + $12,144.7 = **$14,719.7** | $15,975 + $27,047.6 = **$43,022.6** | $30,000 + $37,500 = **$67,500** |
| 2011 | $2,850 + $14,114.3 = **$16,964.3** | $18,200 + $29,400 = **$47,600** | |
| 2012 | $3,900 + $15,600 = **$19,500** | | |

### Explanation

The Berquist-Sherman case outstanding adjustment restates historical case reserves to be consistent with the current reserving philosophy (here, proxied by the current average severity trend of +5% per year). The procedure is:

1. Compute the unadjusted average case outstanding = Case OS / Open Counts for each cell.
2. Anchor to the most recent diagonal (latest available average case per age) and trend earlier accident years back by dividing by the trend factor for each year of lag.
3. Multiply the adjusted average case amounts by the actual open counts to obtain adjusted case OS.
4. Add adjusted case OS to cumulative paid claims to produce the adjusted cumulative reported triangle.

The diagonal at 36 months (AY 2010 = $25k) is left as-is; the 24-month diagonal (AY 2011 = $20k) is the current base for that age, and AY 2010 at 24 months is trended back one year ($20k / 1.05 = $19.048k). Similarly, the 12-month base is AY 2012 = $13k, and earlier years are divided by 1.05 and 1.05² respectively.

### Examiner Report

A majority of candidates received full credit on this part. When there were errors, the most common was a calculation error for Accident Year 2010 at 24 months despite correct answers elsewhere in the final triangle.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Discuss whether IBNR estimated using the Berquist-Sherman case outstanding adjustment technique should be higher or lower than IBNR estimated using an unadjusted reported claim development technique.

### Answer

The B-S adjusted IBNR should be **lower** than the unadjusted IBNR.

### Explanation

Comparing adjusted average case outstanding to unadjusted average case outstanding:

| AY | Age | Unadjusted Avg Case | Adjusted Avg Case |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | 12 | $10.000 | $11.791 |
| 2010 | 24 | $15.000 | $19.048 |
| 2011 | 12 | $10.000 | $12.381 |

The adjusted average case outstanding is **higher** than the unadjusted average case outstanding for all historical cells (i.e., prior accident years at earlier maturities). This means the adjusted reported claim triangle has higher values in the interior cells compared to the unadjusted triangle.

Higher reported amounts in the historical data produce **lower** age-to-age loss development factors (LDFs), because the denominator increases relative to the numerator on the latest diagonal. With lower LDFs, the projection to ultimate is smaller, resulting in **lower IBNR** under the B-S adjustment versus the unadjusted development method.

Intuitively: the unadjusted data makes older accident years look under-reserved (since case adequacy has grown over time), causing the development method to project artificially high future development. The B-S adjustment removes this distortion by restating historical reserves to current adequacy levels, resulting in a more accurate (and in this scenario, lower) IBNR estimate.

### Examiner Report

Many candidates provided answers that were factually correct but did not fully explain the issue at hand and the mechanics of the adjustment. A complete answer should note (a) the adjusted average case amounts are higher than unadjusted in historical periods, (b) this raises the historical reported amounts, (c) which lowers the selected LDFs, and (d) therefore the projected ultimate and IBNR are lower under the B-S method.

An alternative correct observation: whether B-S IBNR is higher or lower than unadjusted IBNR depends on how the actual trend in average case reserves compares to the selected severity trend. Here, the actual trend in average case (from $10k to $13k over two years = ~14% total) is less than what the 5% annual trend would imply ($10k × 1.05² = $11.025k at 12 months, but the selected base is $13k), so the adjusted case amounts exceed the unadjusted amounts, leading to lower B-S IBNR.
