Given the following information:

**Cumulative Closed Claim Counts**

| Accident Half-Year | 6 Months | 12 Months | 18 Months | 24 Months | 30 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2010-1 | 4,898 | 7,349 | 7,571 | 7,647 | 7,647 | 7,647 |
| 2010-2 | 5,576 | 6,786 | 7,487 | 7,569 | 7,569 | |
| 2011-1 | 6,580 | 10,215 | 10,618 | 10,724 | | |
| 2011-2 | 7,514 | 9,564 | 10,953 | | | |
| 2012-1 | 8,894 | 13,807 | | | | |
| 2012-2 | 10,265 | | | | | |

**Age-to-Age Factors**

| Accident Half-Year | 6-12 | 12-18 | 18-24 | 24-30 | 30-36 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2010-1 | 1.500 | 1.030 | 1.010 | 1.000 | 1.000 |
| 2010-2 | 1.217 | 1.103 | 1.011 | 1.000 | |
| 2011-1 | 1.552 | 1.039 | 1.010 | | |
| 2011-2 | 1.273 | 1.145 | | | |
| 2012-1 | 1.552 | | | | |

Assume no closed claim count development after 36 months.

## Part a (1.25 points)

Estimate the ultimate claim count for accident year 2012.

### Answer

Approximately **28,956** (the sum of ultimate estimates for 2012-1 and 2012-2 half-years).

### Explanation

**Step 1: Recognize the seasonal pattern in the age-to-age factors.**

Examining the 6-12 month column:
- The "-1" half-years (first half of calendar year) show factors of 1.500, 1.552, 1.552 — consistently higher.
- The "-2" half-years (second half of calendar year) show factors of 1.217, 1.273 — consistently lower.

This seasonality arises because first-half accident years have claims reported relatively slowly in the first 6 months and then rapidly in the following 6 months. Second-half accident years have a different pattern. We should select **separate factors by half-year suffix**.

**Step 2: Select development factors by half-year type.**

Using simple averages of available data for each half-year group:

*Selected factors for "-1" half-years (2010-1, 2011-1, 2012-1):*

| Age | Available Factors | Selected (-1) |
|---|---|---|
| 6-12 | 1.500, 1.552, 1.552 | **1.535** (avg of 3) |
| 12-18 | 1.030, 1.039 | **1.035** (avg of 2) |
| 18-24 | 1.010, 1.010 | **1.010** (avg of 2) |
| 24-30 | 1.000 | **1.000** |
| 30-36 | 1.000 | **1.000** |

*Selected factors for "-2" half-years (2010-2, 2011-2):*

| Age | Available Factors | Selected (-2) |
|---|---|---|
| 6-12 | 1.217, 1.273 | **1.245** (avg of 2) |
| 12-18 | 1.103, 1.145 | **1.124** (avg of 2) |
| 18-24 | 1.011, 1.000 | **1.011** (avg) |
| 24-30 | 1.000 | **1.000** |
| 30-36 | 1.000 | **1.000** |

**Step 3: Develop 2012-1 to ultimate.**

2012-1 is at 12 months with 13,807 closed claims. Apply the selected "-1" factors from 12-18 onward:

$$\text{CDF}_{12 \to \text{ult}} = 1.035 \times 1.010 \times 1.000 \times 1.000 = 1.045$$

$$\text{Ultimate}_{2012\text{-}1} = 13{,}807 \times 1.045 \approx 14{,}428$$

**Step 4: Develop 2012-2 to ultimate.**

2012-2 is at 6 months with 10,265 closed claims. Apply the selected "-2" factors from 6-12 onward:

$$\text{CDF}_{6 \to \text{ult}} = 1.245 \times 1.124 \times 1.011 \times 1.000 \times 1.000 = 1.415$$

$$\text{Ultimate}_{2012\text{-}2} = 10{,}265 \times 1.415 \approx 14{,}525$$

**Step 5: Combine to get accident year 2012 ultimate.**

$$\text{Ultimate AY 2012} = 14{,}428 + 14{,}525 \approx 28{,}953$$

The examiner's published calculation uses slightly different rounding:

$$\text{Ultimate AY 2012} = 13{,}807 \times (1.035)(1.010) + 10{,}265 \times (1.245)(1.124)(1.011) \approx 14{,}432 + 14{,}524 \approx 28{,}956$$

$$\boxed{\text{Ultimate AY 2012} \approx 28{,}956 \text{ closed claims}}$$

### Examiner Report

Most candidates were able to properly apply development factors. Common mistakes:

- **Failing to reflect seasonality** — selecting a single set of factors averaging across both half-year types, which ignores the clear seasonal pattern in the data. The -1 and -2 half-years have materially different 6-12 month factors (approximately 1.535 vs. 1.245).
- **Developing the 6-month closed claims for the first half of the year instead of the 12-month closed claims** — candidates sometimes developed 2012-1 from the 6-month count rather than the available 12-month count of 13,807.
- **Applying first-half factors to second-half closed claims and vice versa** — mixing up the seasonal selections.
- **Only calculating the ultimate claims for one half of accident year 2012** — the question asks for accident year 2012 as a whole, requiring both 2012-1 and 2012-2.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Briefly discuss two advantages for analyzing this data using accident half-years as opposed to full accident years.

### Answer

1. **Captures seasonal patterns in claims development:** The data shows that first-half accident years develop very differently from second-half accident years (e.g., 6-12 month factor of ~1.535 for -1 periods vs. ~1.245 for -2 periods). Using full accident years would average these together, obscuring the underlying dynamics and leading to less accurate development projections.

2. **Better handles growing exposure and shifting average accident dates:** When claim counts (and presumably exposures) are growing rapidly, the average accident date within a full year shifts earlier or later depending on the growth pattern. Splitting into half-years produces a finer time grain, so the average accident date within each half-year period is better defined, improving the comparability of development patterns across periods.

### Explanation

**Advantage 1 — Seasonality:** The age-to-age factors display a clear seasonal pattern. Half-year granularity allows the actuary to apply separate development patterns to -1 and -2 periods, producing more accurate ultimates than a blended annual factor would.

**Advantage 2 — Exposure growth and average accident date:** The 6-month claim counts are increasing substantially (from 4,898 for 2010-1 to 10,265 for 2012-2 — roughly doubling over two years). When exposures grow rapidly within a year, the average accident date within that year shifts, and annual triangles mix periods with different claim maturity levels. Half-year segmentation reduces this problem because the average accident date within a 6-month period is more stable and better approximated by the midpoint.

### Examiner Report

Most candidates were able to recognize the seasonality advantage. A significant number also recognized the exposure growth / shifting average accident date advantage.

A common mistake was to misinterpret the question as referring to development **age** intervals (6, 12, 18 months, etc., versus 12, 24, 36 months). This led to responses along the lines of "using half-years makes the loss development factors less leveraged" — which is not what the question was asking about. The question is about splitting accident years into half-years, not about using 6-month development ages.
