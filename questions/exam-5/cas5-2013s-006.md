## Part a (0.5 points)

Contrast the components of IBNR for a claims-made policy and an occurrence policy.

### Explanation

IBNR has two main components: **pure IBNR** (claims that have occurred but have not yet been reported) and **IBNER** (incurred but not enough reported — development on known claims).

- **Occurrence policy**: IBNR includes both pure IBNR and IBNER. Because claims may be reported long after the accident date, the occurrence policy accumulates unreported claims over time, in addition to development on claims already known.
- **Claims-made policy**: At the end of a report year, all claims that occurred during the policy period and have been reported are known by definition. Therefore, a mature claims-made policy has **no pure IBNR** — its remaining development is limited to IBNER alone.

The key distinction is that occurrence policies have pure IBNR (due to the reporting lag between occurrence and report), while claims-made policies do not.

### Examiner Report

More than half of candidates provided enough components for both policy types to receive full credit. Many candidates named only the pure IBNR component without stating that it was the key difference. No credit was granted to candidates who stated that claims-made policies have no IBNR, because claims-made policies do have IBNER, which is a component of IBNR. The most common mistake was not including both pure IBNR and IBNER in the contrast, or simply stating "Claims-Made has no IBNR."

## Part b (0.5 points)

Explain why a claims-made policy should cost less than an occurrence policy, provided claim costs are increasing.

### Explanation

A claims-made policy provides coverage for claims **reported** during the policy period, regardless of when the loss occurred. This means that claims-made losses are reported — and therefore priced — at approximately today's cost levels, with a relatively short gap between the loss event and claim reporting.

An occurrence policy, by contrast, must cover claims that **occur** during the policy period but may be reported (and ultimately paid) many years in the future. With rising claim costs, these future settlements will be at higher cost levels. The insurer must price for these higher future costs and faces additional pricing risk from the long reporting lag.

Because the claims-made policy's liability is established at today's (lower) cost levels while the occurrence policy's liability extends into the future at higher cost levels, the claims-made policy costs less when claim costs are increasing.

### Examiner Report

About half of candidates received full credit for referencing occurrence policies having claims reported further into the future at a higher cost level, or the additional pricing risk from a longer projection period. Several candidates incorrectly stated that claims-made policies "have only one year of trend" or are "fully settled at year-end." This received no credit, because it is the report lag — not the settlement lag — that is shorter for claims-made. Inflation acts on both policy types for as long as the settlement lag lasts.

## Part c (0.5 points)

Explain why a change in underlying trends will impact the estimated premium for an occurrence policy more than for a claims-made policy.

### Explanation

The trend period for an occurrence policy extends from the average date of loss (the policy period) to the average date of settlement, which may be many years in the future. A change in the trend rate compounds over this long horizon, so the total impact on estimated losses — and thus the required premium — is substantial.

For a claims-made policy, the trend period only spans from the average date of loss to the date of claim **reporting** (which is within the report year). Settlement may still take years, but the base cost level is anchored to the report date rather than a distant future date. Therefore, a change in trend accumulates over a shorter period for claims-made policies.

Because a trend change leverages over a longer time horizon for occurrence policies, any shift in underlying trends has a greater impact on their estimated premium than on claims-made premium.

### Examiner Report

About half of candidates received full credit by referencing occurrence policies having claims reported further in the future. Common errors were identical to those in Part b: stating that claims-made policies have only one year of trend, which received no credit. Candidates were encouraged to keep responses concise — a one or two sentence explanation was sufficient for full credit.

## Part d (0.5 points)

Briefly describe the provision that exists to eliminate coverage overlap if an insured switches from an occurrence policy to a claims-made policy, and why an overlap would exist without it.

### Explanation

**Provision — Retroactive Date:** A retroactive date is included on the claims-made policy. It restricts coverage to claims arising from losses that **occurred on or after** the retroactive date. Without a retroactive date, the new claims-made policy would cover any claim reported during the report year, regardless of when the loss occurred — including losses that occurred before the policy inception and are now being reported.

**Why overlap exists without it:** Suppose an insured had an occurrence policy in 2011 and switches to a claims-made policy in 2012. The occurrence policy (2011) already covers all losses that occurred in 2011, whenever they are eventually reported. The new claims-made policy (2012), without a retroactive date, would also cover a loss that occurred in 2011 and is first reported in 2012, creating a double-coverage overlap.

Setting the retroactive date to the inception date of the first claims-made policy (e.g., January 1, 2012) eliminates this overlap by restricting the claims-made policy to losses occurring on or after that date.

**Diagram:**

```
Occurrence policy (2011) — covers the diagonal: losses occurring in 2011, reported at lag 0, 1, 2, ...
Claims-made policy (2012, no retro date) — covers entire row: all losses reported in 2012
Overlap: Loss occurring in 2011 (lag 1) reported in 2012
```

| Lag → | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Occ 2011 | L(11,0) | L(11,1) | L(11,2) |
| CM 2012 (no retro) | L(12,0) | **L(11,1)** ← overlap | |

### Examiner Report

More than half of candidates received credit for naming the retroactive date (or first-year/second-year claims-made provision) and for describing the overlap. Common errors: describing the retroactive date as the date after which claims must be reported (which is simply the policy inception date); not mentioning both the reporting and occurrence conditions needed for an overlap; labeling one diagram axis as "AY" with the occurrence policy on the diagonal, which is incorrect. Some candidates described overlap as occurring when both policies were simultaneously in effect (rather than in a subsequent year), which received no credit.

## Part e (0.5 points)

Explain why there would be a coverage gap if an insured switches from a claims-made policy to an occurrence policy, and what an insurer can do to provide coverage.

### Explanation

**Why a gap exists:** Suppose an insured had a claims-made policy in 2011 and switches to an occurrence policy in 2012. The claims-made policy (2011) covers losses that occurred in any prior year and were **reported** in 2011. The new occurrence policy (2012) covers losses that **occur** in 2012, reported at any future time.

Losses that occurred in 2011 (or earlier) but are **not reported until 2012 or later** are covered by neither policy: they occurred before the new occurrence policy period, and they were not reported during the old claims-made policy year. This creates a **coverage gap**.

For example, a loss occurring in 2011 but first reported in 2012 is not covered by the 2011 claims-made policy (not reported in 2011) and not covered by the 2012 occurrence policy (did not occur in 2012).

**Solution — Tail coverage (Extended Reporting Period Endorsement):** The insurer can attach an Extended Reporting Period Endorsement (tail policy) to the expiring claims-made policy. This endorsement extends the reporting window of the claims-made policy beyond its expiration, covering claims that occurred while the claims-made policy was in effect but are reported after its expiration, thereby filling the gap.

**Diagram:**

| Lag → | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| CM 2011 | L(11,0) | — | — |
| Occ 2012 | L(12,0) | L(11,1) not covered | |

L(11,1) — occurs in 2011, reported in 2012 — falls in the gap. Tail coverage on the 2011 claims-made policy fills this gap.

### Examiner Report

Most candidates received at least partial credit for naming the tail policy or extended reporting period endorsement. About half received credit for describing the coverage gap. Common errors: not mentioning both the reporting and occurrence conditions for the gap; labeling the diagram axis as AY with the occurrence policy on the diagonal (incorrect); describing the gap as occurring when both policies were in effect simultaneously rather than in a subsequent year. As with Part d, candidates who were too elaborate often introduced errors that cost them partial credit.
