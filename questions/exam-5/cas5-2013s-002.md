Given the following information for an insurance company:

- Proposed effective date of the next rate change is January 1, 2014.
- Rates will be in effect for 1 year.
- All policies have 12-month terms and are written uniformly throughout the year.
- Calendar year 2012 earned premium at current rate level is $114,208,050.

| 12-Month Period Ending | Written Premium at Current Rate Level | Written Exposures |
|---|---|---|
| December 31, 2011 | $104,500,000 | 110,000 |
| June 30, 2012 | $113,800,500 | 121,000 |
| December 31, 2012 | $123,916,100 | 133,100 |

## Part a (1 point)

Utilizing one-step trending, calculate the calendar year 2012 projected earned premium at current rate level for use in calculating the rate change.

### Answer
≈ $108,611,000

### Explanation
**Step 1 — Select the premium trend.**

Compute the average written premium per exposure for each rolling 12-month period:

| Period Ending | Avg Premium/Exposure |
|---|---|
| Dec 31, 2011 | $104,500,000 / 110,000 = **$950.00** |
| Jun 30, 2012 | $113,800,500 / 121,000 = **$940.50** |
| Dec 31, 2012 | $123,916,100 / 133,100 = **$931.00** |

Each period is 6 months apart. The semiannual change is consistent at $-1\%$ per period ($940.50/950.00 = 0.990$ and $931.00/940.50 \approx 0.990$). Selected annual trend factor: $(0.990)^2 = 0.9801$ (approximately $-2\%$ per year).

**Step 2 — Determine the trend period.**

For one-step trending, trend from the average written date of the historical earned premium to the average written date of the future policy period:

- Average written date of CY 2012 earned premium (annual policies, uniform writes): **January 1, 2012**
- Average written date of future rate period (Jan 1–Dec 31, 2014, uniform writes): **July 1, 2014**

$$\text{Trend period} = \text{Jul 1, 2014} - \text{Jan 1, 2012} = 2.5 \text{ years}$$

**Step 3 — Project the earned premium.**

$$\text{Projected EP} = \$114{,}208{,}050 \times (0.9801)^{2.5} = \$114{,}208{,}050 \times 0.9510 \approx \$108{,}611{,}000$$

Equivalently, using the semiannual trend over 5 half-year periods:

$$\$114{,}208{,}050 \times (0.990)^5 \approx \$108{,}611{,}000$$

### Examiner Report
Most candidates scored well. Common errors included using a semiannual ($-1\%$) rather than an annual trend factor; using the wrong trend period (a frequent error was computing 8.5% or 8.9% trends from total written premium rather than average premium per exposure); applying the trend to written rather than earned premium; or computing EP from the given written premium data instead of projecting the already-given CY 2012 EP at current rate level.

## Part b (0.25 points)

Briefly discuss why a premium trend should be utilized in a rate level indication.

### Explanation
A premium trend is used because the average premium per exposure changes over time due to shifts in the mix of business (e.g., changes in the distribution of coverages, deductibles, or insured characteristics). Without applying a trend, the historical premium would not reflect what the average premium per exposure is expected to be during the future rating period when the new rates are in effect, leading to a distorted loss ratio and an incorrect rate indication.

### Examiner Report
A common error was stating that the premium trend is used to "bring historical premium to the expected future cost level," which describes what the trend does but not why it is necessary. Another mistake was incorrectly characterizing premium trend as capturing the effect of rate changes.

## Part c (0.25 points)

Briefly discuss why it is inappropriate to use written premium at historical rate levels to determine premium trends.

### Explanation
Written premium at historical rate levels changes when rates change, so using it to measure premium trend would incorporate the mechanical effect of rate changes into the selected trend factor. This double-counts the rate change — once explicitly in the on-leveling step and again implicitly in the trend — and produces an inaccurate estimate of the underlying change in average exposure characteristics.

### Examiner Report
A common error was comparing average premium to total premium, rather than comparing premium at historical rate levels to premium at current rate levels. Another mistake was comparing written premium to earned premium.

## Part d (0.5 points)

The insurance company decides to move all existing business with a $100 deductible to a $500 deductible upon renewal during calendar year 2013. Given this new information, discuss whether the true projected earned premium will be higher, lower, or unchanged from that in Part a.

### Explanation
The true projected earned premium will be **lower** than calculated in Part a.

Moving policyholders from a $100 deductible to a $500 deductible transfers more of the loss burden to the insured, reducing the insurer's expected losses. As a result, the insurer should charge a lower premium. The projected premium from Part a does not reflect this shift to higher deductibles, so the true projected premium is lower than the Part a estimate.

### Examiner Report
Most candidates scored well. When points were lost, it was usually because the candidate described how the insurer could address the issue rather than directly answering whether the projected premium would be higher, lower, or unchanged.
