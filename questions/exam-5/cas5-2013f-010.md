An insurance company develops territorial indications using a univariate pure premium analysis and has the following experience:

| Territory | Earned Exposures | Reported Loss & ALAE ($000s) | Current Relativity |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | 100,000 | $60,000 | 1.00 |
| B | 250,000 | $300,000 | 1.40 |
| Total | 350,000 | $360,000 | |

| Amount of Insurance Group | Charged Factor | Territory A Exposures | Territory B Exposures |
|---|---|---|---|
| Low | 0.75 | 50,000 | 25,000 |
| Medium | 1.00 | 30,000 | 75,000 |
| High | 1.50 | 20,000 | 150,000 |
| Total | | 100,000 | 250,000 |

## Part a (0.5 points)

Describe how distortion can occur using a univariate approach.

### Answer

In a univariate approach, the distribution of exposures across other rating variables (here, amount-of-insurance groups) is assumed to be the same in every territory. When the mix of business differs across territories — Territory B has proportionally far more High-amount exposures than Territory A — the higher loss costs associated with larger amounts of insurance are attributed entirely to the territory rather than to the amount-of-insurance factor. This creates a correlation/distributional bias that distorts the indicated territorial relativities.

### Examiner Report

Most candidates received full credit. When candidates lost points, they correctly identified key ideas regarding exposure distributions or correlation of variables but misstated the concept in some way.

## Part b (1.5 points)

Calculate the indicated pure premium relativities, while accounting for distortion that may be occurring due to amount-of-insurance differences by territory.

### Answer

**Territory A indicated relativity: 1.000; Territory B indicated relativity: 1.529**

**Step 1 — Calculate adjusted (on-level) earned exposures** by weighting each territory's raw exposures by the charged amount-of-insurance factors:

$$\text{Adjusted Exposures}_A = 50{,}000 \times 0.75 + 30{,}000 \times 1.00 + 20{,}000 \times 1.50 = 37{,}500 + 30{,}000 + 30{,}000 = 97{,}500$$

$$\text{Adjusted Exposures}_B = 25{,}000 \times 0.75 + 75{,}000 \times 1.00 + 150{,}000 \times 1.50 = 18{,}750 + 75{,}000 + 225{,}000 = 318{,}750$$

$$\text{Adjusted Exposures}_\text{Total} = 97{,}500 + 318{,}750 = 416{,}250$$

**Step 2 — Calculate the indicated pure premium per adjusted exposure:**

| Territory | Adjusted Exposures | Reported Loss & ALAE ($000s) | Pure Premium |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | 97,500 | $60,000 | $615.38 |
| B | 318,750 | $300,000 | $941.18 |
| Total | 416,250 | $360,000 | $864.86 |

**Step 3 — Indicated relativities** (using Total as the base):

$$\text{Indicated Rel}_A = \frac{615.38}{864.86} = 0.7115 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{Rel to base (A=1.000)}: \frac{0.7115}{0.7115} = \mathbf{1.000}$$

$$\text{Indicated Rel}_B = \frac{941.18}{864.86} = 1.0882 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{Rel to base (A=1.000)}: \frac{1.0882}{0.7115} = \mathbf{1.529}$$

### Examiner Report

Most candidates received full credit. Most common mistakes were using the Loss Ratio method instead of the Pure Premium method, or incorporating the current relativities into the adjusted exposure calculation.
