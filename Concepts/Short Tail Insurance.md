**Short-Tail Lines** are lines of insurance where claims are reported and settled quickly — typically within one to two years of the loss occurrence — resulting in compact development patterns and small IBNR reserves relative to case reserves.

- Common short-tail lines include auto physical damage, homeowners property damage, and inland marine; losses in these lines are usually observable immediately and do not involve latent injury or long litigation
- Because development is compact, tail factors are near $1.000$ and IBNR as a percentage of ultimate losses is small (often $5$–$15\%$), making reserve estimates less sensitive to development factor selection than in [[Long Tail Lines]]
- Faster loss emergence provides quicker feedback on rate adequacy, so shorter experience periods are acceptable in ratemaking and trends can be detected with less lag

> [!example]- Short-Tail vs. Long-Tail Development {Example}
> An auto physical damage claim arises from a collision on $3/5/2024$. A workers compensation claim for occupational disease also arises in $2024$.
>
> > [!answer]-
> > The auto physical damage claim is reported within days, inspected, and paid within $2$–$4$ weeks — development is essentially complete by $12$ months. The occupational disease claim may not be reported for years and litigation can extend settlement a decade or more. The short-tail line reaches near-ultimate loss levels with a tail factor close to $1.000$; the long-tail line requires a tail factor that may exceed $1.100$.
