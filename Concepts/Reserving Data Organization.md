**Reserving Data Organization** refers to the grouping of loss and exposure data by time period for use in unpaid claim estimation. The same methods used in ratemaking apply, but the emphasis differs because reserving requires projecting future payments, not just analyzing historical adequacy.

- **[[Accident Year]]** is the most common basis for reserving — losses are grouped by when they occurred, producing development triangles that reflect how losses on a given cohort of claims emerge over time
- **[[Policy Year]]** and **[[Underwriting Year]]** group all claims on policies with the same inception year; this basis is common in reinsurance and provides the most complete view of a book's profitability, but takes the longest to fully develop
- **[[Report Year]]** is natural for claims-made lines, where coverage is triggered by report date; claim count and dollar triangles by report year show how quickly each cohort of reported claims develops to payment
- Data must be organized consistently between loss and premium triangles; mixing accident-year losses with calendar-year premiums produces meaningless loss ratios

> [!example]- Choosing an Organization Basis {Example}
> A workers compensation insurer is estimating IBNR for permanent disability claims, which can take 10–20 years to settle. Which basis is most appropriate?
>
> > [!answer]-
> > **Accident year** is most appropriate. It groups claims by injury date, allowing actuaries to track how a cohort of injuries from a given year develops through reporting, litigation, and settlement. Policy year would also be acceptable and is sometimes used in workers comp, but accident year is the industry standard for most casualty lines.
