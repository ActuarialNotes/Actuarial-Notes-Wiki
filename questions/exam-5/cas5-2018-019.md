One year ago, an automobile insurer implemented a mobile claims reporting platform. The company anticipated that this would lead to shorter reporting patterns.

Since the mobile platform was implemented, the company has identified a shift in mix of business towards younger drivers, with younger drivers having a higher loss cost than older drivers.

Evaluate the effect on the IBNR estimated using each of the following techniques if no adjustments are made:

i. Development Technique  
ii. Expected Claims Technique  
iii. Cape Cod Technique

### Explanation

Two changes are occurring simultaneously: (1) **a shift to shorter reporting patterns** (mobile platform accelerating claim reporting) and (2) **a shift in mix toward higher-loss-cost younger drivers**.

---

**i. Development Technique**

**Direction: IBNR will be overstated.**

The development technique applies historical loss development factors (LDFs) to current reported losses. Since the mobile platform introduced a shorter reporting pattern, the historical LDFs — calculated from data prior to the mobile implementation — are **too high** relative to the true current development pattern. Applying inflated LDFs to the current reported claims implies that a greater proportion of losses remains unreported than is actually the case.

Simultaneously, the shift toward younger, higher-loss-cost drivers means that **reported losses in the current accident year are already higher** than historical averages. Applying the inflated (pre-mobile) LDFs to this higher base of reported losses further amplifies the overstatement.

Both effects compound: higher LDFs applied to a higher loss base result in a materially **overstated IBNR** estimate.

---

**ii. Expected Claims Technique**

**Direction: Little to no effect on IBNR, assuming pricing is adequate.**

The expected claims technique bases IBNR on a predetermined expected loss ratio (or expected claims) multiplied by exposure, and does not rely on actual reported losses or development factors. Therefore:

- The **change in reporting pattern** has no effect — this technique does not use LDFs.
- The **shift to younger, higher-loss-cost drivers** does not distort the result *if* those younger drivers are priced correctly (i.e., the higher loss cost is reflected in higher premiums, and the expected loss ratio or expected claims per unit of exposure remains accurate).

If pricing is adequate for both classes of drivers, the expected claims ratio remains valid and the IBNR estimate is unaffected by either change.

However, if the premium charged for younger drivers is *inadequate*, the expected claims technique would **understate IBNR**, because the higher loss costs would not be captured in the expected loss ratio.

---

**iii. Cape Cod Technique**

**Direction: IBNR will be overstated, but to a lesser extent than the Development Technique.**

The Cape Cod technique uses both development factors and a premium component. The two effects play out as follows:

- The **shorter reporting pattern** caused by the mobile platform means the historical LDFs are **too high**, just as in the development technique. Higher LDFs produce a lower % reported, which reduces used-up premium, which in turn **overstates the expected claims ratio (ECR)**, and ultimately overstates IBNR.

- The **shift to younger drivers with higher loss costs** increases reported claims in the numerator of the ECR calculation. If those drivers are priced accurately (higher premium offsets higher losses), the ECR remains stable. However, the inflated LDFs still distort the % reported, so the used-up premium is understated and the ECR is overstated regardless.

Because the Cape Cod method's premium component partially moderates the distortion (unlike the pure development technique, which has no such anchor), the **overstatement of IBNR is smaller** than under the development technique — but IBNR is still overstated if no adjustments are made to the development factors.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to recognize that there are two changes taking place — a change in reporting pattern and a shift in mix of business — and to address the impact on IBNR if no adjustments are made. This means addressing impacts for each of the three techniques and providing directional impact or lack thereof for each technique accompanied by support for the stated impacts.

Common mistakes include:
- Simply stating a directional impact without providing any support
- Failure to identify the correct directional impact from the changes
- Simply stating IBNR would be impacted or generalizing that a method would not account for changes appropriately
- Focusing on a single change and ignoring the fact that two changes are taking place

Common mistakes for part ii:
- Failure to differentiate between loss cost impact and loss ratio impact, ignoring the premium component of this technique

Common mistakes for part iii:
- Simply stating that this method is a weighted average of the development technique and expected claims technique, without demonstrating real knowledge of the method, stating directional impact to IBNR, or providing support for that impact
