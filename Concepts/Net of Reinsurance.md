**Net of Reinsurance** refers to losses, premiums, or exposures measured after subtracting amounts ceded to reinsurers, reflecting only the risk and revenue retained by the primary insurer.

> $$\text{Net} = \text{Gross} - \text{Ceded}$$

- Ratemaking can be performed on a gross (before reinsurance) or net (after reinsurance) basis; the net basis reflects the insurer's actual retained risk and is appropriate when setting rates for the insurer's own book
- Ceded premiums are treated as an underwriting expense in the permissible loss ratio calculation: $\text{PLR} = 1 - V - F - \text{Ceded Premium}\% - Q$
- Alternatively, the indicated rate can be computed on a gross basis and then a reinsurance cost loading added separately to recover ceded premiums
- Net loss ratios are typically lower than gross loss ratios when reinsurance caps large losses (excess-of-loss treaties reduce net losses); net premiums are lower by the ceded premium amount

> [!example]- Net vs. Gross Loss Ratio {Example}
> An insurer writes $2,000,000 in gross earned premium and cedes 10% ($200,000). Gross incurred losses are $1,300,000; reinsurance recovers $150,000 of large losses. What are the gross and net loss ratios?
>
> > [!answer]-
> > $$\text{Gross LR} = \frac{\$1{,}300{,}000}{\$2{,}000{,}000} = 65\%$$
> > $$\text{Net LR} = \frac{\$1{,}300{,}000 - \$150{,}000}{\$2{,}000{,}000 - \$200{,}000} = \frac{\$1{,}150{,}000}{\$1{,}800{,}000} = 63.9\%$$
> > The net loss ratio is lower because reinsurance recoveries reduce losses more proportionally than premiums.
