Within the last year, an insurer implemented a new claims processing system that resulted in faster payment of claims. However, the claims department failed to communicate this change to the actuarial department, and the actuary continues to use the paid loss development method to select the insurer's ultimate losses.

For each item below, discuss the impact of using the actuary's calculation of ultimate losses on the following estimates.

## Part a (0.5 points)

Ultimate ALAE calculated using a paid ALAE to paid claims ratio method.

### Answer

The actuary's ultimate losses will be **overstated**. Applying the historical paid ALAE-to-paid-claims ratio to an overstated ultimate claims figure will produce **overstated ultimate ALAE**.

Specifically: the paid loss development method uses historical paid development factors derived from a slower payment pattern. Because claims are now being paid faster, current paid losses are higher than the historical pattern would predict. The development factors — calibrated to the old, slower pattern — are still large, so when applied to the already-elevated current paid losses, they project an inflated ultimate. The historical paid ALAE-to-paid-claims ratio (which has not changed) is then applied to this inflated ultimate claims figure, producing an overstated ALAE estimate.

### Explanation

The paid loss development method applied to a book with accelerated claim payments will produce inflated ultimates because:

1. Current-period paid losses are **higher** than they would have been under the old payment pattern (more claims have been paid by the evaluation date).
2. The development factors still assume the old (slower) pattern, adding a development load on top of an already-elevated base.
3. The overstated ultimate is used as the denominator/basis for the ALAE ratio, compounding the overstatement.

The key mechanism: it is the application of the historical paid-to-paid ratio to the **overstated ultimate** that produces the overstated ALAE. Focusing only on the ratio itself (which may be accurate historically) while neglecting the inflated ultimate misses the second-order effect.

### Examiner Report

Many candidates did mention that the ultimate losses would increase. However, a common mistake was not to explain appropriately how this would lead to higher ALAE (it is the application of the historical paid-to-paid ratio to the overstated ultimate that produces the overstated ALAE result). Another common error was to focus just on the paid-to-paid ratio, but neglect that the paid ultimate is going to be significantly higher.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Ultimate ULAE calculated using the Wendy Johnson method.

### Answer

The actuary's overstated ultimate losses have **no impact** on the Wendy Johnson ULAE estimate.

The Wendy Johnson method is a **count-based** technique that allocates ULAE based on the number of claim transactions (openings and closings), not on the dollar amount of losses. Because the new claims processing system resulted in faster payment — not a change in the number of claims or transactions — the count-based Wendy Johnson method is unaffected by the dollar overstatement in ultimate losses.

### Explanation

The Wendy Johnson method assumes that the same amount of ULAE is spent on similar transactions regardless of the size of the claim (i.e., it treats claims handling as a fixed cost per transaction, not a percentage of loss dollars). Therefore:

- Changes in the dollar level of ultimate losses do not feed into the Wendy Johnson ULAE estimate.
- The method is **not** distorted by the actuary's overstatement of ultimate losses.
- This is in contrast to a percent-of-loss ULAE method, which would be directly impacted.

### Examiner Report

Most candidates did not receive credit for this part.

## Part c (0.5 points)

Rates calculated for the upcoming policy year using the pure premium method.

### Answer

The pure premium rates will be **overstated** (too high).

The pure premium method divides projected ultimate losses by earned exposures to calculate a loss pure premium, which is then loaded for expenses and profit to produce the indicated rate. Because the actuary's ultimate losses are overstated due to the faster payment pattern being misread as actual development, the calculated pure premium will be too high, leading to indicated rates that are higher than necessary.

If rates are implemented based on this overstated indication, the insurer will be charging more than needed to cover expected future losses.

### Explanation

$$\text{Pure Premium} = \frac{\text{Projected Ultimate Losses}}{\text{Earned Exposures}}$$

Since projected ultimate losses are inflated, the pure premium is inflated, and the indicated rate change will be upward-biased. This is a prospective impact: the insurer may raise rates unnecessarily, potentially losing competitive position.

### Examiner Report

This part was generally answered quite well. The most common error was to indicate that the ultimate losses/pure premium increased, but not mention anything about the rates themselves increasing.

## Part d (0.5 points)

The reinsurance recoverable for the underlying business on an excess-of-loss reinsurance contract where the retention has been exceeded but the limit has not yet been exhausted.

### Answer

The estimated reinsurance recoverable will be **overstated**.

The actuary's overstated ultimate losses/ALAE mean that the projected loss amounts falling above the retention and below the limit are also overstated. Since the retention has already been exceeded (losses are in the reinsured layer) but the limit has not been exhausted (further recovery is still possible), the overstated ultimate loss projection inflates the estimated amount within the reinsured layer, producing an overstated recoverable from the reinsurer.

### Explanation

For an excess-of-loss (XL) contract with retention $R$ and limit $L$ (maximum recovery $L - R$):

- If projected ultimate L/ALAE exceeds $R$ but is less than $R + L$, the estimated recoverable is: $\min(\text{Ultimate} - R,\ L - R)$
- An overstated ultimate loss moves this projected figure higher within the layer, increasing the estimated recoverable
- If the overstatement were severe enough to project losses above $R + L$, the recoverable would be capped at $L - R$ — but the problem states the limit has not been exhausted, so we are still in the layer where recoverable increases with projected ultimate

The key elements are: (1) the retention has been exceeded — recovery is possible, and (2) the limit has not been exhausted — the recovery amount is not yet capped. Both conditions must be mentioned to receive full credit.

### Examiner Report

This was also answered quite well. A common error was a lack of detail, with credit lost if candidates did not mention the fact that the retention was exceeded or that the limit had not yet been reached.
