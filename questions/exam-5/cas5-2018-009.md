An auto insurer is evaluating the variable "number of vehicles" for inclusion in a rating plan. Given the following Generalized Linear Model (GLM) output:

The first chart shows exposures (bars) and indicated relativities with upper and lower standard error bounds across number-of-vehicles buckets (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7+). The indicated relativities trend upward from about 0.5 at 1 vehicle to about 3.0 at 7+ vehicles, but confidence intervals are wide and overlap 1.0 for most buckets. The chi-square percentage for number of vehicles is **10%**.

The second chart shows the same variable plotted across three calendar years (2015 Data, 2016 Data, 2017 Data), along with exposures. The relativities for buckets 1 and 2 are relatively consistent across years, but buckets 3 through 7+ show substantial variation across years.

- Number of vehicles chi-square percentage: 10%

## Part a (1 point)

Fully justify whether number of vehicles should be included in the rating plan.

### Explanation

Based on the GLM output, the variable **should NOT** be included in the rating plan. The justification rests on three tests:

**Main Effect Test (Chi-square):** The chi-square percentage is 10%, which is above the 5% significance threshold. This means the variable does not pass the main effect test — its overall contribution is not statistically significant at the standard level.

**Statistical Test (Standard Error Bounds):** For nearly all levels of the variable, the value 1.0 falls within the upper and lower standard error bounds. This means the indicated relativities are not statistically distinguishable from 1.0 (i.e., no effect) for most buckets. The variable fails the statistical test.

**Consistency Test:** The relativities for buckets 3 and above vary considerably across the three years (2015, 2016, 2017 data). The pattern is not consistent over time beyond the first few buckets, indicating the variable is not a reliable predictor. The variable fails the consistency test.

Because the variable fails multiple tests (main effect, statistical, and consistency), it should not be included in the rating plan.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to demonstrate knowledge and proper application of tests used to analyze the predictive quality of a variable based on GLM output: Main Effect Test, Consistency Test, Statistical Test, and Judgment. Candidates were also expected to demonstrate a clear decision on whether the variable should or should not be included based on the test results.

Candidates did not receive credit if they incorrectly stated/implied the variable passed the main effect test or the consistency test. Further, candidates did not receive credit for simply stating a recommendation on exclusion/inclusion of variable with no justification.

Common mistakes include:
- Did not clearly indicate whether the variable passed or failed each test
- Did not clearly state whether variable should or should not be used

## Part b (0.75 points)

Briefly discuss three challenges associated with performing GLM analysis on loss ratio data.

### Explanation

Three challenges of using loss ratio data in a GLM:

1. **Premiums must be on-leveled:** Loss ratios use premium as the denominator, but premiums reflect historical rate levels that may differ across years and geographies. Premiums must be brought to a current rate level before computing loss ratios; otherwise the GLM is modeling changes in rate adequacy rather than changes in risk.

2. **No standard default distribution for loss ratios:** GLMs require specifying a distribution family (e.g., Poisson for counts, gamma for severity). There is no natural or widely accepted distribution for the ratio of two random variables like loss ratios, making model specification difficult.

3. **Loss ratios do not exhibit clear trend components:** Frequency and severity each have independent trend factors that can be modeled separately. Loss ratios combine these effects and also embed expense ratios and rate changes, making it difficult to identify or apply trend factors.

Additional valid challenges include:
- Actuaries have no a priori expectation for the loss ratio level, unlike frequency or severity
- Loss ratio models become obsolete when rate changes occur, requiring frequent updates

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to list three challenges of using GLM on loss ratio data.

A common mistake was to list general challenges of GLM without any reference to the specific issues of using loss ratio data.

## Part c (0.25 points)

GLM analysis is widely accepted in classification ratemaking. Briefly discuss one reason that univariate analysis may be more appropriate than GLM analysis.

### Explanation

Any one of the following reasons is acceptable:

- **Simplicity and transparency:** Univariate analysis is easier to compute and understand. Regulators, management, and agents can more readily follow and audit a univariate result than a complex GLM. In some jurisdictions, regulators may explicitly require univariate methods.

- **Insufficient data:** If the company does not have enough data for a GLM to produce credible results (e.g., a small book or a new market), univariate analysis may be more appropriate. GLMs require larger sample sizes to estimate interaction effects reliably.

- **Computational resources:** Univariate analysis does not require the computing infrastructure, software, or actuarial expertise needed to set up and run a GLM. For smaller companies, this may make univariate analysis the practical choice.

- **Simple rating algorithm:** If the rating plan is intentionally kept simple (e.g., for regulatory or competitive reasons), a univariate approach aligned with a simple plan may be more appropriate than introducing higher complexity.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to discuss one reason why univariate analysis could be more appropriate than multivariate analysis.

A common mistake was to describe a difficulty but not specify whether that difficulty applied to multivariate or univariate analysis, so credit could not be awarded.
