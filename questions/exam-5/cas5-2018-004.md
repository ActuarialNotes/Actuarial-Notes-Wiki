Given the following information:

| Report Year | Loss Costs by Report Year Lag ($) | | |
|-------------|------|------|------|
| | **Lag 0** | **Lag 1** | **Lag 2** |
| 2015 | 500 | 300 | 200 |
| 2016 | 525 | 330 | 210 |
| 2017 | 550 | 365 | 220 |

## Part a (0.25 points)

Calculate the report year 2015 loss costs for a claims-made policy.

### Answer
1,000

### Explanation
A claims-made policy covers all claims **reported** during the policy year, regardless of when the loss occurred. Therefore, report year 2015 loss costs include all claims reported in 2015 — which spans all report year lags attributed to accident year 2015 reporting in 2015, plus claims from prior accident years reported in 2015.

The loss costs by report year lag for report year 2015 represent claims reported in 2015 that occurred 0, 1, or 2 years prior (i.e., accident years 2015, 2014, and 2013 respectively):

$$\text{RY 2015 Claims-Made Loss Costs} = 500 + 300 + 200 = 1{,}000$$

*Note: If this is a first-year claims-made policy (covering only losses from the current accident year with no prior acts coverage), then only the lag-0 losses apply: $500. Full credit required stating this assumption explicitly.*

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to know that all loss costs reported during the year 2015 would be covered by a claims-made policy, regardless of when the losses occurred.

A common mistake was to assume that the claims-made policy only covered loss costs reported and occurred in 2015 without stating any assumptions (i.e., $500).

## Part b (0.25 points)

Calculate the accident year 2015 loss costs for an occurrence policy.

### Answer
1,050

### Explanation
An occurrence policy covers all claims **arising from incidents that occurred** during the policy year, regardless of when they are reported or paid. Therefore, accident year 2015 loss costs include all claims from accidents in 2015 — those reported immediately (lag 0), those reported one year later (lag 1 from the 2016 report year row), and those reported two years later (lag 2 from the 2017 report year row):

$$\text{AY 2015 Occurrence Loss Costs} = 500 + 330 + 220 = 1{,}050$$

The lag-0 cost of 500 comes from report year 2015 (reported same year as occurrence). The lag-1 cost of 330 comes from report year 2016 (occurred in 2015, reported in 2016). The lag-2 cost of 220 comes from report year 2017 (occurred in 2015, reported in 2017).

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to know that all loss costs that occurred during the year 2015 would be covered by an occurrence policy, accounting for various lags in reporting.

A common mistake was to assume an occurrence policy covered claims reported in 2015.

## Part c (0.5 points)

Compare the effect of an unexpected increase in underlying trend on the accuracy of the pricing for a claims-made policy and an occurrence policy. Briefly explain why one of the policies is impacted more than the other.

### Explanation
An unexpected increase in underlying trend will distort the accuracy of **occurrence policy** pricing more than claims-made policy pricing.

An occurrence policy is subject to both **report lag** (time from accident to report) and **settlement lag** (time from report to settlement). Claims can continue to be reported for several years after the accident year. All of those claims, regardless of when reported, are subject to the underlying trend — and because the development period is long, the trend compounds over a greater period of time. A change in underlying trend that was not anticipated when rates were set will therefore have a larger cumulative impact on occurrence policies.

A claims-made policy eliminates the pure IBNR component: all claims covered by the policy must be **reported** during the policy year. This removes the multi-year report lag and compresses the exposure to trend into a single report year. As a result, an unexpected change in trend has a much shorter period over which it can distort pricing accuracy for claims-made policies.

In summary: occurrence policies have a longer development period than claims-made policies, making their pricing more sensitive to unexpected trend changes.

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to understand that an occurrence policy has a longer development period than a claims-made policy because claims can still be reported further into the future for an occurrence policy. They were expected to recognize that these claims would be more impacted by a change in future trend.

A common mistake was simply stating that claims-made policies are shorter tailed than occurrence policies, without explaining why they are shorter tailed or why it matters.

## Part d (0.25 points)

Briefly explain why the risk of reserve inadequacy is reduced for a claims-made policy relative to an occurrence policy.

### Explanation
Reserves for a claims-made policy carry **less risk of inadequacy** than reserves for an occurrence policy because claims-made policies have no pure IBNR (Incurred But Not Yet Reported) component.

Under an occurrence policy, reserves must cover two sources of uncertainty:
1. **Pure IBNR (IBNYR)**: claims that have already occurred but have not yet been reported to the insurer.
2. **IBNER**: claims that have been reported but for which the case reserve may be insufficient.

Under a claims-made policy, all covered claims must be reported during the policy period. There is therefore no pure IBNR — the risk of unknown, unreported claims does not exist. Only **IBNER** (development on known claims) remains. This significantly reduces the total reserve uncertainty and the risk of reserve inadequacy.

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to know that the IBNR includes two components: pure IBNR (IBNYR) for losses incurred but not yet reported, and IBNER for losses incurred but not enough reported. Candidates were expected to know that a claims-made policy only has IBNER, while an occurrence policy has both pure IBNR and IBNER.

A common mistake was to mention that claims-made policies had no IBNR, without elaborating why this is meaningful to the question being asked.

## Part e (0.25 points)

Briefly describe why the investment income earned from claims-made policies is less than under occurrence policies.

### Explanation
Investment income is earned on the funds held by the insurer between the collection of premium and the payment of claims. For claims-made policies, all covered claims must be reported during the policy year, which means the time from premium collection to claim settlement is shorter than for occurrence policies.

Under an **occurrence policy**, losses can be reported and settled many years after the premium is collected, giving the insurer a longer period over which to invest reserves and earn investment income.

Under a **claims-made policy**, the requirement that claims be reported during the policy year compresses this timeline. Claims are reported sooner, and settlement typically follows more quickly, leaving a shorter investment period and thus generating less investment income.

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to understand that the investment period is shorter for a claims-made policy since there is less time between the collection of premium and the claim payment, thus resulting in less investment income.

A common mistake was stating that claims-made policies have a longer time lapse between the claim occurrence and the claim payment, instead of between the premium collection (or beginning of coverage) and the claim payment.
