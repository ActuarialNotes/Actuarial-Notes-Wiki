The number of cars passing through the Lexington Tunnel follows a Poisson process with rate:

$$
\lambda(t) =
\begin{cases}
16 + 2.5t & \text{for } 0 < t \le 8 \\
52 - 2t & \text{for } 8 < t \le 12 \\
-20 + 4t & \text{for } 12 < t \le 18 \\
160 - 6t & \text{for } 18 < t \le 24
\end{cases}
$$

Calculate the probability that exactly 50 cars pass through the tunnel between times $t = 11$ and $t = 13$.

- A) Less than 0.01
- B) At least 0.01, but less than 0.02
- C) At least 0.02, but less than 0.03
- D) At least 0.03, but less than 0.04
- E) At least 0.04

## Explanation

This is a **non-homogeneous Poisson process**. The number of cars in $[11, 13]$ is Poisson with mean equal to the integral of the rate function over that interval. The interval spans two pieces of $\lambda(t)$: $[11, 12]$ uses $52 - 2t$ and $[12, 13]$ uses $-20 + 4t$.

$$m = \int_{11}^{12} (52 - 2t)\, dt + \int_{12}^{13} (-20 + 4t)\, dt.$$

First piece:

$$\int_{11}^{12} (52 - 2t)\, dt = \left[52t - t^2\right]_{11}^{12} = (624 - 144) - (572 - 121) = 480 - 451 = 29.$$

Second piece:

$$\int_{12}^{13} (-20 + 4t)\, dt = \left[-20t + 2t^2\right]_{12}^{13} = (-260 + 338) - (-240 + 288) = 78 - 48 = 30.$$

So $m = 29 + 30 = 59$, and the count $N \sim \text{Poisson}(59)$.

$$\Pr[N = 50] = \frac{e^{-59}\, 59^{50}}{50!} \approx 0.0273.$$

This falls in $[0.02, 0.03)$, so the answer is **C**.
