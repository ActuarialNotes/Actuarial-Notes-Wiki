**Line of Business** is a grouping of insurance policies that cover a similar type of risk and share rating, reserving, and regulatory treatment — for example personal auto, homeowners, workers compensation, or general liability. The line is the primary unit at which exposure bases, rate indications, and reserve analyses are organized.

- Choice of line dictates the appropriate [[Exposure Base]] (car-years for auto, payroll for workers comp, sales or square footage for general liability) and the relevant trends and development patterns.
- Lines differ on the dimensions tested in [[Types of Insurance]]: **long-tail vs. short-tail** (settlement speed), **low- vs. high-frequency**, and **occurrence vs. claims-made** coverage triggers — each of which changes how data is aggregated and how unpaid claims are estimated.
- Ratemaking and reserving data are segmented by line to balance [[Homogeneity]] (risks that behave alike) against [[Credibility]] (enough volume to be statistically reliable); over-segmenting sacrifices credibility, under-segmenting sacrifices homogeneity.
- Statutory reporting (Annual Statement Schedule P, NAIC lines) and ASOPs require analysis at defined line-of-business levels, so the actuary's groupings must reconcile to these external categories.

> [!example]- Matching Exposure Base to Line {Example}
> An actuary is asked to set the exposure base for three lines: personal auto, workers compensation, and commercial general liability.
>
> > [!answer]-
> > | Line | Exposure base | Why |
> > |---|---|---|
> > | Personal auto | Car-years | Loss potential scales with vehicles and time exposed |
> > | Workers comp | Payroll (per \$100) | Wage replacement losses scale with payroll |
> > | General liability | Sales / receipts | Third-party exposure scales with business activity |
> >
> > Each base is chosen to be proportional to expected loss, practical to measure, and resistant to manipulation — the criteria for a good exposure base within a line.
