**Fixed Expenses** are underwriting expenses that do not vary proportionally with premium—they are incurred on a per-policy or per-exposure basis regardless of premium size—and must be recovered through a per-exposure loading rather than a percentage-of-premium loading.

> $$\text{Fixed Expense Load per Exposure} = \frac{\text{Total Fixed Expenses}}{\text{Number of Exposures}}$$

- Examples include policy issuance costs, inspection fees, state filing fees, and a portion of agent commissions that are flat per policy rather than percentage-based
- Contrast with [[Variable Expenses]], which are expressed as a constant percentage of premium (e.g., premium taxes, variable commissions); fixed expenses create a dollar amount added per exposure unit
- In the [[Pure Premium Method]], the indicated rate is: $\text{Rate} = (\text{Pure Premium} + \text{Fixed Expense Load}) / (1 - V - Q)$, where $V$ is the variable expense ratio and $Q$ is the profit provision
- Allocating fixed expenses as a percentage of premium (instead of per exposure) cross-subsidizes small policies from large ones, since large-premium insureds pay more than their share of fixed costs

> [!example]- Fixed Expense Loading in Rate Derivation {Example}
> An insurer has $500,000 in fixed expenses and 10,000 earned exposures. The pure premium is $200, variable expenses are 20%, and the profit provision is 5%. What is the indicated rate?
>
> > [!answer]-
> > $$\text{Fixed Load} = \frac{\$500{,}000}{10{,}000} = \$50 \text{ per exposure}$$
> > $$\text{Rate} = \frac{\$200 + \$50}{1 - 0.20 - 0.05} = \frac{\$250}{0.75} = \$333.33 \text{ per exposure}$$
