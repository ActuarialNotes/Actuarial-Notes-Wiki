Given the following:

| Calendar Year | Earned Premium ($) |
|---|---|
| 2017 | 3,850,000 |
| 2018 | 4,200,000 |

| Rate Change Effective Date | Overall Rate Change |
|---|---|
| January 1, 2017 | 10% |
| July 1, 2017 | 5% |

| Quarter and Year | Average Written Premium at Current Rate Level |
|---|---|
| 2Q 2016 | \$1,771 |
| 4Q 2016 | \$1,806 |
| 2Q 2017 | \$1,840 |
| 4Q 2017 | \$1,877 |
| 2Q 2018 | \$1,914 |
| 4Q 2018 | \$1,953 |

- No rate changes occurred in 2016 or 2018.
- All policies are semi-annual.
- All policies are written uniformly throughout the year.

## Part a (1.75 points)

Calculate the trended on-level earned premium for 2017 to be used in a rate change effective July 1, 2019.

### Answer
4549422

### Explanation
**On-leveling (parallelogram method).** With 6-month policies written uniformly throughout the year, calendar year 2017 earned premium is affected by the rate changes effective January 1, 2017 (+10%) and July 1, 2017 (+5%). Splitting the CY2017 parallelogram into the three rate-level regions gives:

| Rate level | Index | Share of CY2017 EP |
|---|---|---|
| Before 1/1/2017 | $1.00$ | 25% |
| 1/1/2017 – 6/30/2017 | $1.00 \times 1.10 = 1.10$ | 50% |
| On/after 7/1/2017 | $1.10 \times 1.05 = 1.155$ | 25% |

Average rate level for CY2017:
$$1.00(0.25) + 1.10(0.50) + 1.155(0.25) = 1.08878$$

Current rate level (CRL):
$$1.00 \times 1.10 \times 1.05 = 1.155$$

On-level factor:
$$OLF = \frac{1.155}{1.08878} = 1.06085$$

On-level EP for 2017:
$$3{,}850{,}000 \times 1.06085 = 4{,}084{,}271$$

**Trending.** The annual change in average written premium at current rate level is approximately:
$$\frac{1{,}840}{1{,}771} - 1 \approx 3.9\%, \qquad \frac{1{,}877}{1{,}806} - 1 \approx 3.9\%$$

Select an annual trend rate of 4%.

For semi-annual policies written uniformly throughout the year, the average written date of CY2017 earned premium is the average earned date (7/1/2017) less half the 6-month policy term (3 months), i.e. **4/1/2017**. The new rates will be in effect 7/1/2019–6/30/2020, with an average written date of **1/1/2020** (the midpoint of that period). The trend period is therefore:
$$1/1/2020 - 4/1/2017 = 2.75 \text{ years}$$

Trended on-level EP:
$$4{,}084{,}271 \times 1.04^{2.75} \approx 4{,}549{,}422$$

Alternatively, a two-step trend (e.g. selecting the latest observed annual trend of about 4.0% and splitting the 2.75-year period into two steps) gives a similar result of approximately \$4,554,000.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Briefly describe two reasons why the 2017 trended on-level earned premium would be higher if all policies were annual rather than semi-annual.

### Explanation
**Reason 1 — longer trend period.** With 12-month policies, the average written date of CY2017 earned premium would be the average earned date (7/1/2017) less half of a 12-month term (6 months), i.e. 1/1/2017, rather than 4/1/2017. The trend period to the future rating period (average written date 1/1/2020) would become 3.00 years instead of 2.75 years. Since the trend factor exceeds 1.0, a longer trend period produces a larger trended premium.

**Reason 2 — higher on-level factor.** With 12-month policies written uniformly, the parallelogram shifts so that a larger share of CY2017 earned premium remains at the older, lower rate levels, making the average historical rate level lower relative to the current rate level than under the semi-annual parallelogram. A lower average rate level relative to the current rate level produces a larger on-level factor, which increases the on-level premium.

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to demonstrate knowledge of on-leveling via the parallelogram method and of premium trending, and to explain how moving from semi-annual to annual policies would affect each.

For Part a, common mistakes included arithmetic errors in the parallelogram method and in the semiannual written-premium trend calculation, applying an annual trend period to a semiannual trend selection, and — for candidates using a two-step trend — trending the 2017Q4 average premium to 2018Q4 without recognizing that this represented only half of 2017's experience.

For Part b, candidates needed to provide a distinct explanation for each of the two reasons. Common mistakes included giving two explanations for the same reason rather than one for each, and simply stating that "the OLF would be higher" without explaining why.
