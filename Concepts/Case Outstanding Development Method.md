**Case Outstanding Development Method** is a reserving technique that projects ultimate losses by applying development factors to case reserves (open claim outstanding amounts) rather than to total incurred losses, isolating the development of reserves already established by the claims department.

> $$\text{Ultimate} = \text{Paid} + \text{Case Reserves} \times f_{\text{case}}$$

> $$\text{IBNR} = \text{Case Reserves} \times (f_{\text{case}} - 1)$$

- The case development factor $f_{\text{case}}$ captures how much additional payment above current case reserves is expected; values $> 1.0$ indicate case reserves are inadequate on average
- Useful when **case reserves are more stable and credible than total incurred**, or when paid loss patterns are distorted by changes in settlement speed
- Can be combined with a separate paid loss projection: develop paid losses with paid CDFs and case outstanding with case factors, then sum
- A case factor significantly above 1.0 may signal systematic reserve inadequacy that should be investigated before relying on this method

> [!example]- Case Outstanding Projection {Example}
> AY 2023 at 24 months: paid $= \$800{,}000$, case outstanding $= \$500{,}000$, total incurred $= \$1{,}300{,}000$.
>
> Selected factors: 24-to-ultimate case factor $= 1.200$; paid 24-to-ultimate factor $= 1.500$.
>
> > [!answer]-
> > Case Outstanding method: $800{,}000 \times 1.500 + 500{,}000 \times 1.200 = 1{,}200{,}000 + 600{,}000 = \$1{,}800{,}000$
> >
> > Traditional incurred method (for comparison): $1{,}300{,}000 \times 1.350 = \$1{,}755{,}000$
> >
> > IBNR (case method) $= 1{,}800{,}000 - 1{,}300{,}000 = \$500{,}000$; the $\$45{,}000$ difference reflects the case method's explicit recognition that current cases will develop beyond their stated amounts
