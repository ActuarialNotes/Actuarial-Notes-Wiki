**Exposure Base** is the unit used to measure the amount of insurance risk underwritten, serving as the denominator in pure premium calculations and as the basis for premium charges.

> $$\text{Pure Premium} = \frac{\text{Losses + LAE}}{\text{Earned Exposures}}$$

- A valid exposure base must be proportional to expected losses, practical to measure and verify, not easily manipulated by the insured, and stable over the policy period
- Common bases by line: car-years (personal auto), house-years (homeowners), payroll per $100 (workers comp), gross sales receipts (general liability), gallons (inland marine fuel)
- Workers compensation uses payroll because higher wages correlate with greater injury costs and replacement wages, making expected losses proportional to the base
- The exposure base is held fixed within a ratemaking analysis; changes to the base require restatement of historical data

> [!example]- Workers Comp Exposure Base Selection {Example}
> An insurer writes workers compensation for two employers: Employer A has $2M payroll and Employer B has $1M payroll in the same class. Why is payroll preferable to number of employees as the exposure base?
>
> > [!answer]-
> > If Employer A has 10 employees earning $200K each and Employer B has 20 employees earning $50K each, the number-of-employees base would assign twice the premium to B, even though A's higher-wage workers generate larger indemnity payments when injured. Payroll correctly assigns Employer A twice the premium of B, reflecting the proportional relationship between wages and expected claim costs.
