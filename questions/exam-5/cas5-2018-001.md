Given the following:

| Policy | Number of Vehicles | Effective Date | Expiration Date |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|-----------------|
| A | 2 | January 1, 2018 | June 30, 2018 |
| B | 3 | March 1, 2018 | August 31, 2018 |
| C | 1 | July 1, 2018 | December 31, 2018 |
| D | 2 | October 1, 2018 | March 31, 2019 |
| E | 1 | November 1, 2018 | April 30, 2019 |

- All policies remain in-force until their expiration date.
- An exposure is defined as one vehicle insured for one year.

## Part a (0.25 points)

Calculate the calendar year 2018 written exposures.

### Answer
4.5

### Explanation
All policies are written in 2018. Each is a 6-month (0.5 year) policy, so written exposure per vehicle is 0.5.

$$0.5 \times (2 + 3 + 1 + 2 + 1) = 4.5$$

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to recognize these are 6-month policies and apply the 0.5 factor.

A common mistake was forgetting to multiply by 0.5 to account for the 6-month term.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Calculate the calendar year 2018 earned exposures.

### Answer
3.67

### Explanation
Policies A, B, and C are fully earned within CY 2018. Policy D earns 3 of its 6 months in CY 2018 (Oct–Dec). Policy E earns 2 of its 6 months in CY 2018 (Nov–Dec).

$$\frac{2 \times 6}{12} + \frac{3 \times 6}{12} + \frac{1 \times 6}{12} + 2 \times \frac{3}{12} + 1 \times \frac{2}{12} = 3.67$$

Alternatively: $0.5 \times \left(2 + 3 + 1 + 2 \times \frac{3}{6} + 1 \times \frac{2}{6}\right) = 3.67$

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to know how to calculate calendar year earned exposure and recognize the 6-month policy term.

Common mistakes included forgetting to multiply by 0.5 to account for the 6-month term, and double-counting the 6-month term by dividing earned months by 12 but still multiplying by 0.5.

## Part c (0.5 points)

Calculate the policy year 2018 earned exposures as of February 28, 2019.

### Answer
4.17

### Explanation
Policies A, B, and C are fully earned in PY 2018 as of February 28, 2019. Policy D has earned 5 of 6 months by that date (Oct 2018–Feb 2019). Policy E has earned 4 of 6 months (Nov 2018–Feb 2019).

$$\frac{2 \times 6}{12} + \frac{3 \times 6}{12} + \frac{1 \times 6}{12} + 2 \times \frac{5}{12} + 1 \times \frac{4}{12} = 4.17$$

Alternatively, start from the CY 2018 earned answer and add the two additional months for Policies D and E:

$$3.67 + 2 \times \frac{2}{6} \times 0.5 + 1 \times \frac{2}{6} \times 0.5 = 4.17$$

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to know how to calculate policy year earned exposure and distinguish it from calendar year earned exposure.

Common mistakes included forgetting to multiply by 0.5 to account for the 6-month term, and double-counting the 6-month term.

## Part d (0.25 points)

Calculate the in-force exposures as of October 15, 2018.

### Answer
3

### Explanation
As of October 15, 2018: Policies A and B have expired; Policies C and D are in force; Policy E has not yet taken effect.

$$0 + 0 + 1 + 2 + 0 = 3$$

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to identify which policies were active on the given date.

A common mistake was counting Policy E as in-force when its effective date of November 1, 2018 had not yet arrived.

## Part e (1 point)

Identify two criteria of an exposure base and briefly evaluate miles driven as an exposure base for personal auto insurance using those criteria.

### Explanation
Any two of the following criteria may be selected:

**Criterion: Proportional to expected losses**
Satisfied. The more miles driven, the greater the exposure to loss for Liability and Collision coverages — more driving means more opportunities for an accident. However, miles driven does not affect Comprehensive losses (e.g. theft, weather), so the relationship is not universal.

**Criterion: Practical / Easy to obtain, inexpensive to verify / Objective**
Mixed. Miles can be obtained via telematics, but telematics may be costly to implement. If self-reported by the insured, miles driven can be easily manipulated and is difficult to verify, making it less objective. Miles driven is itself a number (objective), but obtaining a reliable figure is challenging.

**Criterion: Considerate of historical precedence**
Not satisfied. The current personal auto exposure base is car-years. Changing to miles driven would be costly from an IT perspective, would require adjustment to historical analyses, and could produce large premium swings for individual insureds.

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to know the criteria for an appropriate exposure base and to evaluate miles driven against each chosen criterion with an explanation, not merely a conclusion.

Common mistakes: simply stating miles driven was proportional to losses without explaining why; stating car-years was a better exposure base than miles driven without explaining why; confusing exposure base criteria with social criteria such as causality or controllability.
