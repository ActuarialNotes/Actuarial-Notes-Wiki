An insurance company is considering changing its exposure base for workers compensation from payroll to hours worked.

Evaluate the merits of this change based on **three different criteria of a good exposure base**. For each criterion, evaluate both payroll and hours worked.

## Answer

For each criterion, either exposure base can be argued; the key is providing a supported comparison of both.

**1. Proportional to expected losses.**

Payroll is more proportional to expected losses for indemnity benefits because workers compensation indemnity payments are based on a percentage of wages — higher-paid workers generate higher severity claims, so payroll captures both frequency and severity. Hours worked is more proportional to medical and frequency-related losses because more hours on the job simply means more time exposed to workplace hazards; however, it does not capture the wage-related severity of indemnity claims.

**2. Practical, objectively verifiable, and easy to obtain.**

Payroll is more practical because it is already reported on W-2 forms and other financial records that employers are legally required to maintain, making it easy to verify during audits. Hours worked is harder to verify because it relies on employer time records, which can be subject to manipulation or inconsistent recordkeeping, especially for salaried workers whose hours are not tracked.

**3. Historical precedent / consistency with the current system.**

Payroll has been the standard workers compensation exposure base for many decades and is deeply embedded in rating algorithms, IT systems, and regulatory filings. Switching to hours worked would require costly changes to data collection systems, rating software, and forms, and could cause significant premium disruption for policyholders. Hours worked does not have this established precedent in the industry.

**Overall assessment:** The change to hours worked is not recommended. While hours worked may better capture frequency exposure, the gain is outweighed by its lower verifiability and the substantial practical costs of transitioning away from the well-established payroll base.

## Examiner Report

Nearly all candidates were able to name three criteria for an exposure base. When candidates lost points, it was typically due to only evaluating hours worked as an exposure base, without comparison to payroll. Others lost credit for not supporting the reason, or for simply restating the criteria as the reason. For example, with the proportionality criteria, simply stating that "hours worked is proportional to expected loss" without further explanation was not given full credit. Given justification, candidates could argue for either exposure base for any particular criteria.
