Given the following information for a policy:

- Annual earned premium = $1,000.
- New business expected loss ratio = 60%.
- Losses expected to decrease $25 per year.
- New business expenses = $420.
- Renewal business expenses = $350.
- Probability of first renewal = 85%.
- Probability of second renewal = 90%.
- Probability of third renewal = 0%.
- Assume an annual discount rate of 3%.

## Part a (1.75 points)

Calculate the lifetime value of the expected total profit as a percentage of premium.

### Answer

4.477%

### Explanation

**Setting up the lifetime profit table**

The policy can last a maximum of 3 years (new business + first renewal + second renewal). After the second renewal, the probability of a third renewal is 0%, so the policy terminates.

**Loss amounts by duration:**
- Year 1 (new business): $1,000 × 60% = $600
- Year 2 (first renewal): $600 − $25 = $575
- Year 3 (second renewal): $575 − $25 = $550

**Persistency:**
- Year 1: 100% (all new policyholders)
- Year 2: 85% (probability of first renewal)
- Year 3: 85% × 90% = 76.5% (cumulative probability of second renewal)

**Discount factors:**
- Year 1: $\frac{1}{1.03^0} = 1.000$
- Year 2: $\frac{1}{1.03^1} = 0.97087$
- Year 3: $\frac{1}{1.03^2} = 0.94260$

**Profit per policy in force = Premium − Losses − Expenses**

| Duration | (1) Premium | (2) Loss | (3) Expense | (4) Persistency | (5) Cumulative Persistency | (6) Discount Factor | (7) PV of Profit | PV of Premium |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | $1,000 | $600 | $420 | 100% | 100% | 1.000 | $(20.00) | $1,000.00 |
| 2 | $1,000 | $575 | $350 | 85% | 85% | 1.030 | $61.89 | $825.24 |
| 3 | $1,000 | $550 | $350 | 90% | 76.5% | 1.0609 | $72.11 | $721.09 |
| **Total** | | | | | | | **$114.00** | **$2,546.33** |

**Column (7) calculation — PV of Profit:**

$$\text{PV Profit}_t = \frac{(P_t - L_t - E_t) \times \text{Cum. Persistency}_t}{\text{Discount Factor}_t}$$

- Year 1: $\frac{(1000 - 600 - 420) \times 1.000}{1.000} = \frac{-20}{1} = -20.00$
- Year 2: $\frac{(1000 - 575 - 350) \times 0.85}{1.030} = \frac{75 \times 0.85}{1.030} = \frac{63.75}{1.030} = 61.89$
- Year 3: $\frac{(1000 - 550 - 350) \times 0.765}{1.0609} = \frac{100 \times 0.765}{1.0609} = \frac{76.50}{1.0609} = 72.11$

**PV of Premium:**

$$\text{PV Premium}_t = \frac{P_t \times \text{Cum. Persistency}_t}{\text{Discount Factor}_t}$$

- Year 1: $\frac{1000 \times 1.000}{1.000} = 1000.00$
- Year 2: $\frac{1000 \times 0.85}{1.030} = 825.24$
- Year 3: $\frac{1000 \times 0.765}{1.0609} = 721.09$

**Lifetime profit as a percentage of premium:**

$$\text{Profit \%} = \frac{\$114.00}{\$2{,}546.33} = \mathbf{4.477\%}$$

### Examiner Report

Generally speaking, the candidate pool did very well on both parts of this question.

When candidates did make mistakes, the most common ones were:

1. Only calculating the lifetime value of the expected total profit but not calculating the expected present value of premium (the denominator for the final ratio).
2. Not applying cumulative persistency to the expected premium (denominator).
3. Incorrect discounting (for example, multiplying by 0.97 in year 2 instead of dividing by 1.03).
4. Mathematical errors (with credit given for the remainder of Part a in situations where the correct answer would have been calculated without the math error).

## Part b (0.5 points)

Identify two considerations used in the analysis in part a above that differ from standard actuarial ratemaking techniques.

### Answer

1. The use of multiple policy years (the "lifetime" perspective) — standard ratemaking considers only the period during which rates will be in effect, not the customer's expected lifetime with the insurer.
2. The use of persistency (probability of renewal/retention) — standard ratemaking does not typically model the probability that a policyholder will renew.

### Explanation

Standard actuarial ratemaking focuses on achieving rate adequacy for a single prospective policy period. Lifetime value analysis differs in two fundamental ways:

**Consideration 1 — Multi-year (lifetime) horizon:** Standard ratemaking targets the period rates will be in effect (typically one year). Lifetime value analysis aggregates profit and premium across all future policy years, discounting to present value.

**Consideration 2 — Persistency/retention modeling:** Standard ratemaking does not explicitly model whether policyholders will renew. Lifetime value analysis incorporates renewal probabilities to weight each future year's expected profit by the likelihood the policyholder is still with the company.

Additional differences that may receive credit include: (3) discounting cash flows to present value at an explicit discount rate; and (4) distinguishing new-business versus renewal-business expenses.

### Examiner Report

Candidates scored well on this part. Credit was typically given for the following themes:

1. The use of multiple policy years (i.e., the "lifetime" of the policy).
2. The use of persistency (i.e., "retention").
3. Reflection of discounting.
4. Differences in expenses or losses for new business versus renewal business.
