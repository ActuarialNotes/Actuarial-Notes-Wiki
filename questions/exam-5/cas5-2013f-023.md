The following table summarizes the results of various ultimate claim projection techniques as of December 31, 2012 in total for all accident years:

| Technique | Ultimate Claims |
|---|---|
| Reported Development | $10,000 |
| Paid Development | $8,000 |
| Expected Loss | $8,300 |
| Reported Bornhuetter-Ferguson | $9,500 |
| Paid Bornhuetter-Ferguson | $8,100 |
| Reported Berquist-Sherman | $8,350 |
| Paid Berquist-Sherman | $8,150 |

## Part a (0.5 points)

Describe a potential operational change that explains the results above.

### Answer

**Case reserve strengthening** (or alternatively, a slowdown in claim settlement speed) is the operational change that explains the pattern.

### Explanation

The key observation is the wide divergence between reported-based and paid-based methods:

- The **Reported Development** method produces the highest ultimate ($10,000) and the **Reported BF** produces the second highest ($9,500). Both rely on reported (case-incurred) loss triangles and thus directly reflect the level of case reserves.
- The **Paid** methods (Paid Development $8,000; Paid BF $8,100) produce consistently lower ultimates, as does the **Expected Loss** method ($8,300).
- Critically, the **Berquist-Sherman** adjustments bring the reported-based estimate ($8,350) close to the paid-based estimates — the Reported B-S ($8,350) is near the Paid B-S ($8,150) and the Expected Loss ($8,300). The Berquist-Sherman method adjusts for changes in case reserve adequacy; after this adjustment, the divergence largely disappears.

This pattern is consistent with **strengthening of case reserves**: case reserves have been increased without a corresponding increase in ultimate losses. The reported development method interprets the higher case reserves as a sign of increasing ultimate costs, but the Berquist-Sherman adjustment removes this distortion, aligning reported-based estimates with paid-based estimates.

An alternative explanation is a **slowdown in claim settlement** (claims are being closed more slowly), which would inflate case outstanding balances and similarly cause the reported methods to overstate ultimates relative to paid methods.

### Examiner Report

The majority of candidates scored well on this part. The required answer is case reserve strengthening (with a slowdown in claim settlement being an acceptable alternative), supported by reasoning from the data. Common errors included:

- Stating "change in reserve adequacy" without clear rationale connecting it to the specific pattern of results in the table.
- Stating "all reported ultimates are higher than paid ultimates" without distinguishing that the Berquist-Sherman adjustment is the critical element that narrows the gap.

## Part b (0.75 points)

Identify three questions that the reserving actuary should ask the claims department to better understand the impact of the operational change identified in part a above on the unpaid claims estimate.

### Answer

Three questions to ask the claims department:

1. **Has there been a change in the case outstanding reserving philosophy or approach?** (e.g., was there a directive to set initial reserves more conservatively, or was a new reserve adequacy target adopted?)

2. **Has there been any change to the claims processing system or how automatic case reserve formulas are applied?** (e.g., an upgrade to the claims management system that recalculates reserves differently)

3. **Has there been a change in the composition or experience level of the claims adjusting staff, or a change in the focus on settling small versus large claims?** (e.g., did the company hire more experienced adjusters who set higher initial reserves, or is there a new strategy to prioritize large-claim resolution?)

### Explanation

These questions are designed to distinguish between:
- A **permanent change** in reserving philosophy (which may require a structural adjustment to the development method going forward)
- A **one-time step change** in reserve adequacy (which may need to be stripped out of the reported triangle)
- **External factors** affecting settlement speed

Understanding the source and timing of the change determines whether historical development factors remain valid and whether a Berquist-Sherman adjustment is appropriate.

### Examiner Report

The majority of candidates scored well on this part. Common errors included:

- Simply repeating the operational change from Part a as a "question."
- Asking questions unrelated to the identified operational change.
- Asking whether a recent large loss was reported but not yet paid — this was not accepted because the Reported Berquist-Sherman method would not have converged to the paid methods if a single large unreported-but-reserved claim were the cause.
- Repeating similar questions with only minor alterations (all three questions should be substantively distinct).

## Part c (0.75 points)

Briefly define three diagnostic tools that can be used to test the reasonability of ultimate claim selections.

### Answer

Three diagnostic tools for testing the reasonability of ultimate claim selections:

1. **Implied average frequency** — compute projected ultimate claim counts divided by earned exposures for each accident year. If the ultimate selections are reasonable, the implied frequency should be stable across accident years or show a consistent trend. Erratic movements may indicate unreasonable ultimate selections.

2. **Implied average severity** — compute projected ultimate claims divided by projected ultimate claim counts for each accident year. Severity should show a consistent trend consistent with historical patterns and external benchmarks. A sudden jump may signal that the ultimates are overstated.

3. **Projected loss ratios** — compute projected ultimate claims divided by earned premium. Compare across accident years and against pricing/expected loss ratios. Significant unexplained deviations from expected loss ratios or prior year projections suggest the selections need review.

### Explanation

Each diagnostic provides a different lens on the reasonability of the ultimate:
- Frequency and severity diagnostics decompose the ultimate into its components, making it easier to identify whether any anomaly is driven by claim count changes, severity changes, or both.
- Loss ratio diagnostics anchor the ultimate to the underwriting price adequacy, allowing comparison with expected and historical experience.

If case reserves have been strengthened (as in Part a), ultimates from the reported development method will embed the reserve strengthening. The diagnostics above should show whether the implied frequency, severity, or loss ratio is consistent with trend — if not, the Berquist-Sherman adjusted or paid-based ultimates may be more appropriate selections.

### Examiner Report

The majority of candidates scored very poorly on this part. Most candidates attempted to answer as if Part c were a continuation of Parts a and b, explaining aspects of the operational change identified earlier rather than defining diagnostic tools for ultimate reasonability. Common incorrect answers included disposal rates, statistics related to paid or reported claims, and case reserve counts — these do not provide direct diagnostics of ultimate claim reasonability. The three diagnostics expected were along the lines of implied average frequency, implied average severity, and projected loss ratios (or monitoring of expected versus actual claim emergence).
