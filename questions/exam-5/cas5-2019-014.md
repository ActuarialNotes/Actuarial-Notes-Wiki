Given the following data for an insurance company evaluated as of December 31, 2018:

| Accident Year | Average Case Outstanding 12 | Average Case Outstanding 24 | Average Case Outstanding 36 | Average Case Outstanding 48 |
|---------------|-----------------------------|-----------------------------|-----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| 2015 | 550 | 1,100 | 1,300 | 1,800 |
| 2016 | 575 | 1,150 | 1,500 | |
| 2017 | 600 | 1,300 | | |
| 2018 | 700 | | | |

| Accident Year | Average Reported Claims 12 | Average Reported Claims 24 | Average Reported Claims 36 | Average Reported Claims 48 |
|---------------|----------------------------|----------------------------|----------------------------|----------------------------|
| 2015 | 400 | 800 | 1,200 | 1,500 |
| 2016 | 420 | 835 | 1,250 | |
| 2017 | 440 | 875 | | |
| 2018 | 460 | | | |

- Industry average claim cost trend = 4.5%.

Fully evaluate the reasonableness of using the paid claim development technique for this company.

## Part a (1.25 points)

Fully evaluate the reasonableness of using the paid claim development technique for this company.

### Explanation
To evaluate the paid development technique, we analyze trends in both the average case outstanding and average reported claims triangles.

**Average Reported Claims — age-to-age development:**

$$12\text{-}24: \quad \frac{800}{400} = 2.00, \quad \frac{835}{420} = 1.988, \quad \frac{875}{440} = 1.989$$

$$24\text{-}36: \quad \frac{1{,}200}{800} = 1.50, \quad \frac{1{,}250}{835} = 1.497$$

$$36\text{-}48: \quad \frac{1{,}500}{1{,}200} = 1.25$$

The average reported triangle is very stable — factors are consistent across accident years and show no unusual shift in the most recent diagonal.

**Diagonal trend in average reported claims (growth from one accident year to next at 12 months):**

$$\frac{420}{400} - 1 = 5.0\%, \quad \frac{440}{420} - 1 = 4.8\%, \quad \frac{460}{440} - 1 = 4.5\%$$

Growth in average reported claims tracks closely with the 4.5% industry cost trend — consistent and stable.

**Diagonal trend in average case outstanding at 12 months:**

$$\frac{575}{550} - 1 = 4.5\%, \quad \frac{600}{575} - 1 = 4.3\%, \quad \frac{700}{600} - 1 = 16.7\%$$

There is a dramatic spike in AY 2018 average case outstanding at 12 months (16.7% growth vs. the prior trend of ~4-5%).

**Average case outstanding age-to-age development:**

$$12\text{-}24: \quad \frac{1{,}100}{550} = 2.00, \quad \frac{1{,}150}{575} = 2.00, \quad \frac{1{,}300}{600} = 2.167$$

$$24\text{-}36: \quad \frac{1{,}300}{1{,}100} = 1.182, \quad \frac{1{,}500}{1{,}150} = 1.304$$

The most recent 12-to-24 and 24-to-36 factors for case outstanding are higher, reinforcing that reserves are being set at a higher level in recent periods.

**Conclusion:** The average case outstanding has increased significantly in the latest diagonal, while average reported claims remain on trend. Because reported claims = paid claims + change in case reserves, an increase in case reserves without a corresponding increase in reported claims implies that paid claims have decreased (i.e., settlement rates have slowed). The paid development triangle will be distorted — historical paid factors reflected faster settlement, so applying them to current data will underestimate ultimate claims. The paid claim development technique is **not reasonable** for this company. The reported claim development technique is more appropriate because the reported triangle is unaffected by the change in case reserve adequacy/settlement rates.

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to diagnose the appropriateness of the paid development technique without directly observing the paid triangle. They needed to infer the effect on paid claims from the observed patterns in average reported and average case outstanding data.

The key insight is: Reported = Paid + Change in Case Reserves. Because average case outstanding increased sharply in the latest diagonal while average reported remained stable, paid claims must have decreased (slower settlement). Applying historical paid LDFs would underestimate ultimate claims.

Common mistakes included:

- Attempting to derive the paid triangle directly, which was impossible without knowing claim counts separately for paid and reported — average paid and average reported triangles have different denominators.
- Misdiagnosing the pattern as a simple increase in case reserve adequacy (which would cause average reported to increase above trend, not remain stable).
- Confusion over average case outstanding being numerically larger than average reported — this is common in average triangles because small claims close early, leaving larger claims open to drive up the average case outstanding.
