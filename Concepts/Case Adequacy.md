**Case Adequacy** is the degree to which individual claim (case) reserves accurately reflect the expected ultimate settlement cost of reported claims — if case reserves are set too low (inadequate), reported incurred losses will develop upward as claims mature; if set too high (redundant), they will develop downward.

- A **shift in case reserve adequacy over time** is one of the most important distortions in loss development triangles: if claims adjusters begin setting higher initial reserves in recent years, incurred loss triangles will show lower age-to-age factors in recent diagonals, causing naïve selections to understate ultimate losses
- The **Berquist-Sherman** method diagnoses case adequacy shifts by examining the ratio of average case reserves on open claims across diagonals — a systematic increase in this ratio signals strengthening (rising adequacy), while a decrease signals weakening; historical diagonals are then restated to reflect the current adequacy level
- Paid loss development is unaffected by case reserve changes and serves as a useful cross-check: if paid and incurred development methods produce diverging ultimates, a change in case adequacy is a likely explanation

> [!example]- Detecting a Case Adequacy Shift {Example}
> An insurer's average case reserve per open claim for accident year $2020$ was \$$8{,}000$ at $24$ months and \$$12{,}000$ at $36$ months. For accident year $2022$, the average case reserve at $24$ months is \$$14{,}000$.
>
> > [!answer]-
> > The jump from \$$8{,}000$ to \$$14{,}000$ at the same $24$-month maturity suggests case reserves have been **strengthened** — adjusters are now setting higher initial reserves. The incurred development triangle will appear to develop less (factors closer to $1.000$) for recent years because more of the ultimate cost is already in the case reserve. The Berquist-Sherman adjustment would restate prior years' case reserves at the \$$14{,}000$ level to produce comparable diagonals before selecting factors.
