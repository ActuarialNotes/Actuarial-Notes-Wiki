**Long tail lines** of insurance are those where claims take many years — sometimes decades — to be fully reported, litigated, and paid, resulting in high [[IBNR]] reserves and substantial uncertainty in reserve estimates.

- Common long-tail lines include medical malpractice, workers compensation (permanent total disability), general liability, product liability, and environmental/pollution coverage
- Extended reporting lags arise from delayed injury discovery (occupational disease, latent defects), complex litigation, and legislative or judicial changes that resurface old claims
- Reserve uncertainty compounds over time: small changes in tail development factors produce large dollar impacts because the cumulative LDF from current maturity to ultimate is large
- Actuaries use multiple methods — [[Chain Ladder Method|chain ladder]], [[Bornhuetter-Ferguson Method|BF]], and [[Expected Loss Method]] — and judgment-weighted selections for long-tail lines, since no single method dominates across all maturities

> [!example]- Sensitivity to Tail Factor for Long-Tail Lines {Example}
> A medical malpractice accident year has \$$3{,}000{,}000$ reported at 36 months. Two analysts select different tail factors: $3.50$ vs. $3.75$.
>
> > [!answer]-
> > $$\text{Ultimate}_A = \$3{,}000{,}000 \times 3.50 = \$10{,}500{,}000$$
> > $$\text{Ultimate}_B = \$3{,}000{,}000 \times 3.75 = \$11{,}250{,}000$$
> > A modest 7% difference in the tail factor produces a \$$750{,}000$ reserve difference — illustrating why long-tail reserve uncertainty is so significant.
