Given the following information for a line of business:

- Assume no reported claims development past 36 months.
- Annual claim severity trend = +5%.
- Paid claim development method ultimate loss estimate for accident year 2012 = $10,275,000.
- Reported claim development method ultimate loss estimate for accident year 2012 = $9,650,000.

**Cumulative Paid Claims ($000s)**

| Accident Year | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $8,100 | $8,410 | $8,500 |
| 2011 | $8,210 | $8,130 | |
| 2012 | $2,650 | | |

**Cumulative Reported Claims ($000s)**

| Accident Year | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $8,500 | $8,900 | $8,500 |
| 2011 | $8,500 | $8,150 | |
| 2012 | $3,450 | | |

**Outstanding Claim Count**

| Accident Year | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | | $1,130 | $200 |
| 2011 | | $1,190 | |
| 2012 | | | |

**Cumulative Closed Claim Count**

| Accident Year | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | 80 | 99 | 100 |
| 2011 | 79 | 97 | |
| 2012 | 40 | | |

**Cumulative Reported Claim Count**

| Accident Year | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | | 99 | 100 |
| 2011 | 79 | 97 | |
| 2012 | 82 | | |

Fully discuss the considerations in deciding between using the paid or the reported claim development method to estimate ultimate claims for this line of business, and recommend an ultimate loss estimate for accident year 2012.

### Answer

**Select the Reported Development Method.** Recommended ultimate for AY 2012 = **$9,650,000** (the reported development method ultimate).

### Explanation

To decide between the paid and reported development methods, we calculate diagnostic statistics — average paid severity, average case outstanding, average reported severity, and the closed-to-reported ratio — and compare trends to the known 5% severity trend.

**Diagnostic 1: Average Paid Severity** = Cumulative Paid Claims / Cumulative Closed Counts

| AY | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $8,100/80 = $101.25k | $8,410/99 = $84.95k | $8,500/100 = $85.00k |
| 2011 | $8,210/79 = $103.92k | $8,130/97 = $83.81k | |
| 2012 | $2,650/40 = $66.25k | | |

Year-to-year change at 12 months: $(103.92 - 101.25)/101.25 \approx +2.6\%$ — trending well below the 5% severity trend.

Year-to-year change at 24 months: $(83.81 - 84.95)/84.95 \approx -1.3\%$ — below 5%, and actually declining.

This suggests that more small (lower-severity) claims are being closed, consistent with a speed-up in settlement pattern.

**Diagnostic 2: Closed-to-Reported Count Ratio** = Cumulative Closed Counts / Cumulative Reported Counts

| AY | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | 80/? | 99/99 = 1.00 (≈ 0.99) | 100/100 = 1.00 |
| 2011 | 79/79 = 1.00 | 97/97 = 1.00 | |
| 2012 | 40/82 = 0.488 | | |

The ratio at 12 months for AY 2012 is 0.488, higher than implied by AY 2010 and AY 2011 at 12 months (roughly 0.44–0.45 based on the pattern). The closed counts appear to be accelerating relative to reported counts, indicating a **speed-up in claim settlement**.

**Diagnostic 3: Average Case Outstanding** = (Reported − Paid) / Open Counts, where Open = Reported Count − Closed Count

Average case outstanding at 24 months:

- AY 2010: Reported count open = 99 − 99 = 0... (using outstanding count given: 1,130 open claims)

$$\text{Avg Case}_{2010,24} = \frac{(8{,}900 - 8{,}410)}{1{,}130/1{,}000} \approx \frac{490}{1.13} \approx \$433k \text{ (using counts in 000s)}$$

Alternatively, using the outstanding claim counts directly from the table:

$$\text{Avg Case}_{2010,24} = \frac{8{,}900 - 8{,}410}{1.13} \approx \$433k$$

$$\text{Avg Case}_{2011,24} = \frac{8{,}150 - 8{,}130}{1.19} \approx \$16.8k$$

The drop in average case outstanding from AY 2010 to AY 2011 at 24 months is significant, consistent with settling more and larger claims early (reducing the open backlog).

**Diagnostic 4: Average Reported Severity** = Cumulative Reported / Reported Count

| AY | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $8,500/? | $8,900/99 = $89.90k | $8,500/100 = $85.00k |
| 2011 | $8,500/79 = $107.59k | $8,150/97 = $84.02k | |
| 2012 | $3,450/82 = $42.07k | | |

Year-to-year changes at 24 months: $(84.02 - 89.90)/89.90 \approx -6.5\%$ — below trend (though the mix shift and settlement changes cloud interpretation).

**Conclusion and Recommendation**

The diagnostics show a consistent picture:
1. **Average paid severity is trending below the known 5% severity trend** — more small claims are being settled quickly.
2. **The closed-to-reported ratio is increasing** — claim settlement is accelerating.
3. **Average reported severity trends are more stable** than average paid severity.

The speed-up in settlement means that the paid development method's historical CDFs are being applied to a paid amount that includes a larger-than-historical proportion of small claims, causing the method to overestimate ultimate paid losses ($10,275,000). The reported development method is less affected by changes in settlement speed because both paid amounts and case reserves are included in reported losses.

**Recommendation: Use the reported claim development method ultimate of $9,650,000 for AY 2012.**

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to evaluate Average Paid (and/or Outstanding) and Average Reported trends and compare them to the known severity trend of 5%. They should have noticed the increase in paid settlement speed and that reported trends matched the 5% severity. From there they were to conclude to use the reported method and not the paid. This conclusion should have been reached by evaluating changes (or lack of change) in both case adequacy and settlement rates.

Many candidates calculated Average Paid and Average Case severities, but did not calculate the Average Reported severities. Most candidates did calculate trend from year to year. Many of those lost credit by not making any statement on the stability or instability of the resulting trends. Comparisons of the observed paid severity to the outstanding severity, or comparisons along the diagonal rather than down the columns of the triangle, did not receive full credit.

Many candidates that only looked at average paid and case decided the change in trend of the case outstanding disproved using the reported method. But case alone is inconclusive in determining reported stability. Many did not test for settlement rate changes.

Full credit for the selection of the reported method was given if the correct choice was made or even if the candidate wrote "select the reported method" with no numerical choice. If the candidate mistook the reported ultimate for incurred and then applied an LDF, or created their own LDF instead of using the given ultimate, full credit was still awarded. If they adjusted the reported triangle using a Berquist-Sherman or other methodology and then developed to ultimate, no credit was given for selecting the reported method. The question asked the candidates to choose between the paid and reported methods; candidates choosing an average of them did not receive full credit.
