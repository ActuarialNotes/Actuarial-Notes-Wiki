**The expected loss method** estimates ultimate losses purely from an a priori expected loss ratio (ELR) applied to earned premium, giving zero weight to actual reported losses regardless of what has emerged.

> $$\text{Ultimate Losses} = ELR \times \text{Earned Premium}$$

- Best suited for very immature accident years where actual emergence is too thin to be credible; also useful as a sanity check against development methods
- Represents one extreme of the [[Bornhuetter-Ferguson Method|Bornhuetter-Ferguson]] spectrum: 100% weight on expected losses, 0% on actual reported losses
- The [[Chain Ladder Method|chain ladder]] is the opposite extreme — 100% weight on actual reported losses; BF blends the two proportionally
- The ELR is typically derived from prior years' experience, pricing indications, or industry benchmarks

> [!example]- Applying the Expected Loss Method {Example}
> An accident year is only 3 months old. Earned premium is \$$2{,}000{,}000$ and the selected ELR is $65\%$. Actual reported losses are \$$50{,}000$. Estimate ultimate losses.
>
> > [!answer]-
> > $$\text{Ultimate} = 0.65 \times \$2{,}000{,}000 = \$1{,}300{,}000$$
> > The \$$50{,}000$ of actual emergence is ignored entirely — too immature to be credible at 3 months.
