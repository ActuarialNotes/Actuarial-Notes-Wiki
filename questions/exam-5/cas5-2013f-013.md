The chief underwriter of a company offering homeowners insurance informs the chief actuary that an analysis has been performed on the replacement costs of all properties in the book of business. The book of business contains only two territories, A and B.

The analysis indicates that for the past 10 years, all properties in territory A have been uniformly underinsured by 20% while all properties in territory B have been adequately insured. The current insurance contract at the insurance company does not include any guaranteed cost replacement endorsement or coinsurance clause. The chief actuary is aware that the rating structure has been fully reviewed in the past year.

Discuss the overall premium adequacy, territorial premium adequacy, and the premium equity among the insureds.

### Answer

**Overall Premium Adequacy**

Because the rating structure has been fully reviewed in the past year, the rates currently charged should reflect the actual loss experience of the book — including the systematic underinsurance in Territory A. Since Territory A has been uniformly underinsured by 20% for 10 years, the historical losses used to set rates were generated on underinsured properties; those loss costs (and thus the corresponding rates) are already calibrated to what actually gets paid under the contract. Without a guaranteed replacement cost endorsement or coinsurance clause, the company pays only up to the insured value — the insured bears the shortfall. Therefore, the overall premium level should be adequate, because the premiums reflect the losses actually incurred under the contract terms.

**Territorial Premium Adequacy**

- **Territory A:** Rates should be adequate for Territory A on a stand-alone basis. All properties have been uniformly underinsured by 20%, so the loss experience embedded in the rates already reflects claims paid on these underinsured homes. The territorial rate factor for A implicitly captures this lower insurance-to-value environment. However, if the recent rating review assumed full insured value and did not properly account for the systematic underinsurance, Territory A rates may be inadequate.

- **Territory B:** Rates for Territory B should also be adequate, since properties are fully insured and the rating structure was recently reviewed against actual experience.

*(An alternative defensible view — as illustrated in sample answer 2 from the examiner report — is that Territory A premiums are inadequate because policyholders there are underinsured relative to true replacement cost, meaning rate increases in Territory A and potential rate decreases in Territory B are warranted.)*

**Premium Equity Among Insureds**

Because properties in Territory A are uniformly underinsured, all Territory A policyholders are in the same position relative to each other — there is equity within Territory A. The territory differential in the rating structure should, if properly calibrated to actual loss costs, account for the lower expected losses associated with underinsured properties. If that calibration is correct, then Territory A and Territory B insureds are charged premiums commensurate with their respective expected losses under the contract, and premium equity is maintained across territories.

However, if the territorial differential does not fully reflect the loss cost differences arising from the difference in insurance-to-value, then Territory B policyholders would effectively be subsidizing Territory A policyholders — an inequitable outcome. Additionally, within Territory A, any individual who happened to be adequately insured would be paying a premium that is too low for their risk, since they would have larger expected claim payouts than uniformly underinsured neighbors, yet be charged the same territorial rate.

### Examiner Report

Many candidates did not discuss the impact of the rating structure review on overall and territorial premium adequacy. Partial credit was given for stating overall premium inadequacy due to the underinsurance in Territory A. Candidates also struggled with the equity discussion — many confused equity (each insured paying a premium proportional to their expected loss) with equality (everyone paying the same premium). Several candidates did not recognize the potential loss cost differential between territories and the resulting cross-subsidization or unfairness.
