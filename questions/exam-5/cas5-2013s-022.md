An actuary is assisting a manufacturing company in reserving its self-insured workers compensation program as of December 31, 2012. The program began on January 1, 1998 and has undergone the following changes in recent years:

- On January 1, 2007, the per-occurrence retention was increased from $300,000 to $750,000.
- On January 1, 2010, the company automated some of its production process. As a result, the company replaced a significant portion of its assembly-line staff with sales staff.

The actuary would like to use the following methods and data to estimate ultimate claims as of December 31, 2012:

- Development method using company-specific claim development triangles.
- Expected claims method using payroll as exposure base and the average of the reported and paid claim development projections as initial estimates of ultimate claims.
- Frequency-severity method using company-specific claim count development triangles.

## Part a (1 point)

Discuss necessary adjustments the actuary should make to the company-specific data to use the development method.

### Answer

The actuary must address two major data distortions before applying the development method:

**1. Retention change (effective January 1, 2007):**

The increase in per-occurrence retention from $300,000 to $750,000 fundamentally changed the nature of the claims data. Before 2007, the company was only retaining losses up to $300,000 per occurrence; after 2007, it retains losses up to $750,000. This means:

- Claims from accident years prior to 2007 are capped at $300,000 per occurrence.
- Claims from accident years 2007 and later are capped at $750,000 per occurrence.

The historical development triangle combines two different data series, which will distort the age-to-age development factors. The actuary should **restate the historical triangles** on a consistent retention basis. Ideally, two separate triangles should be constructed:
  - One restated to the $300,000 retention level (using loss limitation/excess factors to reduce post-2007 data)
  - One restated to the $750,000 retention level (using increased limits factors or ILFs to adjust pre-2007 data upward)

The actuary should then apply LDFs selected from the appropriate triangle to each accident year based on the applicable retention.

**2. Change in workforce composition (effective January 1, 2010):**

The replacement of assembly-line staff with sales staff in 2010 materially changed the risk profile. Assembly-line workers have significantly higher claim frequency and likely higher severity than sales staff. Therefore:

- Accident years prior to 2010 reflect the riskier assembly-line workforce.
- Accident years 2010 and later reflect the lower-risk sales workforce.

The actuary should separately analyze the pre-2010 and post-2010 data, using post-2010 development patterns for projecting the current accident years. Alternatively, the actuary may need to adjust the pre-2010 data to reflect the current risk profile before selecting development factors to apply.

**Summary:** The company-specific triangle contains data with at least two structural breaks (2007 retention change, 2010 workforce change). Development factors selected naively from the combined historical data will be biased and should not be applied without appropriate adjustments.

### Examiner Report

Many candidates did not include a detailed discussion of how the changes in retention and/or risk profile would affect the data. Some candidates did not recognize that the actuary was working for a self-insured client and not an insurance company; in these cases, some candidates said premium should be adjusted to current rate level, but the actuary would not have premium to use as an exposure base for the self-insured layer. Candidates needed to recognize both the retention change and the workforce change and explain how each would distort the development triangle.

## Part b (1 point)

Briefly describe four adjustments the actuary should consider making to historical claims and exposures to put them on current levels in the expected claims method.

### Answer

The following four adjustments should be considered:

**1. Adjust losses for the change in per-occurrence retention level:**
Historical claims (pre-2007) were capped at $300,000 per occurrence. To put these on the current $750,000 retention level, the actuary should apply increased limits factors (ILFs) or loss limitation factors (LLFs) to restate the pre-2007 claims to a $750,000 retention basis. This removes the distortion caused by the retention change so that historical and current claims reflect the same layer of loss.

**2. Adjust losses for the change in workforce composition:**
The 2010 shift from assembly-line workers (higher frequency, higher severity) to sales staff (lower frequency, lower severity) means pre-2010 claims overstate expected future losses. The actuary should adjust historical losses downward (or apply a risk profile adjustment factor) to reflect the current lower-risk workforce mix.

**3. Adjust exposures for payroll inflation/trend:**
Payroll (the exposure base) changes over time due to wage inflation and changes in headcount. The actuary should trend historical payrolls to current levels using an appropriate payroll trend factor (e.g., wage inflation indices) so that all years' expected losses per unit of payroll are comparable.

**4. Adjust losses for benefit level changes (workers compensation benefit trend):**
Workers compensation benefit levels change over time due to statutory changes and wage-related factors (since indemnity benefits are often tied to wages). The actuary should apply loss trend factors or benefit level adjustment factors to bring historical losses to the current benefit/wage level, ensuring the initial expected loss ratios (loss per unit of payroll) reflect current conditions.

### Examiner Report

Again, some candidates said premium should be adjusted to current rate level; however the actuary in the question would not have access to premium information for the self-insured layer. Acceptable adjustments included: (1) adjusting to current retention level using ILFs or loss limitation factors; (2) adjusting for the change in type of workers (assembly vs. sales); (3) adjusting exposures for payroll inflation; (4) adjusting losses for benefit level changes or loss trend; and (5) adjusting for the large loss loads associated with the retention change. Candidates needed to identify four distinct adjustments for full credit.

## Part c (1 point)

Describe two diagnostic tests the actuary should perform before using the frequency-severity method.

### Answer

**Diagnostic Test 1: Average Severity Triangle (Paid Losses / Closed Claim Counts)**

Construct a triangle of average paid severity by accident year and development age. Examine this triangle for:

- Consistent year-over-year trends consistent with known severity trends (e.g., wage inflation for indemnity, medical inflation for medical losses)
- Changes in the level of average severity around 2007 (retention increase from $300k to $750k would increase average severity significantly for the same claims, since larger claims are now retained instead of ceded)
- Changes in average severity around 2010 (shift from assembly-line to sales staff may reduce severity if assembly-line injuries tend to be more severe)

If the severity triangle shows a jump in 2007 or a level shift in 2010, the actuary will need to account for these structural changes before applying the frequency-severity method.

**Diagnostic Test 2: Frequency Triangle (Claim Counts / Payroll Exposure)**

Construct a triangle of claim frequency (reported or ultimate claim counts per $100 of payroll) by accident year. Examine this triangle for:

- Consistent year-over-year frequency trends
- A significant decrease in frequency around 2010, consistent with the shift from assembly-line (high injury rate) to sales staff (low injury rate)

If the frequency data shows a structural break in 2010 (lower frequency), the actuary should use only post-2010 data to project future claim frequency, since pre-2010 data reflects a higher-risk workforce that is no longer representative. Similarly, the actuary should check whether the retention change in 2007 affected the claim count data (e.g., if smaller claims below $300k were previously handled differently or not reported under the old retention program).

### Examiner Report

Some candidates discussed the need to review the data for changes in frequency and severity, but failed to identify specific diagnostics that could be used to test for the changes. Full credit required identifying concrete diagnostic tests (such as average severity triangles, frequency triangles, paid-to-reported ratios, closed-to-reported ratios) along with an explanation of what pattern the actuary would look for and how that pattern would relate to the known data changes (retention increase in 2007, workforce change in 2010). Simply stating "look at frequency and severity" without describing what to look for in those diagnostics did not receive full credit.
