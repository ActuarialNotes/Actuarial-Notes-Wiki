When aggregating data for ratemaking purposes, two of the three general objectives are:

1. Accurately match losses and premiums for the policy.
2. Use the most recent data available.

Briefly discuss how well each of the following data aggregation methods achieves these two objectives.

## Part a (0.5 points)

**Calendar year**

### Explanation

**Matching losses and premiums:** Calendar year does a poor job of matching losses and premiums. Premiums are recognized when transactions occur (written) or earned within the calendar year, while losses are included based on accident or transaction date, regardless of when the underlying policy was written. Losses from policies written in a prior year can be matched with premiums from policies written in the current year, creating a mismatch between the premium and loss base.

**Using the most recent data:** Calendar year data is the most responsive. The data is fixed and complete at the end of the calendar year with no further development, so it is immediately available and uses the most current information. There is no need to wait for loss development.

### Examiner Report

The majority of candidates provided comprehensive answers for this part.

## Part b (0.5 points)

**Calendar/accident year**

### Explanation

**Matching losses and premiums:** Calendar/accident year does a better job of matching than pure calendar year, but a mismatch remains. Losses are grouped by the accident date (all accidents occurring in a given calendar year), which links losses more closely to the policies in force during that year. However, the premium is still based on calendar-year transactions and is not restricted to policies that generated the accident-year losses. The match is better than calendar year but not perfect.

**Using the most recent data:** Calendar/accident year data is not fixed at the end of the accident year because losses continue to develop (IBNR, IBNER). The actuary must wait for or project additional development, which means the data is not as immediately available as calendar-year data, though it is still more timely than policy year.

### Examiner Report

Some candidates struggled to articulate how the calendar/accident year aggregation method met the objective of matching premium to loss.

## Part c (0.5 points)

**Policy year**

### Explanation

**Matching losses and premiums:** Policy year provides the best match between premiums and losses. All premiums and all losses for policies written during a given policy year are grouped together regardless of when they are earned or when losses occur. This creates a perfect correspondence between the loss base and the premium base, since all items relate to the same cohort of policies.

**Using the most recent data:** Policy year data is the least responsive. For annual policies, a policy year does not close until policies written at the end of the year expire (up to two calendar years of data collection), and losses continue to develop thereafter. It can take many years for policy-year data to fully develop, making it the least current of the three methods.

### Examiner Report

The majority of candidates provided comprehensive answers for this part. Candidates generally answered this question correctly overall. Some candidates did not provide any explanation of why or how the data aggregation method achieved the objective and in those cases were not afforded full credit.
