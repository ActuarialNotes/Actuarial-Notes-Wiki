**Gross Losses** are an insurer's losses before any reinsurance cessions or other recoveries are removed — the full amount of claims the insurer is liable for on a direct basis. Gross is the starting point from which [[Ceded Losses|ceded]], recovery, and [[Net Losses|net]] amounts are derived.

> $$\text{Gross Losses}$$
>
> $$= \text{Net Losses} + \text{Ceded Losses} + \text{Other Recoveries}$$

- Reserve analyses are typically performed on **gross losses first** because the gross book is the largest and most homogeneous data set; ceded and net are then computed, often as gross minus the other.
- "Gross" most often means *gross of reinsurance*. Be careful with terminology: losses may also be quoted gross or net of [[Salvage and Subrogation]] and of deductible recoveries — always confirm which recoveries the "gross" figure excludes.
- Gross and ceded losses develop on **different patterns**: excess cessions respond only to large claims, which report and settle more slowly, so the ceded triangle cannot simply be scaled from gross (except under quota share).
- For ratemaking, choosing a gross vs. net basis depends on whether reinsurance cost is treated as a separate provision; net ratemaking reflects retained risk but imports the reinsurer's pricing.

> [!example]- Reconciling Gross, Ceded, and Net {Example}
> AY 2024 net retained losses $= \$800{,}000$. Excess-of-loss reinsurance recoverable (ceded) $= \$300{,}000$. Salvage and subrogation recovered $= \$50{,}000$.
>
> > [!answer]-
> > Gross of reinsurance only $= \text{Net} + \text{Ceded} = 800{,}000 + 300{,}000 = \$1{,}100{,}000$
> >
> > Gross of all recoveries $= 800{,}000 + 300{,}000 + 50{,}000 = \$1{,}150{,}000$
> >
> > The \$$50{,}000$ gap shows why the recovery basis must be stated: the same book is "\$1.10M gross of reinsurance" or "\$1.15M gross of all recoveries."
