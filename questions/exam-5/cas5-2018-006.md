Two methods of deriving expense provisions in ratemaking include the premium-based projection method and the exposure-based projection method.

## Part a (1 point)

For each method, briefly describe how both fixed and variable expenses are treated.

### Explanation

**Premium-Based Projection Method**

Total expenses are first separated into fixed and variable components.

- **Variable expenses**: Variable expenses are divided by either written premium (if incurred at policy inception) or earned premium (if incurred throughout the policy period) to produce a variable expense ratio to premium.
- **Fixed expenses**: Fixed expenses are divided by written or earned premium to produce a fixed expense ratio to premium. Because fixed expenses do not vary proportionally with premium, a trend may need to be applied if fixed expenses and premium are trending at different rates.

**Exposure-Based Projection Method**

Total expenses are again separated into fixed and variable components.

- **Variable expenses**: Treated identically to the premium-based method — divided by written or earned premium to produce a variable expense ratio.
- **Fixed expenses**: Instead of being divided by premium, fixed expenses are divided by written or earned exposures (or policy count) to produce an average fixed expense per exposure (a dollar amount rather than a percentage). A trend may be applied to project the fixed expense per exposure to future levels.

The key distinction is that in the exposure-based method, fixed costs are expressed as a dollar amount per exposure, which avoids the distortion that arises when premium changes cause fixed expense ratios to fluctuate even though the underlying fixed costs have not changed.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to distinguish fixed and variable expenses from total expenses, as well as the differences between and details of both expense provision approaches.

Common mistakes include:

- No mention of separating total expenses into fixed and variable buckets.
- Not identifying the use of exposure or policy count for the fixed expense portion of the exposure-based method.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Briefly describe one shortcoming (or distortion) of each method.

### Explanation

**Premium-Based Projection Method — Shortcoming**

Rate changes during or after the experience period distort the historical fixed expense ratios. Because fixed expenses are expressed as a percentage of premium, a rate increase reduces the apparent fixed expense ratio even if the actual dollar amount of fixed costs is unchanged. This means the method implicitly assumes fixed expenses grow in lockstep with premium, which is not appropriate. Additionally, high-premium policyholders are effectively charged higher fixed costs than low-premium policyholders, even though true fixed costs do not vary by risk.

**Exposure-Based Projection Method — Shortcoming**

If the book of business is growing or shrinking, economies of scale may cause the actual fixed expense per exposure to change over time in ways that distort projections. In addition, some costs that are classified as "fixed" actually vary by risk characteristics (e.g., new business vs. renewal business have different acquisition costs), so allocating a uniform fixed expense per exposure to all classes may produce distorted class-level provisions.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to understand and describe a potential shortcoming or distortion of each method.

Common mistakes include:

**Premium-based method**
- Stating a need for on-level premium without explaining why.

**Exposure-based method**
- Stating simply that exposures must be clearly defined or are not readily available.
- Stating that exposures are difficult to estimate or subject to change.
