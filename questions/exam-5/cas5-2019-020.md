Given the following information as of December 31, 2018:

**Incremental Closed Claim Counts as of (months)**

| Accident Year | 72 | 84 | 96 |
|---------------|----|----|-----|
| 2011 | 141 | 81 | 13 |
| 2012 | 145 | 61 | |
| 2013 | 59 | | |

**Incremental Paid Claims (\$000s) as of (months)**

| Accident Year | 72 | 84 | 96 |
|---------------|-----|------|------|
| 2011 | 7,600 | 6,100 | 2,400 |
| 2012 | 8,800 | 3,900 | |
| 2013 | 5,600 | | |

| | |
|---|---|
| 6% | Annual severity trend |
| 20% | Reduction in claim costs from legislative change for claims occurring after January 1, 2012 |

## Part a (1.5 points)

Estimate the trended tail severity for age 84 and older at 2018 cost levels.

### Answer
101.66

### Explanation
The tail severity covers all incremental development at 84 months and older. Available data:
- AY 2011 at 84 months: 81 claims, \$6,100K paid
- AY 2011 at 96 months: 13 claims, \$2,400K paid
- AY 2012 at 84 months: 61 claims, \$3,900K paid

**Adjustments needed:**

1. **Legislative change**: The 20% reduction in claim costs applies to claims occurring **after January 1, 2012**. AY 2011 claims occurred before this date, so AY 2011 data must be multiplied by 0.8 to reflect the expected lower cost level going forward. AY 2012 and later claims are already at the post-reform cost level.

2. **Severity trend**: Trend to 2018 cost levels using 6% annual severity trend. Since accident years are measured from their midpoint (approximately July 1 of each year), the trend period for AY 2011 is approximately 7 years (mid-2011 to mid-2018) and for AY 2012 is approximately 6 years.

**Adjusted incremental paid claims (\$000s):**

$$\text{AY 2011, age 84+} = (6{,}100 + 2{,}400) \times 1.06^7 \times 0.8 = 8{,}500 \times 1.50363 \times 0.8 = 10{,}225$$

$$\text{AY 2012, age 84} = 3{,}900 \times 1.06^6 = 3{,}900 \times 1.41852 = 5{,}532$$

**Total adjusted claims and claim counts:**

$$\text{Total adjusted paid} = 10{,}225 + 5{,}532 = 15{,}757 \text{ (\$000s)}$$

$$\text{Total claim count} = 81 + 13 + 61 = 155$$

**Tail severity (in \$000s):**

$$\text{Tail Severity} = \frac{10{,}225 + 5{,}532}{155} = \frac{15{,}757}{155} \approx 101.66 \text{ (\$000s)}$$

Equivalently, computing severity per claim by accident year/period before pooling:

$$\text{Tail Severity} = \frac{(6{,}100 + 2{,}400) \times 1.06^7 \times 0.8 + 3{,}900 \times 1.06^6}{81 + 13 + 61} = 101.66$$

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to calculate a tail severity for maturity age 84 and older by pooling the experience of the relevant older development periods, adjust 2011 incremental paid claims for the legislative change and trend to 2018 cost level.

Common mistakes included:
- Applying the legislative change factor to the wrong years, or not applying it at all
- Not determining the proper trend period
- Correctly determining severity by accident year/development period but failing to properly weight them to provide a meaningful estimate
- Calculating the trended tail severity for age 72 and older rather than 84 and older

## Part b (0.5 points)

Briefly describe two considerations when choosing the maturity age of the tail severity.

### Explanation
**Consideration 1: Data stability/credibility at older ages**

The maturity age should be chosen at a point where the data becomes erratic or sparse. As development ages increase, the number of remaining open claims shrinks and the data becomes less reliable. The tail should begin where age-to-age factors become unstable, so that the pooled older data provides a more stable and credible estimate than individual annual factors.

**Consideration 2: Volume of data moved into the tail versus available for explicit factors**

The actuary should consider the overall impact on total projections. If the maturity age is set too late, there may not be enough data in the tail to produce a credible tail severity. If set too early, potentially reliable individual age-to-age development factors are discarded in favor of the pooled tail estimate. There is also a trade-off with the availability of industry data: if industry data is available at a particular maturity age, aligning the company's tail cutoff with that age makes it easier to supplement sparse internal data.

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to provide two distinct relevant elements to consider when choosing the maturity age of the tail severity and to explain their reasoning.

Common mistakes included:
- Repeating the same statement twice but phrased differently (e.g., "point at which data becomes volatile" and "point at which data becomes erratic" — these are the same consideration)
- Blanket statements such as "judgment of the actuary"
- Stating the tail should start when development factors are close to 1
- Not providing sufficient explanation (e.g., simply stating "credibility of data" without identifying which data elements needed to be credible and why)
