Given the following information:

| Accident Year | Earned Premium | Expected Claim Ratio | Reported Claims | Reported CDF to Ultimate |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $19,800 | 50% | $6,900 | 1.400 |
| 2011 | $18,900 | 50% | $5,800 | 1.700 |
| 2012 | $21,200 | 50% | $3,200 | 3.100 |

## Part a (1.75 points)

Estimate the unpaid claim amount as of December 31, 2012 using the Benktander technique for accident years 2010 through 2012.

### Answer

**Total unpaid claims = $13,802**

**Benktander Method — Step 1: Compute the BF Ultimate (first iteration)**

Expected ultimate claims (a priori):
$$Q = EP \times ECR$$

Reported percentage:
$$\%\text{Reported} = \frac{1}{\text{CDF}}$$

Unreported percentage:
$$\%\text{Unreported} = 1 - \frac{1}{\text{CDF}}$$

BF Ultimate = Reported Claims + (Expected Ultimate × % Unreported)

| AY | EP | ECR | Expected Ult | Reported | CDF | % Unrptd | BF Ultimate |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | 19,800 | 50% | 9,900 | 6,900 | 1.400 | 28.57% | 6,900 + 9,900 × 0.2857 = **9,729** |
| 2011 | 18,900 | 50% | 9,450 | 5,800 | 1.700 | 41.18% | 5,800 + 9,450 × 0.4118 = **9,692** |
| 2012 | 21,200 | 50% | 10,600 | 3,200 | 3.100 | 67.74% | 3,200 + 10,600 × 0.6774 = **10,381** |

**Step 2: Benktander Ultimate (second iteration)**

Benktander replaces the a priori expected ultimate with the BF Ultimate from Step 1, then applies the unreported percentage again:

$$\text{Benktander Ultimate} = \text{Reported} + \text{BF Ultimate} \times \%\text{Unreported}$$

| AY | Reported | BF Ult | % Unrptd | Benktander Ultimate | Unpaid |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | 6,900 | 9,729 | 28.57% | 6,900 + 9,729 × 0.2857 = **9,680** | 9,680 − 6,900 = **2,780** |
| 2011 | 5,800 | 9,692 | 41.18% | 5,800 + 9,692 × 0.4118 = **9,791** | 9,791 − 5,800 = **3,991** |
| 2012 | 3,200 | 10,381 | 67.74% | 3,200 + 10,381 × 0.6774 = **10,232** | 10,232 − 3,200 = **7,032** |

$$\text{Total Unpaid} = 2,780 + 3,991 + 7,032 = \mathbf{13,803}$$

*(Minor rounding differences from $13,802 are acceptable.)*

### Explanation

The **Benktander method** (also called the iterated BF or GB method) is a two-step credibility-weighted blend between the Bornhuetter-Ferguson (BF) method and the development (chain-ladder) method.

**Step 1 — BF Ultimate:**

$$U_{BF} = C + Q \cdot \left(1 - \frac{1}{f}\right)$$

where:
- $C$ = reported claims to date
- $Q = EP \times ECR$ = a priori expected ultimate claims
- $f$ = CDF to ultimate
- $1 - 1/f$ = % unreported

**Step 2 — Benktander Ultimate:**

$$U_{GB} = C + U_{BF} \cdot \left(1 - \frac{1}{f}\right)$$

The Benktander method uses the BF ultimate as the "expected" rather than the pure a priori estimate, giving more weight to actual observed claims. This makes it a credibility-weighted blend that lies between BF and development.

**Unpaid Claims:**

$$\text{Unpaid} = U_{GB} - C$$

A majority of the candidates received full credit on this part. Some candidates applied the Bornhuetter-Ferguson method instead of the Benktander method, resulting in loss of some credit.

## Part b (0.25 points)

Estimate the unpaid claim amount as of December 31, 2012 using the Benktander technique with 1,000 iterations for accident year 2012 only.

### Answer

With 1,000 iterations, the Benktander method converges to the **development (chain-ladder) method**:

$$\text{Unpaid}_{1000\text{ iter}} = C \times (f - 1) = 3{,}200 \times (3.100 - 1) = 3{,}200 \times 2.100 = \mathbf{6{,}720}$$

### Explanation

The Benktander method can be iterated indefinitely. At each iteration $k$, the "expected" ultimate from the previous iteration replaces the prior expected ultimate:

$$U^{(k)} = C + U^{(k-1)} \cdot \left(1 - \frac{1}{f}\right)$$

As $k \to \infty$, the method converges to the pure development technique ultimate:

$$U^{(\infty)} = C \times f$$

Therefore, unpaid claims at convergence:

$$\text{Unpaid}^{(\infty)} = C \times f - C = C(f - 1) = 3{,}200 \times (3.1 - 1) = 3{,}200 \times 2.1 = 6{,}720$$

This makes intuitive sense: with enough iterations, the method places full credibility on the observed reported claims and applies the development factor directly, giving no weight to the a priori expected claim ratio.

### Examiner Report

Roughly half of the candidates received full credit on this part. With a high level of iterations, the result will converge to the development technique. If candidates did not know that, they did not receive credit on this part.
