Given the following information:

| Size of Loss | $100,000 Limit Claims | $100,000 Limit Losses | $250,000 Limit Claims | $250,000 Limit Losses | $500,000 Limit Claims | $500,000 Limit Losses |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| $X \leq \$100{,}000$ | 100 | $8,000,000 | 35 | $1,800,000 | 35 | $1,800,000 |
| $\$100{,}000 < X \leq \$250{,}000$ | | | 40 | $7,400,000 | 25 | $3,900,000 |
| $\$250{,}000 < X \leq \$500{,}000$ | | | | | 15 | $5,200,000 |

| Limit | Indicated Factor (pure premium generalized linear model analysis) |
|---|---|
| $100,000 | 1.00 |
| $250,000 | 0.95 |
| $500,000 | 1.15 |

For the $250,000 policy limit:

## Part a (1.25 points)

Calculate the indicated increased limits factor, assuming a basic limit of $100,000.

### Answer

**Indicated ILF($250,000) = 1.599**

The ILF is the ratio of the limited average severity at the higher limit to the limited average severity at the basic limit.

**Limited average severity at $100,000** — using all policies with limits $\geq \$100{,}000$ (all 270 claims):

Total claims with limit $\geq \$100{,}000$: $100 + 35 + 35 + 40 + 25 + 15 = 250$

$$\text{LAS}(\$100{,}000) = \frac{\$8{,}000{,}000 + \$1{,}800{,}000 + \$1{,}800{,}000 + (40 + 25 + 15) \times \$100{,}000}{250}$$

$$= \frac{\$8{,}000{,}000 + \$1{,}800{,}000 + \$1{,}800{,}000 + \$8{,}000{,}000}{250} = \frac{\$19{,}600{,}000}{250} = \$78{,}400$$

**Average loss in the layer $100k–$250k** — using only policies with limits $\geq \$250{,}000$ (claims from $250k and $500k policies):

Claims reaching the layer ($250k and $500k policies): $40 + 25 + 15 = 80$

$$\text{Layer losses} = \$7{,}400{,}000 - 40 \times \$100{,}000 + \$3{,}900{,}000 - 25 \times \$100{,}000 + 15 \times (\$250{,}000 - \$100{,}000)$$

$$= \$7{,}400{,}000 - \$4{,}000{,}000 + \$3{,}900{,}000 - \$2{,}500{,}000 + \$2{,}250{,}000 = \$7{,}050{,}000$$

$$\text{Average layer loss per claim (using all 250 claims in denominator)} = \frac{\$7{,}050{,}000}{250} = \$28{,}200$$

Wait — the per-exposure average layer loss uses all 250 claims (the same base as LAS at $100k):

$$\text{LAS}(\$250{,}000) = \$78{,}400 + \frac{\$7{,}050{,}000}{250} = \$78{,}400 + \$28{,}200 = \$106{,}600$$

Alternatively, treating the layer separately:

$$\text{LAS}(\$250{,}000) = \$78{,}400 + \frac{80}{250} \times \frac{\$7{,}050{,}000}{80} = \$78{,}400 + \frac{\$7{,}050{,}000}{250} = \$106{,}600$$

$$\boxed{\text{ILF}(\$250{,}000) = \frac{\$106{,}600}{\$78{,}400} \approx 1.599}$$

### Examiner Report

The majority of candidates scored well on this part. This question involved a straightforward calculation.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Explain the difference between the indicated increased limits factor calculated in part a above and the generalized linear model results.

### Answer

The traditional empirical ILF calculated in part a assumes that claim frequency is the same regardless of policy limit — it treats the loss development from each limit group as coming from a homogeneous population. The GLM, by contrast, does not assume uniform frequency across limits. It models behavioral differences among insureds who choose different policy limits (e.g., higher-limit policyholders may be more risk-averse, may have different claim-filing propensities, or may be a systematically different risk profile). These frequency/behavioral differences can cause the GLM-indicated factors to diverge from the empirically-calculated factors — and can even produce counterintuitive results such as a factor below 1.00 at an intermediate limit (here, 0.95 at $250k).

### Examiner Report

Almost half of all candidates received full credit for indicating that, unlike the traditional ILF approach, the GLM approach does not assume frequency is the same for all limits, or that the GLM approach recognizes behavioral differences among insureds at different limits. Many candidates received no credit for stating the GLM considers correlations between variables or accounts for sparse data — while potentially true, these do not explain the specific difference between the two methods in this problem.

## Part c (0.5 points)

Select an increased limits factor and briefly explain the rationale for the selection.

### Answer

**Selection: 1.599 (the empirically calculated ILF from part a)**

The GLM-indicated factor of 0.95 for the $250,000 limit is counterintuitive and unreasonable: it implies the insured should be charged less for a higher policy limit than the basic $100,000 limit. The $250,000 ILF must lie between the $100,000 factor (1.00) and the $500,000 factor (1.15 per GLM), but the GLM produces a reversal/discontinuity at $250,000. The empirical factor of 1.599 is consistent with the expected pattern that ILFs increase (at a decreasing rate) as limits rise. Therefore, the traditionally-calculated ILF of 1.599 is the more defensible selection.

*(Full credit was also awarded for selecting the GLM factor or a blend, provided the rationale addressed the behavioral/frequency differences and acknowledged the counterintuitive GLM result.)*

### Examiner Report

The majority of candidates received full credit for providing an acceptable selection with rationale. Full credit was given for selecting the traditionally-calculated ILF (citing the counterintuitive GLM reversal) or the GLM factor (citing its recognition of behavioral/frequency differences), or a reasonable blend. Partial credit was given for a reasonable selection without supporting explanation.
