Given the following information for a company that writes the same line of business in Region 1 and Region 2, as of December 31, 2018:

**Reported Claims**

| Accident Year | Region 1 | Region 2 | Combined |
|---------------|----------|----------|----------|
| 2016 | 180,000 | 200,000 | 380,000 |
| 2017 | 150,000 | 180,000 | 330,000 |
| 2018 | N/A | 180,000 | 180,000 |

**Reported Age-to-Ultimate Factors**

| Age | Region 1 | Region 2 |
|-----|----------|----------|
| 12-Ult | 2.283 | 1.558 |
| 24-Ult | 1.154 | 1.110 |

**Earned Premium**

| Calendar Year | Region 1 | Region 2 | Combined |
|---------------|----------|----------|----------|
| 2016 | 900,000 | 900,000 | 1,800,000 |
| 2017 | 1,000,000 | 1,000,000 | 2,000,000 |
| 2018 | 1,610,000 | 690,000 | 2,300,000 |

**Annual Industry Trends**

| | Region 1 | Region 2 | Combined |
|-|----------|----------|----------|
| Frequency | -2% | 0% | -1% |
| Severity | 10% | 0% | 5% |

- 45.8% / Region 2 accident year 2016 expected claims ratio
- 40.0% Combined accident year 2016 expected claims ratio
- Equal amount of business is underwritten in Regions 1 and 2 for the entire industry.
- The company is subject to the same claims trends as the industry.
- Each region is fully credible.
- There have been no rate changes in 2017 or 2018.
- There is no premium trend.

## Part a (1 point)

Estimate the ultimate claims for region 1 for accident year 2017 using the reported Bornhuetter-Ferguson technique.

### Answer
224224

### Explanation
**Step 1: Derive Region 1 ELR for 2016**

The combined ELR uses equal industry weights for Region 1 and Region 2, so:

$$\text{Combined ELR} = \frac{ELR_{R1} \times EP_{R1} + ELR_{R2} \times EP_{R2}}{EP_{R1} + EP_{R2}}$$

$$45.8\% = \frac{ELR_{R1} \times 900{,}000 + 40\% \times 900{,}000}{1{,}800{,}000}$$

$$ELR_{R1}^{2016} = 51.6\%$$

**Step 2: Trend Region 1 ELR to AY 2017**

Apply one year of frequency and severity trends:

$$ELR_{R1}^{2017} = 51.6\% \times (1 - 2\%) \times (1 + 10\%) = 51.6\% \times 0.98 \times 1.10 = 55.62\%$$

**Step 3: Apply BF formula for AY 2017 Region 1**

At 24 months of development, the reported age-to-ultimate factor is 1.154:

$$\% \text{ unreported} = 1 - \frac{1}{LDF_{24-Ult}} = 1 - \frac{1}{1.154} = 13.35\%$$

$$\text{Ult. Claims} = \text{Reported} + ELR_{R1}^{2017} \times EP_{R1}^{2017} \times \left(1 - \frac{1}{LDF_{24-Ult}}\right)$$

$$= 150{,}000 + 55.62\% \times 1{,}000{,}000 \times \left(1 - \frac{1}{1.154}\right) = 150{,}000 + 74{,}224 = 224{,}224$$

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to know how to derive the individual region ELR from the combined ELR and trend it to the appropriate accident year. They were also expected to know how to use the BF formula to obtain ultimate claims.

Common mistakes included:
- Using either the combined ELR or Region 2 ELR instead of calculating the Region 1 ELR
- Not trending the selected ELR to the appropriate period/level or not properly applying the trends
- Using the wrong reported age-to-ultimate factor in the calculation
- Not properly applying the BF formula

## Part b (1.25 points)

Calculate the total ultimate claims for region 1 and region 2 for accident year 2018 using the reported Bornhuetter-Ferguson technique.

### Answer
821722

### Explanation
**Step 1: Derive Region 1 ELR at 2018 level**

Apply two years of trends to the 2016 Region 1 ELR:

$$ELR_{R1}^{2018} = 51.6\% \times (1 - 2\%)^2 \times (1 + 10\%)^2 = 51.6\% \times 0.9604 \times 1.21 = 60.0\%$$

Region 2 has zero frequency and severity trend:

$$ELR_{R2}^{2018} = 40.0\% \times (1 + 0\%)^2 \times (1 + 0\%)^2 = 40.0\%$$

**Step 2: Apply BF formula for each region at AY 2018 (12-month maturity)**

For Region 1:

$$IBNR_{R1} = ELR_{R1}^{2018} \times EP_{R1}^{2018} \times \left(1 - \frac{1}{LDF_{R1, 12-Ult}}\right) = 60\% \times 1{,}610{,}000 \times \left(1 - \frac{1}{2.283}\right) = 641{,}722$$

For Region 2:

$$IBNR_{R2} = ELR_{R2}^{2018} \times EP_{R2}^{2018} \times \left(1 - \frac{1}{LDF_{R2, 12-Ult}}\right) = 40\% \times 690{,}000 \times \left(1 - \frac{1}{1.558}\right) = 143{,}000$$

**Step 3: Total ultimate claims**

$$\text{Ult. Claims}_{Combined} = \text{Reported}_{Combined} + IBNR_{R1} + IBNR_{R2}$$

$$= 180{,}000 + 641{,}722 + 143{,}000 = 821{,}722 \approx 821{,}722$$

(Note: minor rounding differences across samples yield values of approximately 821,393–821,722.)

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to know how a change in an underlying assumption (i.e., mix of business) would affect the BF method. Candidates were expected to recognize that they must use data from both regions separately since they don't have the reported age-to-ultimate factor for the combined claims nor the individual reported claims for accident year 2018.

Common mistakes included:
- Using a mixture of Region 1 CDF and Region 2 CDF to obtain a combined CDF instead of using both separately
- Using inappropriate ELRs for the individual regions
- Using inappropriate premiums for the individual regions
- Not trending or not properly trending Region 1's ELR to the 2018 level
- Not properly calculating the BF IBNR for Region 1 and/or for Region 2
- Applying trends to Region 2's ELR
- Not estimating the total ultimate claims

## Part c (0.5 points)

Construct an argument against using the combined data to estimate the ultimate claims for accident year 2018.

### Explanation
The two regions have distinctly different characteristics that make combining them problematic:

1. **Different loss development patterns**: Region 1 has a 12-to-ultimate LDF of 2.283, while Region 2 has 1.558. Combining these would produce a blended factor that is inappropriate for either region's true reporting pattern.

2. **Different trends**: Region 1 has a -2% frequency trend and +10% severity trend, while Region 2 has 0% for both. Combining regions with different trends distorts the expected loss ratio.

3. **Shifting mix of business**: In 2016 and 2017 the premium split was 50/50, but in 2018 Region 1 represents 1,610,000/2,300,000 = 70% of total premium. As Region 1 grows relative to Region 2, using a combined development pattern or combined ELR derived from prior years would underestimate ultimates because Region 1 has higher expected claim ratios and slower development.

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to know in which circumstances actuarial assumptions selected for Region 1 and Region 2 separately should be used instead of combined.

Common mistakes included:
- Giving only reasons related to the claims and not talking about the change in mix of business (earned premium has changed)
- Only saying earned premiums are not equal without providing details and an argument of the implication to the results
- Giving only reasons that premium has changed without talking about the implication to the claims (reporting pattern, trends, etc.)
