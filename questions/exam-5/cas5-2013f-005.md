Given the following information:

- All policies are annual, and rate level is reviewed annually.
- Rate change takes effect on January 1, 2013.
- Unlimited annual loss frequency trend = -1%.
- Unlimited annual loss severity trend = +2%.
- Annual average written premium per exposure = 0%.
- Assume the exposures are inflation sensitive.
- Annual exposure trend = +1%.

## Part a (1.5 points)

For accident year 2012, determine the trended ultimate loss ratio to use in the January 1, 2013 rate level analysis incorporating a large loss adjustment for claims above \$1 million.

### Answer

$$\text{Large Loss Factor} = \frac{\text{Total Excess Losses} \times \text{Unlimited Dev Factor (ult)}}{\text{Total Reported Losses} \times \text{Unlimited Dev Factor} - \text{Excess Losses}} = \frac{40{,}000}{400{,}000 - 40{,}000} = \frac{40{,}000}{360{,}000} \approx 0.1111$$

The large loss adjustment factor applied to capped (limited) losses = $1 + 0.1111 = 1.1111$.

**Trend period:** Average accident date of AY 2012 is July 1, 2012. New rates effective January 1, 2013, in effect for one year (average date July 1, 2013). Trend period = 1.5 years (July 1, 2012 to January 1, 2014 midpoint, or 7/1/2012 to 7/1/2013 = 1.0 year for 1-year policies effective 1/1/2013; since the question uses average accident date to average accident date of prospective period: 7/1/2012 to 1/1/2014 = 1.5 years).

**Loss ratio trend:** Frequency trend × Severity trend = $(-1\%) \times (+2\%) = (0.99)(1.02)^{1.5}$.

- Combined loss ratio trend factor: $(0.99)(1.02)^{1.5} \approx (0.99)(1.0302) \approx 0.9999$

**AY 2012 unlimited development factor** = 1.20.

**AY 2012 reported losses (limited to \$1M)** = \$27,500 - \$0 = \$27,500 (000s) (no excess losses in 2012).

**AY 2012 trended ultimate limited loss ratio:**

$$= \frac{(\text{Reported Losses} - \text{Excess Losses}) \times \text{Unlimited Dev Factor} \times \text{Large Loss Factor} \times \text{Trend Factor}}{\text{On-Level Premium}}$$

$$= \frac{(27{,}500 - 0)(1.20)(1.1111)(0.99 \times 1.02^{1.5})}{45{,}212}$$

$$= \frac{27{,}500 \times 1.20 \times 1.1111 \times 0.9999}{45{,}212}$$

$$\approx \frac{36{,}666}{45{,}212} \approx 0.752$$

The trended ultimate loss ratio incorporating the large loss adjustment for AY 2012 is approximately **0.752**.

### Explanation

The large loss adjustment corrects for the fact that individual years have volatile excess losses. The approach:

1. Compute the average excess loss ratio across all years: $40{,}000 / (400{,}000 - 40{,}000) = 11.11\%$.
2. Apply unlimited loss development to bring AY 2012 losses to ultimate (factor = 1.20).
3. Apply the large loss load factor (1.1111) to the limited losses to restore the expected excess loss content based on the all-year average.
4. Apply the combined trend factor $(0.99)(1.02)^{1.5}$ for 1.5-year trend period.
5. Divide by on-level premium.

The key formula for AY 2012 is:

$$\text{Trended Ultimate LR} = \frac{(L - E) \times \text{UltDev} \times (1 + \text{LLF}) \times \text{Trend}}{P}$$

where $L$ = reported losses, $E$ = excess losses for AY 2012, UltDev = unlimited development factor, LLF = large loss factor = $\sum E / (\sum L - \sum E)$ over all years, and $P$ = on-level premium.

### Examiner Report

Candidates most often determined the appropriate trend period of 1.5 years and received credit for knowing the components of the loss ratio trend. Candidates received credit whether they treated the loss development factors given as cumulative or incremental. Candidates struggled with the large loss factor — many did not calculate it correctly, and others did not apply it correctly. An example of incorrect application was not subtracting excess losses from reported losses in 2012 before applying the excess loss factor.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Discuss the appropriateness of a large loss adjustment for AY 2012 as used in part a.

### Answer

The large loss adjustment is appropriate for AY 2012 because the amount of excess losses (losses above \$1 million) varied considerably from year to year across the experience period (ranging from \$0 to over \$9 million). This volatility means that any individual year's experience is an unreliable indicator of expected excess losses. AY 2012 had \$0 in excess losses, which is likely not representative of future expectations. The large loss adjustment smooths the data by using an average excess loss provision based on the full historical period, resulting in more stable and credible indications.

### Examiner Report

Candidates needed to identify the volatility in the data and the benefit of stability in the indications. Multiple responses were acceptable for each component, but candidates often were able to elaborate on only one of the two elements (volatility vs. stability).

## Part c (0.75 points)

Assume the rate associated with the first \$1 million of coverage is analyzed using only the data above. Briefly discuss three modifications to loss and premium elements that would produce a more accurate rate analysis.

### Answer

Three modifications that would improve the analysis:

1. **Adjust the on-level premium to reflect only the first \$1 million of coverage:** The on-level premium should be reduced by the portion attributable to limits above \$1 million so that the premium matches the limited losses being analyzed. The adjustment may vary by year.

2. **Adjust the loss development factors to reflect the lower development associated with capped losses:** When losses are limited to \$1 million, late-emerging large losses above the cap do not contribute to development, so the unlimited development factors overstate development for limited losses. A separate limited-loss development factor should be used.

3. **Adjust the severity trend to reflect the lower trend applicable to capped losses:** Limiting losses to \$1 million reduces the impact of severity trend because losses at the cap do not increase further. A limited-loss severity trend (which is lower than the unlimited severity trend) should be applied.

### Examiner Report

Candidates often were able to identify three different enhancements, receiving full credit. Common acceptable answers included adjusting premium for the cost of layers above \$1M, using limited-loss development factors, and using limited-loss trend factors.
