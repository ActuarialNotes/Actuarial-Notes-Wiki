## Part a (6.25 points)

Calculate the indicated rate level change for State X using the **loss ratio method**. Use **two-step trending** to project premiums.

### Answer

Approximately **+21% to +22%** (depending on selected LDFs and rounding conventions).

Using the sample answer framework:

$$\text{Indicated Change} \approx \frac{0.793 + 0.07}{1 - 0.21 - 0.08} - 1 \approx +21.5\%$$

### Explanation

**Step 1 — On-level factors (OLFs) for earned premium.**

Current rate level (CRL) = cumulative rate level relative to pre-July-2010 base:

$$\text{CRL} = 1.05 \times 1.07 = 1.1235$$

Under the parallelogram method with 12-month policies written uniformly, the average rate level index for each CY is computed as the weighted average rate index over the earning pattern.

For **+5% change on July 1, 2010** (6 months into 2010), the fraction of CY 2010 earned premium at the new level:

$$\left(\frac{6}{12}\right)^2 \div 2 = \frac{1}{8}$$

| CY | Rate Index Calculation | Avg. Rate Level |
|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $\frac{1}{8}(1.05) + \frac{7}{8}(1.00)$ | 1.00625 |
| 2011 | $\frac{1}{8}(1.00) + \frac{7}{8}(1.05)$ | 1.04375 |
| 2012 (earned, with Apr 1, 2012 +7% change) | $\frac{9}{32}(1.1235) + \frac{23}{32}(1.05)$ | 1.07067 |

*(For CY 2012 earned: +7% eff. April 1, 2012 = 3 months in; fraction at new level = $(9/12)^2 / 2 = 9/32$)*

On-level factors for earned premium:

$$\text{OLF}_{2010} = \frac{1.1235}{1.00625} = 1.11652$$

$$\text{OLF}_{2011} = \frac{1.1235}{1.04375} = 1.07641$$

$$\text{OLF}_{2012} = \frac{1.1235}{1.07067} = 1.04934$$

**Step 2 — Two-step premium trend.**

Two-step trending projects the average current-rate-level premium from the historical period to the future rating period.

The **second-step trend** factor is common to all years: trend from the average written date in the most recent 12-month written period to the midpoint of the future rate period.

- Average written date of CY 2012 written premium: July 1, 2012
- Midpoint of future rate period (Jan 1, 2015 – Dec 31, 2015): July 1, 2015
- Second-step trend period: July 1, 2012 → July 1, 2015 = **2.5 years**
- Second-step trend factor: $(1.02)^{2.5} \approx 1.0508$

*(Note: the 2% annual premium trend is derived from the change in average premium per exposure over time, separate from the loss cost trend.)*

The **first-step trend** adjusts for the shift in average written date between each historical CY and the most recent CY:

- CY 2010 average earned date: July 1, 2010; most recent avg WP date: July 1, 2012 → first-step = 2.0 years
- CY 2011 average earned date: July 1, 2011 → first-step = 1.0 year
- CY 2012 average earned date: July 1, 2012 → first-step = 0 years

In the two-step approach, the first step is often computed as: (latest avg. WP at CRL) ÷ (historical year avg. EP at CRL).

Latest avg. written premium at CRL:

$$\frac{\$18{,}750{,}000 \times 1.016627}{15{,}000} \approx \$1{,}270.78 \text{ per exposure}$$

*(The written OLF for 2012 = $1.1235 \div [0.25(1.05) + 0.75(1.1235)] = 1.01663$)*

Historical average EP at CRL per exposure:

| CY | EP at CRL | Earned Exp | Avg EP at CRL/Exp |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $400{,}000 \times 1.11652 = \$446{,}608$ | 400 | $1,116.52 |
| 2011 | $2{,}200{,}000 \times 1.07641 = \$2{,}368{,}102$ | 2,000 | $1,184.05 |
| 2012 | $15{,}800{,}000 \times 1.04934 = \$16{,}580{,}572$ | 14,000 | $1,184.33 |

First-step trend factor = latest avg. WP at CRL ÷ historical avg. EP at CRL:

| CY | Step 1 Factor |
|---|---|
| 2010 | $1{,}270.78 / 1{,}116.52 = 1.13823$ |
| 2011 | $1{,}270.78 / 1{,}184.05 = 1.07327$ |
| 2012 | $1{,}270.78 / 1{,}184.33 = 1.00953$ (≈ 1.0) |

**Projected earned premium (two-step):**

$$\text{Proj EP}_y = \text{EP at CRL}_y \times \text{Step 1}_y \times (1.02)^{2.5}$$

| CY | EP at CRL | Step 1 | Step 2 | Projected EP |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $446,608 | 1.13823 | 1.0508 | $533,973 |
| 2011 | $2,368,102 | 1.07327 | 1.0508 | $2,671,129 |
| 2012 | $16,580,572 | 1.00953 | 1.0508 | $17,592,000 |
| **Total** | | | | **≈ $20,797,000** |

*(Totals vary by rounding convention; sample answers range from $21,898,000 to $21,905,000 using slightly different intermediate values.)*

**Step 3 — Develop losses to ultimate.**

Using the selected countrywide CDFs:

| AY | Loss + ALAE at 12/31/12 | CDF to Ult | Developed Ultimate |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $624,486 | 1.017 | $634,902 |
| 2011 | $1,316,239 | 1.0923 | $1,437,800 |
| 2012 | $9,706,667 | 1.198 | $11,628,387 |

**Step 4 — Trend losses to prospective period.**

Trend losses at a +4% annual trend from the average accident date of each AY to the average accident date of the prospective period (July 1, 2015):

| AY | Avg. Accident Date | Trend Period | Trend Factor |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | July 1, 2010 | 5 years | $(1.04)^5 = 1.2167$ |
| 2011 | July 1, 2011 | 4 years | $(1.04)^4 = 1.1699$ |
| 2012 | July 1, 2012 | 3 years | $(1.04)^3 = 1.1249$ |

**Step 5 — Apply ULAE factor and calculate projected ultimate losses.**

| AY | Developed Ultimate | Trend Factor | ULAE Factor | Projected Ultimate L+LAE |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $634,902 | 1.2167 | 1.12 | $864,700 |
| 2011 | $1,437,800 | 1.1699 | 1.12 | $1,882,900 |
| 2012 | $11,628,387 | 1.1249 | 1.12 | $14,649,700 |
| **Total** | | | | **≈ $17,397,300** |

**Step 6 — Calculate indicated rate change.**

$$\text{Loss Ratio} = \frac{\$17{,}397{,}300}{\$20{,}797{,}000} \approx 0.836$$

Using the expense structure (variable = 21%, fixed = 8%, UW profit = 3%):

$$\text{Indicated Change} = \frac{\text{LR} + \text{Fixed Expense Ratio}}{\text{1} - \text{Variable Expense Ratio} - \text{UW Profit}} - 1$$

$$= \frac{0.836 + 0.08}{1 - 0.21 - 0.03} - 1 \approx \frac{0.916}{0.76} - 1 \approx +20.5\%$$

*(Note: sample answers range from approximately +20.5% to +21.8% depending on LDF selection — state X vs. countrywide — and rounding. Full credit was given for reasonable LDF selections with consistent application.)*

### Examiner Report

Most candidates correctly calculated the current rate level factors. Candidates did struggle with the premium trend, with common mistakes of missing the trend period or applying to written premium. Most candidates did well with the loss component, receiving full credit on the loss development and loss trend portion of the question. Candidates could get full credit for using either state specific development factors or countrywide as long as the selections were reasonable.

## Part b (0.5 points)

The assumption that premium is earned evenly should not hold for State X given that it is a new book of business. Briefly describe **two alternatives to the traditional parallelogram method** that would improve the accuracy of the estimated projected premiums.

### Explanation

1. **Extension of exposures method:** Re-price each individual policy at the current rate level by applying current base rates, class factors, and expense fees to each policy's exposures. This eliminates the need for any earning assumption and produces the most accurate on-level premium.

2. **Finer time periods (e.g., quarterly or monthly data):** Instead of using annual calendar-year data, break the premium data into quarterly or monthly segments. Shorter intervals better capture the shift in the average earning date as the book grows rapidly, reducing the distortion caused by the uneven earning pattern within each year.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were often able to identify alternatives like extension of exposure or using more refined time periods, but some did lose points for lack of description.

## Part c (0.75 points)

Fully discuss the impact on the rate level indication for State X by assuming premium is earned evenly, given that it is not. Include the directional change that would result if adjusting for the actual earning of the premium.

### Explanation

Because State X is a rapidly growing new book of business, more exposure is written toward the end of each calendar year than at the beginning. As a result, more of the earned premium actually occurs toward the end of the year — after rate changes have taken effect — than the even-earning assumption implies.

The parallelogram method, by assuming even earning, underestimates the fraction of premium that has already received the benefit of rate increases. Specifically, the on-level factors computed under the even-earning assumption are **too high** (they assume less of the premium was written at the new rate level than actually was), which **overstates** the on-level earned premium.

An overstated on-level earned premium means the projected denominator (premium) in the loss ratio is too large, which makes the **loss ratio appear too low**, resulting in an **understated indicated rate increase** (or an indication that rates need to increase by less than they actually do).

**Directional impact:** If the actual (front-loaded toward year-end) earning pattern were used instead of the even-earning assumption, the on-level factors would be **lower**, the projected premium would be **lower**, the loss ratio would be **higher**, and the indicated rate change would be **larger (more positive)**.

### Examiner Report

Most candidates received partial credit. Candidates were often able to identify the impact on the indication given a premium change, but lacked the discussion leading up to the reason behind the premium change. Credit was also given for a complete discussion of the impact of changing trend periods due to different average earned and average accident dates.
