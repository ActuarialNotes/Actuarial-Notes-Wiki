Given the following information:

| Claim Number | Total Limits Loss ($000s) |
|---|---|
| 1 | 15 |
| 2 | 21 |
| 3 | 24 |
| 4 | 55 |

- Each total limits loss is subject to an 8% annual severity trend.
- Basic limit = \$25,000.

## Part a (1 point)

Calculate the basic limits loss trend over a one-year timeframe.

### Answer
4.56

### Explanation
Trend each total limits loss by 8%, then cap both the original and trended losses at the \$25,000 (i.e. 25, in \$000s) basic limit:

| Claim | Loss at $t=0$ | Capped at 25 | Loss at $t=1$ ($\times 1.08$) | Capped at 25 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 15 | 15 | 16.20 | 16.20 |
| 2 | 21 | 21 | 22.68 | 22.68 |
| 3 | 24 | 24 | 25.92 | 25.00 |
| 4 | 55 | 25 | 59.40 | 25.00 |
| **Total** | | **85** | | **88.88** |

$$\text{Basic Limits Loss Trend} = \frac{88.88}{85} - 1 \approx 4.56\%$$

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to trend each loss and cap it at the basic limit before summing. A common mistake was incorrectly totaling the trended basic limit losses, such as by failing to include all four claims.

## Part b (0.75 points)

Calculate the excess loss trend over a one-year timeframe.

### Answer
17.73

### Explanation
Excess loss for each claim is the total limits loss in excess of the \$25,000 basic limit.

Original excess loss:
$$(15-15) + (21-21) + (24-24) + (55-25) = 30$$

Trended total (uncapped) losses, from Part a:
$$16.20 + 22.68 + 25.92 + 59.40 = 124.20$$

Trended excess loss is the trended total loss less the trended basic limits loss (88.88 from Part a):
$$124.20 - 88.88 = 35.32$$

$$\text{Excess Loss Trend} = \frac{35.32}{30} - 1 \approx 17.73\%$$

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to calculate the excess loss trend from the change in total excess losses. Common mistakes included applying trend only to the already-excess portion of each loss rather than trending the full loss and then taking the excess, and using a basic limit other than the \$25,000 given to split losses into basic and excess.

## Part c (0.5 points)

Discuss the appropriateness of applying total limits trend to losses at basic limits for purposes of ratemaking.

### Explanation
It is **not appropriate**. As shown in Parts a and b, the basic limits loss trend (≈4.56%) and the excess loss trend (≈17.73%) differ substantially from each other — and from the underlying total limits severity trend (8%). This occurs because once a loss grows large enough to cross the basic limit, additional trend on that loss flows entirely into the excess layer rather than the basic limit layer, and losses near the limit can cross into the excess layer entirely as they trend upward. Applying the 8% total limits trend directly to basic limits losses would therefore overstate the basic limits loss trend. The basic limits and excess layers should instead be trended separately, each using a trend rate appropriate to that layer.

### Examiner Report
Candidates needed to explain that the basic limits and excess loss trends differ, why they differ, and the impact of applying the total limits trend to basic limits losses (or to propose a valid alternative, such as trending the layers separately). Common mistakes included stating that the trends differ without explaining why or how this affects the estimate, giving internally conflicting statements about the direction of the effect, citing volatility of excess losses or the need to cap/separately treat large losses (issues unrelated to trend) as the reason, and stating that applying total limits trend to basic limits losses is appropriate without noting that this only holds when the basic, excess, and total limits trends coincide, or when the total trend is applied to the sum of basic and excess loss.
