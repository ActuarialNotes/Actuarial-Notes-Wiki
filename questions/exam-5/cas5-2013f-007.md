Two methods of deriving expense provisions in ratemaking include the Premium-Based Projection Method and the Exposure/Policy-Based Projection Method.

## Part a (1 point)

For each method, briefly describe how both fixed and variable expenses are treated.

### Answer

**Premium-Based Projection Method:**

Both fixed expenses and variable expenses are separated from each other. Each category is then divided by written or earned premium to produce a fixed expense ratio and a variable expense ratio, respectively. These ratios are then applied to projected premium to derive the expense provision in the rate.

**Exposure/Policy-Based Projection Method:**

Variable expenses are separated out and divided by written or earned premium to derive a variable expense ratio (same as the premium-based method). Fixed expenses are separated out and divided by earned exposures or policy counts (rather than premium) to derive an average fixed expense per exposure or per policy. This average fixed expense can be trended if necessary to reflect projected cost levels.

### Explanation

The critical distinction is how **fixed** expenses are handled:

- In the Premium-Based method, fixed expenses are expressed as a ratio to premium. This means the fixed expense "load" in the rate scales proportionally with premium level — a distortion if rates change.
- In the Exposure/Policy-Based method, fixed expenses are expressed per exposure or per policy. This correctly treats fixed expenses as independent of premium level, since these costs (e.g., policy issuance, billing) are incurred once per policy regardless of the premium charged.

Variable expenses (e.g., commissions, premium taxes) are proportional to premium by definition and are handled as a ratio to premium in both methods.

### Examiner Report

More than half of the candidates received full credit for this part. Common mistakes were: (1) stating that both fixed and variable expenses are treated as one ratio in the Premium-Based method; (2) stating that variable expenses are related to exposures/policy counts instead of premium for the Exposure/Policy-Based method.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Briefly describe one shortcoming (or distortion) of each method.

### Answer

**Premium-Based Projection Method — Shortcoming:**

The fixed expense ratio will be distorted if there are rate changes during or after the experience period. Since fixed expenses do not actually vary with premium, expressing them as a ratio to historical premium and then applying that ratio to future (changed) premium will over- or understate the fixed expense provision. A rate increase will inflate the denominator (premium) while the numerator (fixed expense dollars) is unchanged, causing the ratio to understate future fixed costs per policy.

**Exposure/Policy-Based Projection Method — Shortcoming:**

This method does not account for economies of scale in a growing book of business. As the book grows, the fixed expense per exposure may decline (since certain fixed costs are spread over more policies), but the method uses a historical average that may overstate or understate the true per-exposure cost. Alternatively, if exposures trend at a different rate than fixed expenses, the method may not accurately project fixed costs.

### Examiner Report

A majority of candidates were able to correctly describe a shortcoming for the Premium-Based method, while many had difficulty doing the same for the Exposure/Policy-Based method. A common mistake was referencing a shortcoming of the pure premium or loss ratio methods in general, which are not necessarily shortcomings of the methods for deriving expense provisions specifically.
