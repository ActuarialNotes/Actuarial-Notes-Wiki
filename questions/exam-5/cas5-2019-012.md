Given the following outputs from a retention model:

**Number of Policies Renewed by Rate Change:**

| Rate Change | Territory A | Territory B |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| -20% | 11,610 | 6,825 |
| -10% | 11,475 | 6,750 |
| 0% | 11,408 | 6,600 |
| +10% | 10,800 | 6,300 |
| +20% | 9,490 | 6,000 |

**Current book information:**

| | Territory A | Territory B |
|--|-------------|-------------|
| Current Policy Count | 13,500 | 7,500 |
| Current Premium (\$) | 2,700,000 | 1,350,000 |
| Ultimate Loss Ratio | 80% | 70% |

- All expenses are variable.
- Policy count retention level does not affect premium projections.
- Rates are set to meet management targets:
  - i. 70% permissible loss ratio for total book.
  - ii. 80% minimum retention ratio in each territory.

## Part a (1.5 points)

Recommend rate changes for territories A and B using the loss ratio method, taking management targets into account.

### Explanation

**Step 1: Calculate minimum policy retention counts.**

$$\text{Min retention}_A = 13{,}500 \times 0.80 = 10{,}800$$
$$\text{Min retention}_B = 7{,}500 \times 0.80 = 6{,}000$$

From the retention table, reading what rate change corresponds to each minimum retention:

- Territory A: 10,800 policies retained at **+10%** rate change (exactly at minimum)
- Territory B: 6,000 policies retained at **+20%** rate change (exactly at minimum)

**Step 2: Calculate indicated rate change for each territory using the loss ratio method.**

$$\text{Indicated change}_A = \frac{\text{ULR}_A}{\text{PLR}} - 1 = \frac{0.80}{0.70} - 1 = +14.3\%$$
$$\text{Indicated change}_B = \frac{\text{ULR}_B}{\text{PLR}} - 1 = \frac{0.70}{0.70} - 1 = 0.0\%$$

**Step 3: Apply retention constraints as caps.**

- Territory A: indicated +14.3%, but retention cap is +10% (at +14.3%, retention would drop below 10,800). Cap rate change at **+10%**.
- Territory B: indicated 0%, so any positive change would be above indication. However, the overall book must achieve a 70% loss ratio, so we need to check whether Territory B needs an offset adjustment.

**Step 4: Determine if an offset is needed due to Territory A's cap.**

Premium shortfall from capping Territory A below indication:

$$\text{Shortfall} = (\text{Ind. factor}_A - \text{Cap factor}_A) \times \text{Premium}_A$$
$$= (1.1429 - 1.10) \times \$2{,}700{,}000 = 0.0429 \times \$2{,}700{,}000 = \$115{,}714$$

To achieve the overall 70% PLR, this shortfall must be recovered from Territory B:

$$\text{Addl. B change} = \frac{\$115{,}714}{\$1{,}350{,}000} = 8.57\%$$

**Step 5: Propose final rate changes.**

Territory B indicated change was 0%, plus the 8.57% offset:

$$\text{Proposed change}_B = 0\% + 8.57\% = +8.57\%$$

Check Territory B retention at +8.57%: from the retention table, +10% gives 6,300 and +20% gives 6,000. At +8.57% (less than +10%), retention will be above 6,300, which is well above the minimum of 6,000. Constraint satisfied.

**Recommended rate changes:**
- Territory A: **+10%**
- Territory B: **+8.57%**

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to recommend a set of rate changes that satisfy the operational constraints and management targets. The full solution required:

1. Calculating the indicated rate change for each territory.
2. Identifying the territory cap restriction from retention constraints and applying it as needed.
3. Recognizing that the overall loss ratio would not be achieved due to the territory A cap and then determining the premium shortfall.
4. Determining an adjustment to Territory B to bring the total loss ratio across all territories to 70%.

Although an unnecessary additional step, many candidates took the time to determine territory relativities, an overall rate change, and then combined the two to determine territory rate changes.

Common mistakes included: calculating rate changes but using them as relativities (or vice versa); calculating the premium shortfall and offset adjustment by multiplying an additive factor or adding a multiplicative factor; calculating the indicated rate change and applying the territory cap but then not taking the next step to offset the shortfall; adjusting the expected losses (the losses have not changed — the insurer is adjusting rates to cover the same losses); and simply stating proposed rate changes without any justification or calculation.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Describe how the rate changes recommended in part a could drive adverse selection.

### Explanation

Under the recommendations, Territory A receives a +10% increase while Territory B receives a +8.57% increase. Territory A is underpriced relative to its indication (+14.3% indicated), while Territory B is overpriced relative to its indication (0% indicated vs. +8.57% implemented).

If competitors recognize the true territory differentiation and price accordingly, Territory B insureds will face lower rates elsewhere and non-renew with this company. Meanwhile, Territory A insureds — who are effectively undercharged relative to their true cost — will find this company more attractive and stay or even increase in volume. Over time, the portfolio shifts toward a higher concentration of the less profitable Territory A business and away from the more profitable Territory B business, worsening the overall loss ratio and potentially leading to a cycle of further adverse rate action.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to understand how the deviation of rate implementation from indication could lead to adverse selection — specifically the change in portfolio mix that would result.

Common mistakes included: not describing the portfolio mix change that would occur; stating the rate increase as a factor rather than recognizing that adverse selection is driven by undercharging or overcharging; citing risk classification as a reason when the question asked to relate adverse selection to the part a rate change recommendation; and not identifying which territory was underpriced versus overpriced.

## Part c (0.5 points)

Identify two non-pricing solutions that could return the fundamental insurance equation to balance.

### Explanation

Any two of the following (must identify the component to adjust and how, not just name a category):

1. **Reduce expenses**: Cut operating or administrative costs (e.g., lay off employees, reduce overhead) to lower the expense ratio without changing rates.
2. **Adjust underwriting guidelines**: Tighten underwriting standards to target more profitable risks — for example, restricting new business in Territory A or requiring higher credit scores — reducing expected loss costs.
3. **Reduce target profit**: Lower the required profit/contingency provision, accepting a smaller margin.
4. **Increase investment income**: Pursue a more aggressive investment strategy to generate higher investment profit, offsetting underwriting losses.
5. **Reduce benefits/coverage**: Restrict policy coverages or limits to reduce expected losses.
6. **Target marketing toward lower-risk insureds**: Market specifically to lower-risk segments to shift the portfolio mix toward more profitable risks.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to understand the fundamental insurance equation and identify non-pricing alternatives to bring it back into balance. A complete answer required both identifying what component of the equation to adjust (expenses, expected loss, profitability load, etc.) and giving an example of how this could be done without altering price.

Common mistakes included: not providing a thorough response (e.g., only naming a component without explaining how to adjust it); identifying pricing changes rather than non-pricing alternatives; and listing two actions that both adjust the same component of the equation (e.g., restricting writing in Territory A through underwriting guidelines AND marketing heavily to Territory B are both adjustments to the risk mix/expected loss component).
