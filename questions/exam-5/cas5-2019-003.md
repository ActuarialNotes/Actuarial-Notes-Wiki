## Part a (0.5 points)

Briefly describe one advantage and one disadvantage of calendar year data aggregation.

### Explanation
**Advantage:** Calendar year data is finalized as soon as the period ends — both the premium/exposure and the loss data require no further development — so it is the fastest and least costly basis to compile and is available immediately for analysis.

**Disadvantage:** Calendar year aggregation mixes premium/exposure and losses from multiple accident or policy periods within the same calendar period, so it provides a poor match between the losses analyzed and the premium/exposure that generated them. This makes calendar year data more susceptible to distortion from rate level changes, mix shifts, or changes in claims handling that occurred partway through the period.

### Examiner Report
Candidates needed to identify one advantage and one disadvantage of calendar year aggregation, addressing both the premium/exposure side and the loss side. Common mistakes included discussing only loss data or only premium/exposure data, citing the lack of loss development as a disadvantage, and describing calendar year's suitability for financial reporting as its ratemaking advantage rather than its immediate availability.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Justify an appropriate data aggregation approach for a risk classification plan for a long-tailed line of business.

### Explanation
Accident year (or policy year) aggregation is appropriate. A risk classification analysis compares loss experience across classes, so it is important that the losses being compared are matched to the exposures/premium that produced them. Accident year data groups losses by the period in which they occurred and ties them to the exposures earned during that same period, giving a far better match between losses and premium than calendar year data — even though, for a long-tailed line, the accident year losses will need to be developed to an ultimate value. Policy year aggregation gives an even cleaner match (all losses tied to a single cohort of policies), at the cost of an even longer development tail.

### Examiner Report
Candidates were expected to select accident year and/or policy year aggregation and justify it in terms of the match between losses and premium/exposure for a long-tailed line. Common mistakes included selecting calendar year or report year aggregation (without assuming claims-made coverage), justifying the choice by referencing IBNR or loss development rather than the premium/loss match, and overstating accident year as providing a "perfect" match between premium and losses.

## Part c (0.5 points)

Justify using report year data aggregation for a claims-made line of business.

### Explanation
Report year aggregation is appropriate for a claims-made line. Under claims-made coverage, the policy that responds to a claim is determined by the date the claim is *reported*, not the date it occurred — so grouping losses by report year matches them to the premium/exposure of the policy that actually provides coverage, giving the best available match between losses and premium. In addition, because claims-made policies have essentially no pure IBNR once a policy period ends (only IBNER on claims already reported), report year data develops quickly toward its ultimate value, making it a practical and reliable basis for this line.

### Examiner Report
Candidates needed to explain why report year aggregation is appropriate for a claims-made line, tying the justification to the claims-made coverage trigger. Common mistakes included providing no justification beyond restating that report year is appropriate, giving overly brief responses (e.g. "claims-made policies cover reported claims" or "there is no pure IBNR" with no further explanation), stating that claims-made policies have no IBNR at all without clarifying that IBNER still exists, and justifying the use of claims-made coverage itself rather than the appropriateness of report year aggregation.
