A risk manager has the following concerns about a quote for a large deductible policy compared to the company's current small deductible policy:

i. The expense provision in the rates includes expenses associated with claims below the deductible.
ii. The rates include a provision associated with the chance of the insured becoming bankrupt. This does not appear to be relevant to the exposure being underwritten.
iii. The profit margin is higher than the one used for the small deductible option.

Explain why each of the concerns may be invalid.

## Part a (0.5 points)

Explain why concern i — that the expense provision includes expenses for claims below the deductible — may be invalid.

### Explanation

For large deductible policies, the insurer typically handles and pays all claims on a ground-up basis, including claims that fall entirely below the deductible. The insurer then seeks reimbursement from the insured for the deductible portion. Because the insurer is involved in administering and paying claims below the deductible (incurring claim handling, billing, and processing costs), it is appropriate to include a provision for those expenses in the rates. The concern is therefore **invalid** — the expense load is justified by the insurer's actual claim-handling activities below the deductible.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to understand the difference between a small deductible policy and a large deductible policy, specifically the common practice whereby insurers handle all claims and seek reimbursement. A complete answer required identifying this practice AND tying it to the need for an expense provision.

Common mistakes included explanations in the wrong direction (suggesting the concern was valid); not understanding the functionality of a large deductible policy; and identifying the common practice without connecting it to why expenses are still incurred.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Explain why concern ii — that a bankruptcy provision is not relevant to the exposure being underwritten — may be invalid.

### Explanation

Under a large deductible arrangement, the insurer pays claims ground-up and then bills the insured for the deductible portion. If the insured becomes bankrupt, the insurer may be unable to recover the deductible amounts it has already paid out. This creates **credit risk** for the insurer that does not exist (or is much smaller) under a small deductible policy. A provision for this insolvency/credit risk is therefore a legitimate cost of writing the business, making the concern **invalid**.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to identify the mechanism by which bankruptcy risk materializes for the insurer under a large deductible policy (i.e., non-recovery of deductible reimbursements) and connect this to the appropriateness of including such a provision.

Common mistakes included explanations in the wrong direction; not understanding the functionality of a large deductible policy; and not tying the explanation back to why this risk is relevant to the insurer's cost structure.

## Part c (0.5 points)

Explain why concern iii — that the profit margin is higher than for the small deductible option — may be invalid.

### Explanation

Large deductible policies involve greater uncertainty and volatility in the losses that the insurer retains. Because claims below the deductible are ultimately reimbursed by the insured, the insurer's retained losses tend to be higher-severity (above the deductible), which are more difficult to predict and more volatile. Additionally, the credit risk exposure is greater for large deductible policies. Higher risk warrants a higher expected return (profit margin). Therefore, a higher profit margin for large deductible policies is appropriate, and the concern is **invalid**.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to identify that higher risk — stemming from higher retained severity, greater volatility, and credit risk — justifies a higher profit margin for large deductible policies.

Common mistakes included explanations in the wrong direction; not understanding the functionality of a large deductible policy; and incomplete explanations that identified the risk differential but did not tie it to the justification for a higher profit margin.
