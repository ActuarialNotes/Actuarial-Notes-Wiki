**Deductible Recovery** is the amount an insurer recoups from a policyholder for loss payments the insurer advanced on the policyholder's behalf that fall within the policyholder's deductible obligation, most commonly arising in **large-deductible programs**.

- In large-deductible programs the insurer pays claims in full (for cash flow and licensing reasons) and then bills the insured for the deductible portion; deductible recoveries therefore develop over time as billings are collected, and must be estimated separately from gross losses
- Deductible recoveries are conceptually similar to [[Salvage and Subrogation]] — both reduce the insurer's net loss cost — but deductible recoveries carry **credit risk** (the policyholder may become insolvent before reimbursing the insurer)
- Net losses under a large-deductible program equal gross paid losses minus collected deductible recoveries; the uncollected portion represents a contingent asset that actuaries may discount for collectability

> [!example]- Large-Deductible Recovery {Example}
> A workers compensation policy has a \$$250{,}000$ per-occurrence deductible. The insurer pays a \$$400{,}000$ claim in full and bills the insured for the deductible portion.
>
> > [!answer]-
> > The insurer's **gross paid loss** is \$$400{,}000$. The **deductible recovery** due from the policyholder is \$$250{,}000$, making the insurer's **net loss** \$$150{,}000$. If the policyholder only remits \$$200{,}000$ before becoming insolvent, the net loss rises to \$$200{,}000$ and the \$$50{,}000$ shortfall must be recognized as uncollectible.
