**Ratemaking Constraints** are practical limitations that cause the implemented rate change to differ from the actuarially indicated change, arising from regulatory, competitive, operational, or legal considerations.

- Regulatory constraints: state insurance departments may require prior approval, cap maximum rate changes, or disallow certain rating variables, requiring the insurer to file within approved bounds
- Competitive constraints: a fully indicated increase may cause policyholder attrition if it prices the insurer above market; conversely, a large decrease may trigger adverse selection concerns
- Operational and system constraints: rating algorithms, policy administration systems, or agent workflows may limit the granularity or timing of rate changes that can be implemented
- Legal constraints include anti-discrimination statutes that prohibit or restrict use of certain rating variables (e.g., credit score in some jurisdictions, gender in others)

> [!example]- Regulatory Cap on Rate Change {Example}
> An actuary's indicated rate change is +18%. The state regulator caps single-filing rate increases at 10%. What options does the insurer have?
>
> > [!answer]-
> > The insurer implements the maximum permitted 10% now, leaving a residual 8% inadequacy. It may file an additional increase in the next cycle, restrict new business writings in the affected territory, or pursue non-rate actions (tighter underwriting guidelines, reduced coverage options) to manage loss exposure until full rate adequacy is achieved.
