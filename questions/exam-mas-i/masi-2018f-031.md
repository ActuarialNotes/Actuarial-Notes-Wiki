An actuary fits two GLMs, $M_1$ and $M_2$, to the same data in order to predict the probability of a customer purchasing an automobile insurance product. You are given the following information about each model:

| Model | Explanatory Variables | Degrees of Freedom Used | Log Likelihood |
|-------|-----------------------|-------------------------|----------------|
| $M_1$ | Offered Price, Number of Vehicles, Age of Primary Insured, Prior Insurance Carrier | 10 | -11,565 |
| $M_2$ | Offered Price, Number of Vehicles, Age of Primary Insured, Gender of Primary Insured, Credit Score of Primary Insured | 8 | -11,562 |

The actuary wants to evaluate which of the two models is superior.

Determine which of the following is the best course of action for the actuary to take.

- A) Perform a likelihood ratio test
- B) Compute the F-statistic and perform an F-test
- C) Compute and compare the deviances of the two models
- D) Compute and compare the AIC statistics of the two models
- E) Compute the Chi-squared statistic and perform a Chi-squared test

## Explanation

$M_1$ contains "Prior Insurance Carrier" (absent from $M_2$), while $M_2$ contains "Gender" and "Credit Score" (absent from $M_1$). Because each model includes variables the other lacks, the models are **not nested**.

Likelihood ratio tests, deviance comparisons, F-tests, and chi-square difference tests all require nested models. For **non-nested** models with different numbers of parameters, the appropriate tool is a penalized information criterion such as the **AIC**, which trades off goodness of fit against model complexity.

The answer is **D**.
