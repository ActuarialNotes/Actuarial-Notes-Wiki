**Claims Coding Changes** are modifications to how claims are categorized, classified, or assigned to coverage types within the claims system, which can produce apparent shifts in development patterns or loss levels that reflect reclassification rather than any true change in underlying loss experience.

- A common example is a system migration or recodification that moves claims between coverage lines (e.g., from general liability to products liability); losses appear to drop in one line and rise in another without any change in total loss cost
- The diagnostic tool is to compare the distribution across coding categories **before and after** the change date on the same diagonal — a sharp, sudden shift concentrated at one valuation date is evidence of a coding change rather than genuine development
- Unlike [[Claims Processing Changes]] (which alter settlement timing), coding changes primarily affect the **allocation** of losses across lines or categories; total reserve indications may be unaffected if the change is pure reclassification, but line-specific indications can be severely distorted

> [!example]- Coverage Reclassification {Example}
> An insurer migrates to a new claims system on $1/1/2023$. In the prior system, slip-and-fall claims at insured premises were coded under "general liability — premises." In the new system, the same claims are coded under "general liability — operations."
>
> > [!answer]-
> > The premises sub-line triangle will show a sharp **downward** jump at the $12/31/2022$ diagonal and the operations sub-line will show a corresponding **upward** jump. An actuary selecting development factors from both old and new data will compute distorted factors. The fix is to recode historical claims consistently or to select factors using only post-migration diagonals for affected segments.
