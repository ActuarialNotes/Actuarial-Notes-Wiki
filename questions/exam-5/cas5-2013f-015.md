## Part a (1 point)

According to the *Statement of Principles Regarding Property and Casualty Loss and Loss Adjustment Expense Reserves*, discuss two actuarial considerations for designing a reserve study for Company C.

### Answer

Two valid considerations (with explanation):

1. **Homogeneity / Differences in payment pattern**: The actuary must assess whether the two companies' data is sufficiently homogeneous to be combined. Differing claims handling practices, reserving philosophies, or settlement speeds between Company A and B could distort development patterns if the data is combined without adjustment.

2. **Credibility**: The actuary must determine whether sufficient data exists to produce credible and reliable reserve estimates. If one company is much smaller than the other, combining may be necessary to obtain credibility; alternatively, if the data is not homogeneous, separate analyses may each lack credibility.

Other acceptable considerations include:
- Differences in case reserve adequacy between the two companies
- Changes in the book of business (policy limits, classes, territories) that affect how data should be combined or which reserving methods are appropriate
- Relative size of the two companies — if one is much larger, the smaller company's data may be immaterial

### Explanation

The *Statement of Principles* directs the actuary to consider both the **appropriateness** (homogeneity, consistency) and **credibility** of the data used in a reserve analysis. When two companies merge, the actuary must evaluate whether their historical data is compatible before blending it into a single development study.

Simply listing a consideration without explanation or tying it to the data context received only partial credit.

### Examiner Report

The question asked for a discussion, but many candidates simply stated a consideration. Candidates needed both the consideration and something to tie it to either the data or an explanation of why it was important. Considerations had to relate in some way to a reserving study, and the candidate's logic and reasoning had to be valid. Candidates received only partial credit for lack of explanation. Very rarely did a candidate get zero credit on this part unless the question was skipped.

Simply listing diagnostics without computing them received partial credit only. Diagnostics that add no diagnostic value (e.g., total case reserves, open claim counts, loss ratios) did not receive credit.

### Examiner Report

Very few candidates lost credit on this part. Most candidates who showed three calculations got full credit. Simply listing three valid diagnostics received some credit, with some dialog on how each related to the data receiving more credit (but not full credit). Common reasons for losing points: not performing three calculations; only describing a calculation without computing it; calculations that added no diagnostic value (total case reserves, open claim counts, loss ratios).

## Part c (1 point)

Assume the mix of business is comparable (i.e., similar limits, classes, territories, etc.) for Company A and Company B. Argue whether or not it is appropriate to combine the historical data to estimate unpaid claims for accident years 2012 and prior as of December 31, 2012 for Company C.

### Answer

Arguments **for** combining (sample answer):

Company B has very little data relative to Company A. Given that the mix of business is assumed comparable, and disposal rates are similar between the two companies, it is reasonable to combine the data. Company B's results are more volatile due to small volume — this is expected. Since the settlement patterns are similar and the mix is comparable, distributional differences will not significantly distort the combined development factors. Using paid losses to estimate unpaid claims will be reasonable. Combining improves the overall credibility of the analysis.

Arguments **against** combining (also acceptable):

Company B's average paid severity is inconsistent and increasing in recent years, particularly at early maturities. Average case outstanding for Company B also shows different patterns from Company A, suggesting differences in case reserving practices. Combining the data would distort the development patterns for AY 2012 and prior, producing unreliable reserves. The two companies should be analyzed separately.

### Explanation

Either conclusion (combine or not combine) was acceptable for full credit, provided the argument was logically consistent with the diagnostic evidence from Part b and included a clear recommendation. The most common reason for losing points was not actually recommending to combine or not combine, or not explaining why the Part b diagnostics were or were not relevant to the combination decision.

### Examiner Report

Candidates generally did well on this question. Arguments either for or against combining the data were accepted, provided they were valid and logical. Some candidates lost points for not actually recommending to combine or not combine. The most common reason for losing points was not explaining why whatever reasons from Part b were relevant (or not relevant).

## Part d (1 point)

Assume that Company C's claims department will adopt Company A's claims practices. Argue whether or not it is appropriate to combine historical data to estimate unpaid claims for accident year 2013 as of December 31, 2013 for Company C.

### Answer

For AY 2013, Company C will operate entirely under Company A's claims practices going forward. This creates a different situation than AY 2012 and prior:

- Company B's historical data (AY 2012 and prior) was developed under Company B's own claims practices. If Company C adopts Company A's practices, the future development pattern for AY 2013 claims will follow Company A's pattern, not a blended or Company B pattern.
- It may be appropriate to combine the data only if Company B's historical data is adjusted to reflect Company A's claims practices (e.g., adjusting case reserves to Company A's philosophy using a Berquist-Sherman type adjustment).
- If no such adjustment is made, using Company B's unadjusted historical data alongside Company A's data to project AY 2013 would introduce a systematic distortion — the development factors derived from Company B's history would not apply to claims handled under Company A's practices.
- Given the size difference between the two companies (Company B is much smaller), simply excluding Company B's historical data and using only Company A's history for AY 2013 may be the most defensible approach.

### Explanation

The key distinction from Part c is that **going forward**, all claims will be handled under Company A's practices. Historical data generated under different practices is no longer a direct guide to future development unless adjusted. Arguments that simply restated Part c's reasoning without addressing the impact of the claims practice change received no credit.

### Examiner Report

Candidates did not do as well on this part. Simply re-stating the reasons from Part c did not get any credit. The arguments needed to be logical. Some candidates may not have read the question correctly, arguing why Company B should or should not use Company A's case reserving philosophy. Some candidates did not provide enough detail behind size difference implications, which resulted in some lost credit.
