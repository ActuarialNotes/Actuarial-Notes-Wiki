Given the following information:

| Amount | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| $500 | Current average premium |
| $600 | Indicated average premium |
| $510 | All competitors' average premium |

## Part a (0.5 points)

List two likely consequences of the company implementing the indicated rate.

### Explanation

Any two of the following:

1. **Reduced retention / policyholder non-renewals**: Policyholders will shop around and switch to competitors offering lower rates ($510 average), resulting in a decrease in the company's renewal rate.

2. **Loss of market share**: Both renewals and new business will be harder to write, as customers will find cheaper alternatives with competitors. The company's hit/conversion rate will decline.

3. **Improved loss ratio and profitability**: Because the rate increase brings premiums closer to the indicated (adequate) level, the loss ratio will improve and profitability per risk will increase.

4. **Regulatory scrutiny**: Depending on the jurisdiction, a large rate increase may be subject to objections or challenges from regulators.

5. **Competitor repricing**: Competitors observing the company's rate increase may also raise their own rates over time.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to provide two different consequences of implementing the indicated rate (both positive and negative consequences were accepted).

Common mistakes include:

- Stating the company is subject to adverse selection (this is a consequence of *not* implementing the indicated rate, not of implementing it).
- Stating the company's loss ratio will deteriorate (implementing the indicated rate should improve, not deteriorate, the loss ratio).
- Stating the company should not implement the indicated premium (not a consequence).

## Part b (0.5 points)

Briefly describe two factors that affect an insured's propensity to renew.

### Explanation

Any two of the following:

1. **Price / premium level**: If competitors offer similar coverage at a lower price, the insured is more likely to switch. The overall cost level of the product matters — higher-priced lines (like commercial) tend to see more shopping behavior.

2. **Rate change at renewal**: A large rate increase at renewal triggers insureds to shop around, even if the final price remains competitive in the market. The size of the rate change, not just the absolute level, influences the renewal decision.

3. **Customer service / claim handling experience**: Insureds who have had a positive claim handling or service experience are more likely to remain loyal to the company, regardless of small premium differences.

4. **Brand loyalty / tenure**: Long-tenured policyholders exhibit stronger loyalty and are less likely to non-renew than newly acquired customers. Younger insureds tend to shop around more than older ones.

5. **Competitor pricing**: If competitors are offering materially lower rates, even loyal customers may choose to switch.

6. **Need for coverage**: If the coverage is no longer needed (e.g., the insured sells the insured property), the policy will not be renewed regardless of price.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to briefly describe two factors affecting the insureds' propensity to renew.

Common mistakes include:

- Listing "Price" without any description as to why this affects renewal.
- Listing other factors without a description or explanation.

## Part c (0.75 points)

The company has decided to not implement the indicated rate. List three non-pricing solutions the company could implement to ensure profitability does not deteriorate.

### Explanation

Any three of the following:

1. **Tighten underwriting guidelines**: Modify underwriting rules to target more profitable (lower-risk) policies and non-renew or decline high-risk exposures. Improving the quality of risks written reduces expected losses without changing prices.

2. **Reduce expenses**: Cut fixed or variable expenses — such as reducing marketing spend, renegotiating vendor contracts, or reducing commission levels — to improve profitability without raising rates.

3. **Improve claims management**: Hire experienced claim adjusters, implement anti-fraud programs, or introduce loss mitigation programs to reduce claims paid and avoid claim leakage, thereby improving the loss ratio.

4. **Shift the mix of business**: Change the book composition by writing more policies in profitable segments and writing fewer in unprofitable segments (e.g., reducing concentration in geographic areas with poor experience).

5. **Reduce coverage / increase deductibles**: Offer higher-deductible products or lower policy limits to reduce the company's exposure to large losses, effectively reducing the expected loss cost without a headline rate change.

6. **Target favorable risks for marketing**: Focus acquisition efforts on risk profiles that are more likely to be profitable under current rates, rather than writing all available business.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to list three non-pricing solutions to maintain profitability.

Common mistakes include:

- "Better segment risks by changing relativities" — changing relativities is a pricing solution, not a non-pricing solution.
- "Change mix of business" without specifying the direction (i.e., shifting toward a *better-performing* mix).
- "Change coverage" without indicating a direction (e.g., reduce benefits, raise deductibles).
- "Increase investment income" — this is not within the company's direct operational control as a ratemaking solution.
- "Purchase reinsurance" — this transfers risk but does not directly improve underlying profitability.

## Part d (0.5 points)

Identify an issue with comparing one company's premium to another and briefly propose a solution to this issue.

### Explanation

**Issue**: Two companies' average premiums may not be directly comparable because they may serve different mixes of business, underwriting segments, coverage levels, or geographic territories. A company writing predominantly low-risk policyholders may have a lower average premium not because it is cheaper per unit of risk, but because it insures better risks. Similarly, differences in coverage limits, deductibles, or optional endorsements can make average premiums appear different even at the same underlying rate level.

**Solution**: Rather than comparing simple average premiums, the company should compare premiums on a like-for-like basis. This can be done by:
- Comparing premiums by segment (e.g., by class, territory, or coverage level) rather than in aggregate.
- Selecting a benchmark risk profile and obtaining quotes from both the company and each competitor for that specific profile.
- Re-rating the company's book using a competitor's rate manual (if publicly available via rate filings) to compute what the competitor would charge for the company's own policyholders.

### Examiner Report

Candidates were expected to show understanding of conditions required for a fair premium comparison between companies.

Common mistakes for identifying the issue:

- Premiums are not comparable due to different expense assumptions (the main issue is mix of business, not expense structure).
- Premiums are not comparable due to different profit provisions.

Common mistakes for proposing a solution:

- "Look at pure premium instead" — pure premium does not address the mix-of-business problem.
- "Adjust for bias and then compare" — too vague without specifying what adjustment to make.
