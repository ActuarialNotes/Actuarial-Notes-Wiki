An actuary is conducting a reserve review for a line of business and calculates the following:

| | Claims as of 12/31/2012 | | Projected Ultimate Claims | | | | | |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Accident Year** | **Reported** | **Paid** | **Development — Reported** | **Development — Paid** | **BF — Reported** | **BF — Paid** | **Claim Count and Severity Technique** | **Disposal Rate Technique** |
| 2009 | $76,700 | $75,800 | $77,501 | $77,483 | $77,758 | $78,022 | $77,474 | $77,817 |
| 2010 | $104,000 | $98,100 | $113,782 | $113,828 | $113,374 | $113,165 | $112,669 | $106,363 |
| 2011 | $107,200 | $55,100 | $130,379 | $94,770 | $127,393 | $102,646 | $132,743 | $107,447 |
| 2012 | $58,100 | $20,400 | $120,014 | $89,600 | $121,397 | $115,159 | $123,383 | $93,012 |

## Part a (1.5 points)

Suggest a reason for the disparity between the estimates of ultimate claims for accident year 2011 and propose diagnostic tests that would verify the assumption.

### Answer

**Suggested reason:** Case reserve strengthening (an increase in case reserve adequacy) for accident year 2011, with no corresponding change in the underlying payment pattern.

**Diagnostic tests:**
1. Compare average case outstanding per open claim across accident years and maturities. Look for a jump in average case reserves between AY 2010 and AY 2011 at comparable maturities (e.g., at 24 months) that is larger than the trend in average paid severity down the column — a disproportionate increase would support case strengthening.
2. Compare the paid-to-reported claims ratios (or closed-to-reported count ratios) across accident years at the same maturity. If the ratio is lower for AY 2011 than for prior accident years at the same age, this would indicate a slowdown in claim settlement, which is an alternative or complementary explanation.

### Explanation

The key observation is that AY 2011 shows a large gap between the development method ultimates for reported ($130,379) versus paid ($94,770) claims, and similarly between BF reported ($127,393) versus BF paid ($102,646). The frequency-severity technique ($132,743) is close to the reported development method, while the disposal rate technique ($107,447) is closer to the paid methods. This pattern is consistent with either:

**Case reserve strengthening:** If AY 2011 case reserves were strengthened (increased in adequacy) without a change in the actual ultimate loss level, the reported claims triangle would show artificially high development, causing the reported development method to project a higher ultimate than is warranted. Historical LDFs derived from prior years' patterns (when case reserves were lower) get applied to the inflated current reported amounts, producing an overestimate. The paid development method, unaffected by case changes, provides a lower and potentially more accurate estimate.

**Alternatively — slowdown in claim settlement:** If claims for AY 2011 are settling more slowly than historical patterns (without any case adequacy change), paid amounts will be lower at the current evaluation and the paid development method will apply historically-fast CDFs to a lower-than-expected base, understating the ultimate. This would explain the disparity in the opposite direction.

To verify case reserve strengthening, examine the average case outstanding per open claim column across accident years at the same maturity for a step-change. To verify a settlement slowdown, examine closed-count-to-reported-count ratios or paid-to-reported ratios for AY 2011 relative to AY 2009 and AY 2010 at comparable maturities.

### Examiner Report

There were many potential causes to the discrepancy — the most common responses were case reserve strengthening, claim payment slowdown, and the presence of an unpaid large loss. Credit was given to any explanation that made sense given the data.

In addition to stating a reason for the discrepancy between paid and reported methods, candidates received credit for explaining how the ultimates for specific methods were impacted. A more complete answer gives case reserve strengthening as the reason and explains that the same historical CDFs are applied to higher reported losses, resulting in a possible overestimate from the reported development method.

The question asked candidates to propose "diagnostic tests" to verify the assumption. Full credit required more than one test and some indication of how each test would be used to verify the assumption — merely listing test names without explanation did not receive full credit.

Common errors included assuming a speed-up in claim settlement when the data was more consistent with a slowdown (candidates could still receive partial credit with this assumption applied consistently) and merely stating there was a change in claim settlement without identifying the direction or the diagnostic to confirm it.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Determine what steps the actuary should take to determine the most appropriate methodology to project ultimate claims for accident year 2011.

### Answer

1. **Consult the claims department** to determine whether there was a deliberate change in case reserving practices or claims settlement procedures for AY 2011 and confirm the direction and magnitude of any change.
2. **Examine payment and settlement patterns** (e.g., paid-to-reported ratios, closed-count-to-reported-count ratios, average paid severity, average case outstanding per open claim) to determine whether the data supports the assumed cause of the disparity.
3. **Select the method consistent with findings:** If case reserves were strengthened without a payment pattern change, the paid development method or BF paid method is more appropriate (as it is not distorted by the case change). If the data can be adjusted using the Berquist-Sherman technique to restore consistent case reserve levels, the adjusted reported development method may be used. If a settlement slowdown is confirmed, the reported development or BF reported methods are more appropriate. Once the cause is confirmed, select the method (or combination of methods) that is not distorted by the identified data anomaly.

### Explanation

The actuary should not simply select among the six methods mechanically — understanding the cause of the disparity is essential. If case strengthening is confirmed, the reported triangle is distorted and methods relying on it (Development Reported, BF Reported, Claim Count and Severity) are likely overstated. The actuary would then weight toward paid-based methods or apply a Berquist-Sherman case outstanding adjustment to the reported triangle before developing. Conversely, if a settlement slowdown is confirmed, the paid triangle is distorted and paid-based methods understate the ultimate.

### Examiner Report

Some candidates listed diagnostic tests in part b rather than part a. Credit was given in part a for diagnostic tests listed in part b.

Many candidates gave only half the answer — either explaining how to confirm the reason for the discrepancy without following up with a solution, or providing only a solution without confirming steps. Full credit required indicating how the findings or confirmation steps would lead to a specific methodology selection. A complete answer should identify both (1) the investigative steps to confirm the cause of the disparity, and (2) the resulting choice of method and rationale.
