Given the following information for a portfolio written on a claims-made policy form:

| Calendar Year | Paid ULAE | Paid Claims | Year-End Outstanding Case Reserve | Year-End Outstanding IBNR |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2009 | $409 | $3,625 | $7,575 | $6,250 |
| 2010 | $476 | $5,875 | $10,450 | $7,500 |
| 2011 | $614 | $7,950 | $13,750 | $8,750 |
| 2012 | $761 | $10,375 | $16,500 | $10,625 |

Claim amounts include ALAE.

## Part a (1.5 points)

Calculate a ULAE provision as of December 31, 2012 using the Kittel adjustment.

### Answer

**ULAE Provision = $1,132.5**

### Explanation

**Step 1: Calculate reported (incurred) claims for each calendar year**

Under the Kittel adjustment, reported claims for a calendar year = paid claims + change in case reserves + change in IBNR:

$$\text{Reported}_t = \text{Paid}_t + (\text{Case}_{t} - \text{Case}_{t-1}) + (\text{IBNR}_{t} - \text{IBNR}_{t-1})$$

Note: CY 2009 is the first year with prior reserve data, so the change in reserves cannot be computed for it. Use CY 2010–2012:

| CY | Paid Claims | ∆ Case Reserve | ∆ IBNR | Reported Claims |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $5,875 | $10,450 − $7,575 = $2,875 | $7,500 − $6,250 = $1,250 | **$10,000** |
| 2011 | $7,950 | $13,750 − $10,450 = $3,300 | $8,750 − $7,500 = $1,250 | **$12,500** |
| 2012 | $10,375 | $16,500 − $13,750 = $2,750 | $10,625 − $8,750 = $1,875 | **$15,000** |

**Step 2: Compute the ULAE ratio for each year**

The Kittel ratio uses the average of paid and reported claims in the denominator:

$$\text{ULAE Ratio}_t = \frac{\text{Paid ULAE}_t}{\frac{1}{2}(\text{Paid Claims}_t + \text{Reported Claims}_t)}$$

| CY | Paid ULAE | Avg(Paid, Reported) | ULAE Ratio |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $476 | $\frac{5{,}875 + 10{,}000}{2} = \$7{,}937.5$ | $476 / 7{,}937.5 = 0.0600$ |
| 2011 | $614 | $\frac{7{,}950 + 12{,}500}{2} = \$10{,}225$ | $614 / 10{,}225 = 0.0600$ |
| 2012 | $761 | $\frac{10{,}375 + 15{,}000}{2} = \$12{,}687.5$ | $761 / 12{,}687.5 = 0.0600$ |

The ratios are very stable. **Selected ULAE ratio = 0.0600.**

**Step 3: Apply the ULAE provision formula**

For a claims-made portfolio, ULAE is incurred on claims at two stages: opening (reporting) and closing (payment). The classical method applies 50% of the ratio to reserves (for ULAE on open claims yet to close) and 100% to IBNR (for ULAE yet to be incurred on unreported claims). On a claims-made form there is no pure IBNR for claims not yet reported under that policy year, but there is IBNER (development on reported claims) captured in the IBNR figure. The provision formula is:

$$\text{ULAE Provision} = r \times \left(0.5 \times \text{Case Reserve}_{12/31/2012} + 1.0 \times \text{IBNR}_{12/31/2012}\right)$$

$$= 0.0600 \times (0.5 \times 16{,}500 + 1.0 \times 10{,}625)$$

$$= 0.0600 \times (8{,}250 + 10{,}625)$$

$$= 0.0600 \times 18{,}875 = \mathbf{\$1{,}132.5}$$

### Examiner Report

Candidates generally did not score well on this part.

Many candidates received partial credit for: using the average of paid and incurred (reported) losses in the denominator of the ULAE ratio; selecting an appropriate ULAE ratio given the ratios calculated by year; and calculating the ULAE provision.

Most candidates failed to properly calculate incurred (reported) losses as the sum of paid losses, the change in case reserves, and the change in IBNR. Common errors included simply adding paid losses to the year-end reserve values or not including IBNR in the reported loss calculation. Some candidates did not use the average of paid and incurred losses in the denominator. Many candidates calculated a ULAE ratio based on the sum of all years rather than examining the ratio by year to identify potential trends.

Of candidates that did calculate a ULAE provision, almost all failed to apply it correctly. The most common final-step errors were applying the ratio to the sum of year-end case reserves and IBNR for all calendar years, or applying the ratio to 50% of case reserves and 100% of IBNR — despite the question clearly identifying the policy as being claims-made. For a claims-made book, the formula applies 50% to case reserves and 100% to IBNR as shown above.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Explain the purpose of the Kittel adjustment.

### Answer

The Kittel adjustment accounts for ULAE on claims that have been reported but not yet paid — i.e., claims that are currently open. It is an improvement over the classical (paid-to-paid) method for lines of business that are growing, because in a growing book, paid claims in the current period understate the volume of claims on which ULAE costs are being incurred (since many open claims will not be paid until future periods). By using the average of paid and reported (incurred) losses in the denominator, the Kittel method better captures the total claim activity generating ULAE expense.

### Examiner Report

Most candidates received either no credit or partial credit on this part. Many candidates failed to describe the purpose of the Kittel adjustment and simply mentioned that the adjustment uses the average of paid and reported losses in the denominator. Candidates receiving partial credit often failed to mention that the adjustment is intended to improve upon the classical method specifically in the case of growing lines of business where paid claims lag behind reported claims.

## Part c (0.25 points)

Briefly explain a shortcoming of the classical method that is not addressed by the Kittel adjustment.

### Answer

The classical method assumes that 50% of all ULAE is incurred when a claim is opened and 50% is incurred when a claim is closed. This 50%/50% split is inflexible and does not distinguish between the cost of maintaining an open claim over time and the cost of closing a claim. The Kittel adjustment does not address this shortcoming — it retains the same 50%/50% assumption in the ULAE provision formula.

### Examiner Report

The majority of candidates who attempted this part provided an acceptable response. The key point is the inflexibility of the 50%/50% assumption that is shared by both the classical and Kittel methods. Another acceptable shortcoming is that neither method addresses situations where inflation affects paid ULAE and paid claims at different rates.
