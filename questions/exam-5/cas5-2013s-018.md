## Part a (0.25 points)

Briefly explain the key assumption of the Bornhuetter-Ferguson method.

### Answer

Losses reported (or paid) to date do not tell you anything about the losses that are yet to be reported (or paid). Unreported losses are better estimated based on an a priori initial expected ultimate rather than on experience to date.

### Explanation

The BF method assumes that the actuary's a priori estimate of expected ultimate claims is a better indicator of the unreported (or unpaid) portion than the emerging experience in the accident year itself. In other words, the development to date carries no information about future development — only the prior expectation does.

Formally, the BF ultimate is:

$$\text{BF Ultimate} = \text{Claims to Date} + (1 - \%\text{Reported}) \times \text{Expected Ultimate Claims}$$

The key assumption is that the $(1 - \%\text{Reported})$ portion is entirely driven by the a priori, not by extrapolating from claims reported so far.

### Examiner Report

The majority of candidates received full credit. Those that did not receive full credit typically lost points because they did not differentiate between the total claim estimate versus the unreported/unpaid portion. The key distinction is that the assumption applies specifically to the unreported (or unpaid) portion of claims, not to the total ultimate.

## Part b (0.5 points)

Briefly explain how the Bornhuetter-Ferguson method can be considered a credibility-weighted method and how the credibility is calculated.

### Answer

The BF method is a credibility-weighted average of the Development Method ultimate and the Expected Claims (a priori) ultimate, where the credibility weight $Z$ equals the percentage of claims already reported (or paid):

$$\text{BF Ultimate} = Z \times \text{Development Method Ultimate} + (1-Z) \times \text{Expected Claims Ultimate}$$

$$Z = \%\text{ Reported (or \% Paid)}$$

### Explanation

To see the credibility interpretation, note that the standard BF formula can be rewritten:

$$\text{BF Ult} = \text{Claims Reported} + (1 - \%\text{Rptd}) \times \text{ELR} \times \text{Premium}$$

The development method ultimate is:

$$\text{Dev Ult} = \text{Claims Reported} \times \text{CDF}_\text{ult} = \frac{\text{Claims Reported}}{\%\text{Rptd}}$$

Substituting into the credibility form:

$$\text{BF Ult} = \%\text{Rptd} \times \frac{\text{Claims Reported}}{\%\text{Rptd}} + (1 - \%\text{Rptd}) \times \text{Exp Cl Ult}$$

$$= \%\text{Rptd} \times \text{Dev Ult} + (1 - \%\text{Rptd}) \times \text{Exp Cl Ult}$$

So $Z = \%\text{ Reported}$ (or $\%\text{ Paid}$ for the paid BF). As more claims are reported, greater credibility is placed on the development method and less on the a priori estimate.

### Examiner Report

The majority of candidates received full credit. Those that did not were often mentioning the credibility calculation but were not specifying to which method the factor $Z$ would apply (the Development Method ultimate). Another common mistake was to write $Z \times \text{(Actual Reported or Paid Claims)}$ instead of $Z \times \text{(Development Method Ultimate)}$.

## Part c (0.25 points)

Briefly describe one situation where the credibility-weighted assumption underlying the Bornhuetter-Ferguson method may not apply.

### Answer

The credibility-weighted assumption does not apply when the cumulative percent reported (or percent paid) exceeds 100%. In credibility theory, the weight $Z$ cannot exceed 1, so using a $\%\text{ Reported} > 100\%$ violates the underlying credibility assumption.

### Explanation

This situation arises on lines with salvage and subrogation recoveries, or on short-tailed lines with very strong case reserves where cumulative paid or reported amounts exceed the ultimate. For example, a liability line with aggressive subrogation may show paid amounts that exceed the estimated ultimate, producing a $\%\text{ Paid} > 100\%$. In such cases the BF credibility interpretation breaks down, and a different method should be considered.

### Examiner Report

The majority of candidates did not receive full credit. A common mistake was to describe a situation where the BF method itself was not appropriate (e.g., immature accident years with little data), rather than describing a situation where the specific credibility-weighting assumption underlying the method fails. The question targeted the credibility mechanics specifically — the case where $\%\text{ paid or reported} > 1$, which is not permitted in standard credibility theory.

## Part d (0.5 points)

Explain whether the paid or reported Bornhuetter-Ferguson method is more responsive in a situation where claim ratios are increasing.

### Answer

The **reported BF method** is more responsive to increasing claim ratios.

### Explanation

In the credibility framework, the weight given to the development method ultimate is $Z = \%\text{ Reported}$ (or $\%\text{ Paid}$). Because the cumulative percent reported is generally larger than the cumulative percent paid at any given evaluation date (reported claims include both paid and outstanding case reserves), the reported BF assigns more weight to the development method and less to the a priori estimate compared to the paid BF.

When claim ratios are increasing, the development method ultimate will exceed the a priori estimate. By placing more weight on the development method, the reported BF picks up the adverse emergence faster:

$$\%\text{Reported} \geq \%\text{Paid}$$

$$\Rightarrow Z_\text{reported} \geq Z_\text{paid}$$

$$\Rightarrow \text{BF Ult}_\text{reported} \text{ is more responsive to emerging experience}$$

### Examiner Report

The majority of candidates did not receive full credit. Most candidates correctly identified the reported method as more responsive, but only a few clearly explained why. The key is that $\%\text{ Reported} > \%\text{ Paid}$ at early maturities, giving the reported BF a higher credibility weight on the development method (which reflects the adverse emergence), while the paid BF gives more weight to the a priori estimate, making it slower to react.

## Part e (0.5 points)

Compare and contrast the Cape Cod method and the Bornhuetter-Ferguson method by providing one similarity and one difference.

### Answer

**Similarity:** Both the Cape Cod and BF methods assume that the unreported (or unpaid) portion of claims is better estimated from an initial expected loss ratio than from development of reported claims to date. Neither relies solely on extrapolating the development method for the unreported portion.

**Difference:** The two methods derive the initial expected loss ratio (IELR) differently. The BF method uses an a priori, externally selected loss ratio. The Cape Cod method calculates the IELR endogenously from the data using the reported losses and the "used-up premium":

$$\text{CC IELR} = \frac{\sum \text{Reported Losses}_i}{\sum \text{Premium}_i \times \%\text{Reported}_i}$$

As a result, the Cape Cod method is more responsive to emerging experience than the BF method because its IELR updates with actual data.

### Examiner Report

Most candidates received full credit on this part. The most common correct similarity noted was that both methods assume the unreported amount should be based on a loss ratio estimate rather than on development alone (i.e., both are credibility-weighted between the development method and an expected claims technique). The most common correct difference was that the BF method uses an a priori selected loss ratio while the Cape Cod method derives it from the actual reported losses divided by used-up premium.
