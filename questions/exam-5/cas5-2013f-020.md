Given the following information:

| Accident Year | Earned Premium | On-Level Adjustment | Reported Claims | Pure Premium Trend Factors | Tort Reform Factors | Reported CDF to Ultimate |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | $50,000 | 0.900 | $25,000 | 1.061 | 0.750 | 1.250 |
| 2011 | $52,000 | 0.950 | $20,000 | 1.030 | 0.900 | 1.750 |
| 2012 | $54,000 | 1.000 | $10,000 | 1.000 | 1.000 | 2.500 |

Using the Cape Cod technique, estimate the IBNR for accident year 2011.

## Part a (2 points)

Calculate the IBNR for accident year 2011 using the Cape Cod method.

### Answer

$12,897 (approximately)

### Explanation

The Cape Cod method estimates IBNR as:

$$\text{IBNR} = \text{Premium} \times \text{ECR} \times \left(1 - \frac{1}{\text{CDF}}\right)$$

**Step 1 — Re-state on-level premiums to AY 2011 level.**

The given on-level adjustments bring each AY to the current rate level; to compare across AYs at the same (AY 2011) level, divide each AY's factor by the AY 2011 factor (0.950):

| AY | Earned Premium ($000) | Relative On-Level Adj | On-Level GP ($000) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | 50 | 0.900 / 0.950 = 0.9474 | 47.368 |
| 2011 | 52 | 0.950 / 0.950 = 1.0000 | 52.000 |
| 2012 | 54 | 1.000 / 0.950 = 1.0526 | 56.842 |

**Step 2 — Calculate used-up premium** (on-level GP × percent reported = on-level GP ÷ CDF):

| AY | On-Level GP ($000) | CDF | Used-Up Premium ($000) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2010 | 47.368 | 1.250 | 47.368 / 1.250 = 37.894 |
| 2011 | 52.000 | 1.750 | 52.000 / 1.750 = 29.714 |
| 2012 | 56.842 | 2.500 | 56.842 / 2.500 = 22.737 |
| **Total** | | | **90.345** |

**Step 3 — Adjust reported claims to AY 2011 level** (apply trend and tort reform factors restated to AY 2011):

The trend factor for AY 2011 relative to itself is 1.030/1.030 = 1.000; relative trend factors adjust each AY's claims to AY 2011 level. More directly, multiply reported claims by trend factor × tort reform factor restated to AY 2011:

- AY 2010: trend = 1.061 / 1.030 = 1.030; tort = 0.750 / 0.900 = 0.833  
  Adjusted: $25,000 × 1.030 × 0.833 = $21,449
- AY 2011: trend = 1.000; tort = 1.000  
  Adjusted: $20,000
- AY 2012: trend = 1.000 / 1.030 = 0.9709; tort = 1.000 / 0.900 = 1.111  
  Adjusted: $10,000 × 0.9709 × 1.111 = $10,787

Total adjusted reported claims = $21,449 + $20,000 + $10,787 = **$52,236**

**Step 4 — Estimate the Expected Claims Ratio (ECR):**

$$\text{ECR} = \frac{\text{Total adjusted reported claims}}{\text{Total used-up premium}} = \frac{52{,}236}{90{,}345} \approx 0.5782$$

**Step 5 — Calculate IBNR for AY 2011:**

$$\text{IBNR}_{2011} = \text{On-level GP}_{2011} \times \text{ECR} \times \left(1 - \frac{1}{\text{CDF}_{2011}}\right)$$

$$= 52{,}000 \times 0.5782 \times \left(1 - \frac{1}{1.750}\right)$$

$$= 52{,}000 \times 0.5782 \times 0.4286$$

$$\approx \$12{,}897$$

### Examiner Report

Candidates were able to handle the basic component of the Cape Cod method but struggled with the adjustments. Common mistakes included:

- Forgetting to adjust the estimated claim ratio to bring it to the AY 2011 level.
- Using the reported CDF to ultimate factor to adjust the reported claims to ultimate (this produces the same yearly adjusted ECRs but causes an error in the volume-weighting of the total).
- Not utilizing the concept of used-up premium, which is central to the method.
- Misapplying the pure premium trend and tort reform factors to the earned premium instead of to the claims, or not applying them at all.
- Providing expected ultimate claims as the answer instead of IBNR, or providing IBNR for an accident year other than 2011.
- Not calculating IBNR by applying the unreported percentage to the expected ultimate claims — the typical error was subtracting the reported claims or adjusted claims from the expected ultimate claims instead.
